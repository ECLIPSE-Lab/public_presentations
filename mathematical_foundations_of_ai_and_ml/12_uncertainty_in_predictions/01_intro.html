01. Title + Unit 12 positioning

  • Unit 11 discovered structure in data. Unit 12 asks: how confident are our predictions? {.fragment}
  • Single-point predictions are insufficient for engineering decisions. {.fragment}
  • We need principled uncertainty quantification — from Bayesian inference to Gaussian Processes. {.fragment}

02. Learning outcomes for Unit 12

By the end of this lecture, students can:

  • derive the Bayesian predictive distribution and interpret the variance decomposition, {.fragment}
  • describe the evidence framework and the concept of effective parameters, {.fragment}
  • define a GP, derive its posterior, and interpret uncertainty bands, {.fragment}
  • compare practical UQ methods: MC Dropout, ensembles, and MDNs. {.fragment}

03. Why point predictions are not enough

  • A model predicting 450 MPa tensile strength is useless without knowing if the uncertainty is \(\) or \(\) MPa. {.fragment}
  • In safety-critical applications, the uncertainty drives the decision, not the prediction. {.fragment}
  • Overconfident models are more dangerous than inaccurate ones. {.fragment}

04. Recall: aleatory vs epistemic uncertainty (Unit 8)

  • Aleatory: inherent noise in the data-generating process — irreducible. {.fragment}
  • Epistemic: uncertainty from limited data or model capacity — reducible. {.fragment}
  • A complete UQ framework must quantify and distinguish both types. {.fragment}
  • As training data grows, epistemic uncertainty should shrink; aleatory uncertainty should not. {.fragment}

05. The Bayesian predictive distribution

  • Instead of predicting with a single \(\), integrate over all plausible \(\): {.fragment}

\[ p(\mathbf{y}^* | \mathbf{x}^*, \mathcal{D}) = \int p(\mathbf{y}^* | \mathbf{x}^*, \theta) \, p(\theta | \mathcal{D}) \, d\theta \]

  • This accounts for parameter uncertainty — the full posterior contributes to the prediction. {.fragment}
  • The result is a distribution over predictions, not a single point. {.fragment}

06. Variance decomposition

\[ \text{Var}[\mathbf{y}^*] = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_\theta[\sigma^2(\theta)]}_{\text{aleatory}} + \underbrace{\text{Var}_\theta[\boldsymbol{\mu}(\theta)]}_{\text{epistemic}} \]

  • Aleatory component: average noise variance across parameter values.
  • Epistemic component: how much the prediction mean varies across plausible parameters.
  • The epistemic term shrinks with more data; the aleatory term does not.

08. When uncertainty matters most

  • Safety-critical: structural components, medical devices — failure consequences are severe. {.fragment}
  • Expensive experiments: each new alloy costs \$10K to synthesize — guide experiments with uncertainty. {.fragment}
  • Extrapolation: new compositions, extreme conditions — the model is outside its training domain. {.fragment}
  • Active learning: acquire data where uncertainty is highest for maximum information gain. {.fragment}

09. Practical UQ: a taxonomy

  • Exact Bayesian: Gaussian Processes — closed-form posterior, principled, but (O(N^3)). {.fragment}
  • Approximate Bayesian: MC Dropout, variational inference — scale to large data, approximate. {.fragment}
  • Frequentist ensembles: deep ensembles — no Bayesian formalism, practical uncertainty from disagreement. {.fragment}
  • Direct prediction: MDNs — predict distribution parameters directly. {.fragment}

11. The marginal likelihood (evidence)

\[ p(\mathcal{D} | \mathcal{M}) = \int p(\mathcal{D} | \theta, \mathcal{M}) \, p(\theta | \mathcal{M}) \, d\theta \]

  • Measures how well model \(\) explains the data, averaging over all parameter values. {.fragment}
  • Automatically balances fit (likelihood) and complexity (prior spread) (Murphy 2012). {.fragment}

12. Evidence as automatic Occam’s razor

  • Simple model: prior concentrated on few parameter values → high evidence if data is simple. {.fragment}
  • Complex model: prior spread thinly over many parameters → lower evidence unless data demands complexity. {.fragment}
  • The evidence automatically penalizes unnecessary complexity — no need for explicit regularization. {.fragment}

13. Model comparison via evidence

  • Bayes factor: \( = \). {.fragment}
  • With equal model priors: the model with higher evidence is preferred. {.fragment}
  • Unlike cross-validation, this uses all the data for both fitting and evaluation. {.fragment}

14. Effective number of parameters

\[ \gamma = \sum_i \frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda_i + \alpha} \]

  • \(_i\): eigenvalues of the data precision matrix. \(\): prior precision. {.fragment}
  • \(\) total number of parameters. Often \(\) total parameters. {.fragment}
  • Interpretation: only \(\) parameters are effectively constrained by the data (Bishop 2006). {.fragment}

15. Empirical Bayes

  • Instead of fixing hyperparameters (prior variance, noise level), optimize them by maximizing the evidence. {.fragment}
  • \(, ^2 = _{, ^2} p( | , ^2)\). {.fragment}
  • This is a principled alternative to cross-validation for hyperparameter selection. {.fragment}
  • Also called type-II maximum likelihood. {.fragment}

16. Checkpoint: evidence interpretation

  • Question: Model A has 100 parameters and log-evidence −500. Model B has 10 parameters and log-evidence −480. Which is preferred? {.fragment}
  • Answer: Model B — higher evidence means it explains the data better relative to its complexity. {.fragment}

17. What is a Gaussian Process?

  • A GP is a distribution over functions: (f (m(), k(, ’))). {.fragment}
  • Any finite collection of function values ([f(_1), , f(_N)]) is jointly Gaussian. {.fragment}
  • The GP is fully specified by its mean function (m()) and kernel function (k(, ’)). {.fragment}

18. GP as infinite-dimensional Gaussian

  • A multivariate Gaussian is a distribution over vectors.
  • A GP extends this to a distribution over functions (infinite-dimensional objects).
  • The kernel function \(k(, ’)\) plays the role of the covariance matrix.
  • This is a Bayesian nonparametric model — complexity grows with data (Murphy 2012).

19. Mean function m(x)

  • \(m() = [f()]\): the expected function value at each input.
  • Common choice: \(m() = 0\) (zero-mean prior).
  • Can encode prior knowledge: \(m() = a + b\) for a linear trend.
  • The mean function is updated to the posterior mean after observing data.

20. Kernel (covariance) function k(x, x’)

  • \(k(, ’) = [f(), f(’)]\): encodes the correlation between function values.
  • The kernel determines the properties of sampled functions:
    • Smoothness, periodicity, length scale, amplitude.
  • The kernel must be positive semi-definite (valid covariance matrix for any finite set of points).

21. The RBF (squared exponential) kernel

\[ k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \sigma_f^2 \exp\!\left(-\frac{\|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}'\|^2}{2\ell^2}\right) \]

  • Length scale \(\): controls how far apart inputs can be and still be correlated.
  • Signal variance \(_f^2\): controls the amplitude of function variation.
  • Produces infinitely differentiable (very smooth) functions.

22. Other kernels

  • Matérn: \(k(,’) \) Bessel function — adjustable smoothness via parameter \(\).
  • Periodic: captures repeating patterns.
  • Linear: \(k(,‘) = ^2 ^’\) — equivalent to Bayesian linear regression.
  • Composite: sums and products of kernels combine properties (e.g., smooth + periodic).

23. GP prior: sampling functions

  • Before seeing data, sample functions from the prior: \(f (0, k)\).
  • With RBF kernel: smooth, random functions with length scale \(\) and amplitude \(_f\).
  • Different kernel parameters produce visually different function families.
  • The prior encodes our beliefs about what functions are plausible.

24. GP posterior: conditioning on data

  • Observe \( = {(i, y_i)}{i=1}^N\) with \(y_i = f(_i) + \), \((0, _n^2)\).
  • The posterior \(f | \) is also a GP with updated mean and covariance.
  • The posterior passes through (or near) the training points.
  • Away from data, the posterior reverts to the prior.

25. GP posterior: closed-form formulas

\[ \boldsymbol{\mu}^*(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{k}_*^\top (\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{y} \]

\[ \sigma^{*2}(\mathbf{x}^*) = k(\mathbf{x}^*, \mathbf{x}^*) - \mathbf{k}_*^\top (\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{k}_* \]

  • (): kernel matrix ([k(_i, _j)]{N N}). (*): vector ([k(^*, _i)]).
  • The key operation is inverting (( + _n^2 )) — cost (O(N^3)).

26. GP posterior: interpretation

  • Mean \(^(^)\): best prediction — a weighted combination of training outputs.
  • Variance \(^{2}(^)\):
    • Small near training data (low epistemic uncertainty).
    • Large far from training data (high epistemic uncertainty).
    • Approaches prior variance \(_f^2\) as distance from data grows.

27. GP uncertainty bands

  • Plot \(() ()\): the 95% credible band.
  • Bands are narrow near observed data (confident predictions).
  • Bands widen away from data (uncertain predictions).
  • This is honest uncertainty — the GP admits what it does not know.

28. GP hyperparameter learning

  • Optimize kernel hyperparameters \(, _f, _n\) by maximizing the log marginal likelihood:

\[ \log p(\mathbf{y} | \mathbf{X}) = -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{y}^\top(\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{y} - \frac{1}{2}\log|\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I}| - \frac{N}{2}\log 2\pi \]

  • Three terms: data fit, complexity penalty, normalization.
  • Gradient-based optimization (L-BFGS is common).

29. Length scale effect

  • Short \(\): wiggly functions, fits local patterns (and possibly noise).
  • Long \(\): smooth functions, captures global trends (may miss local structure).
  • Optimal \(\): balances data fit and smoothness — determined by marginal likelihood.
  • Visualize: same data, three length scales → very different posterior functions.

30. GP: computational cost

  • Training: \(O(N^3)\) for matrix inversion + \(O(N^2)\) storage.
  • Prediction: \(O(N)\) per test point (after training).
  • Practical limit: \(N ^3 - 10^4\) for exact GPs.
  • Approximations exist for larger datasets: sparse GPs, inducing points, random features.

31. GP: strengths and limitations

Strengths

  • Principled uncertainty quantification
  • Automatic complexity control (evidence)
  • Interpretable hyperparameters
  • Works well with small data

Limitations

  • (O(N^3)) training cost
  • Kernel design requires domain knowledge
  • Gaussian assumption may be limiting
  • Scales poorly to high-dimensional inputs

32. Checkpoint: GP prediction

  • Question: A GP is trained on 10 data points. You query a point very far from all training data. What happens to the uncertainty?
  • Answer: The posterior variance grows toward the prior variance \(_f^2\). The GP honestly reports high uncertainty in unexplored regions.

33. Mixture-Density Networks (MDNs)

  • A standard NN outputs a single \(\) (or \(\)). An MDN outputs parameters of a mixture of Gaussians:

\[ p(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k(\mathbf{x}) \, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y} | \boldsymbol{\mu}_k(\mathbf{x}), \sigma_k^2(\mathbf{x})) \]

  • The network predicts mixing coefficients, means, and variances — all functions of input \(\) (Neuer et al. 2024).

34. MDN: capturing multi-modal uncertainty

  • Standard regression assumes unimodal output distribution.
  • MDNs can represent branching predictions: “this composition could yield phase A or phase B.”
  • The number of mixture components \(K\) is a design choice.
  • Particularly useful for inverse problems with multiple solutions.

35. MC Dropout for uncertainty estimation

  • Standard dropout: randomly zero neurons during training.
  • MC Dropout: keep dropout active at test time.
  • Run \(T\) stochastic forward passes → \(T\) predictions \({_1, , _T}\).
  • Mean = prediction. Variance across samples ≈ epistemic uncertainty.

36. MC Dropout: interpretation

  • Each forward pass uses a different randomly thinned network.
  • Equivalent to sampling from an approximate posterior over network architectures.
  • Theoretical connection to variational inference (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016).
  • Advantage: zero additional training cost — uncertainty is free at test time.

37. Deep ensembles

  • Train \(M\) independent networks (different random initializations, same architecture).
  • Each network produces a prediction \(_m\).
  • Mean: \({y} = _m _m\). Variance: \(_m (_m - {y})^2\).
  • Empirically produces well-calibrated uncertainties. Cost: \(M\times\) training.

38. Stochastic enrichment

  • Add noise to inputs during prediction: \( = + \), \( (, _)\).
  • Run multiple predictions with different noise realizations.
  • High variance across perturbed predictions = model is sensitive = high uncertainty.
  • Matches real-world measurement noise propagation (Neuer et al. 2024).

39. Calibration: are uncertainties trustworthy?

  • A model is well-calibrated if predicted \(p\)% confidence intervals contain \(p\)% of test points.
  • Calibration plot: predicted confidence level vs observed coverage.
  • Perfect calibration = diagonal line.
  • Overconfident: intervals too narrow (common in NNs). Underconfident: intervals too wide.

40. Recalibration methods

  • Temperature scaling: divide logits by a learned temperature \(T\) before softmax.
  • Platt scaling: fit a logistic regression on validation predictions.
  • Isotonic regression: non-parametric calibration mapping.
  • Applied post-hoc on a held-out calibration set — does not change the model.

41. Comparison of UQ methods

Method Type Cost Calibration Scalability
GP Exact Bayesian \(O(N^3)\) Excellent Small \(N\)
MC Dropout Approx. Bayesian \(T \) inference Good Any
Deep ensemble Frequentist \(M \) training Very good Any
MDN Direct 1× training Requires tuning Any

42. Checkpoint: choosing a UQ method

  • Small dataset, need exact UQ: Gaussian Process.
  • Large dataset, budget for training: deep ensemble.
  • Large dataset, need cheap inference: MC Dropout.
  • Multi-modal outputs: Mixture-Density Network.

46. Physics-informed uncertainty reduction (preview of Unit 13)

  • Embedding physical constraints (conservation laws, symmetries) reduces epistemic uncertainty.
  • The model is forced to respect known physics → fewer plausible functions → tighter uncertainty.
  • This is equivalent to a more informative prior in the Bayesian framework.

49. Exam-aligned summary: 10 must-know statements

  1. The Bayesian predictive distribution integrates over parameter uncertainty.
  2. Total prediction variance = aleatory variance + epistemic variance.
  3. The marginal likelihood (evidence) measures model fit with automatic complexity penalty.
  4. A GP is a distribution over functions specified by mean and kernel functions.
  5. The GP posterior has closed-form mean and variance (for Gaussian likelihood).
  6. GP uncertainty grows away from training data — honest epistemic uncertainty.
  7. Kernel hyperparameters (length scale, signal variance) control GP behavior.
  8. MC Dropout approximates Bayesian inference by sampling sub-networks at test time.
  9. Deep ensembles provide uncertainty via disagreement among independently trained models.
  10. Calibration plots verify that predicted confidence matches observed accuracy.
