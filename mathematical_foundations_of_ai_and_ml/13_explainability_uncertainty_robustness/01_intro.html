01. Title: Physics-Informed Learning, Explainability, and Scientific Trust

  • The Capstone: Moving from “What data says” to “What the laws of nature demand.”
  • Unit Role: Integrating physical consistency, transparency, and robustness into the ML pipeline.
  • Goal: Final synthesis of mathematical rigor and scientific domain knowledge.

02. Learning outcomes

By the end of this final unit, students can: - Formulate Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) by embedding ODEs/PDEs into loss functions. - Apply Automatic Differentiation to enforce physical constraints. - Critically assess model transparency using the Explainability Taxonomy. - Design Causal Process Chains to distinguish between correlation and prediction in engineering.

03. Recap from Units 1–12

  • Units 1-7: Optimization, Linear Algebra, and Generalization.
  • Units 8-12: Probability, Latent Spaces, and Bayesian Inference.
  • Integrated View: Learning is a probabilistic search for a model that minimizes risk while respecting prior knowledge.

04. The Trust Mandate in Science

  • In social media AI, a wrong prediction costs a click.
  • In materials engineering, a wrong prediction costs a bridge or a reactor.
  • Scientific AI must be:
    1. Physically Consistent.
    2. Transparent (Explainable).
    3. Robust to noise and shift.

05. The Small Data Challenge

  • Physical experiments are expensive.
  • pure black-box models (Units 4-6) require massive datasets.
  • The Solution: Inject the “Rules of the Game” (Physics) to constrain the search space.

06. Physics-Informed Machine Learning (PIML)

  • Philosophy: Use domain expertise to “guide” the learner.
  • Three paths:
    1. Data Enrichment: Pre-process features with physical laws (e.g., FFT).
    2. Architecture: Design layers that satisfy symmetries (e.g., equivariance).
    3. Loss Function: Penalize violations of physical laws (Neuer et al. 2024).

07. Embedding Analytical Expressions

  • If we know \(y\) must satisfy \(f(y, x) = 0\):
  • Add a term to the loss: \(J = J_{data} + \lambda \|f(\hat{y}, x)\|^2\).
  • The optimizer now searches for a solution that is both accurate AND physically valid.

08. Automatic Differentiation (AD) for Physics

  • To enforce laws like \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \alpha \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\), we need derivatives of the network’s output with respect to its inputs.
  • AD (Unit 5) provides these gradients exactly and efficiently, allowing us to treat the NN as a differentiable surrogate for numerical solvers.

09. Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINN)

  • The Residue: \(r(x, t) = \frac{\partial \hat{u}}{\partial t} - \mathcal{N}[\hat{u}]\).
  • We minimize the squared residual over a set of “collocation points” in the domain.
  • No Labels Needed: PINNs can learn from the equation alone in unsupervised mode!

10. Boundary and Initial Conditions

  • Physics is defined by its boundaries.
  • Hard Constraints: Using mathematical substitutions (e.g., Lagaris method) to force the network to 0 at the edges.
  • Soft Constraints: Adding a boundary loss term \(J_{BC}\).

11. Data Enrichment: FFT and Wavelets

  • Don’t just feed raw time-series to an MLP.
  • Feed the Fourier Spectrum.
  • Reason: The network doesn’t have to “discover” frequency; we provide it as a physically meaningful representation (Neuer et al. 2024).

12. Occam’s Razor in PIML

  • A model that satisfies the heat equation has much lower “Information Complexity” than one that fits the points with arbitrary curves.
  • PIML is the ultimate form of Regularization.

13. Scientific Explainability Taxonomy

  • Local: Why did the model predict this specific sample is defective?
  • Global: What general features characterize high-strength alloys?
  • Intrinsic: Using naturally interpretable models (Trees, Linear).
  • Post-hoc: Probing black-boxes after training.

14. Semantic Structures: Digitizing Meaning

  • AI doesn’t know what “Temperature” means; it just sees a column index.
  • Taxonomies: Hierarchical ordering (e.g., “Steel” \(\subset\) “Ferrous Metal”).
  • Ontologies: Mapping relationships (e.g., “Yield Strength” is measured by “Tensile Test”).

15. The Role of Ontologies

  • By linking model inputs to an ontology, we can perform Deductive Reasoning.
  • “The model is sensitive to feature 5. Feature 5 is ‘Impurity’. Therefore, the model’s decision is driven by chemical contamination.”

16. Saliency Maps and Feature Importance

  • Permutation Importance: How much does the loss increase if we scramble feature \(j\)?
  • SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations): Assigning each feature a “contribution” based on cooperative game theory.
  • Caveat: High correlation between features can make these maps misleading (Bishop 2006).

17. Visualization of Hidden Manifolds

  • Using UMAP (Unit 10) to show that the model’s internal representation matches physical phase boundaries.
  • Trust: If the model groups “FCC” and “BCC” correctly without being told, we trust its internal logic.

18. Causality vs. Correlation

  • Correlation: \(A\) and \(B\) move together.
  • Causality: \(A\) makes \(B\) happen.
  • Standard ML finds correlations. Structural Causal Models (SCM) are needed to predict the result of an intervention (e.g., “What if I change the cooling rate?”).

19. Causality in Process Chains

  • Detection: Identifying a defect at the end of the line (Correlation).
  • Prediction: Foreseeing the defect while the part is still in the furnace (Causal).
  • Goal: Move AI interventions as early as possible in the causal chain (Neuer et al. 2024).

20. Uncertainty Taxonomy (Refresher)

  • Aleatory: Measurement noise. We model this with GMMs/MDNs.
  • Epistemic: Model ignorance. We model this with Bayesian posteriors or Ensembles.
  • Integrated UQ: Predictive distributions must reflect both.

21. Calibration: The Reliability Gap

  • A model that predicts “Success” with 99% probability but fails 20% of the time is a liability.
  • ECE (Expected Calibration Error): Quantifying the gap between confidence and accuracy.
  • Remedy: Temperature scaling or Bayesian averaging.

22. Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Detection

  • The Problem: A model trained on Steel will confidently predict properties for Wood, even if it has no clue.
  • The Signal: Use the Latent Density (Unit 10) or Bayesian Variance (Unit 12) to detect when a sample is too far from the training manifold.

23. Distribution Shift: Covariate and Concept

  • Covariate Shift: The inputs change (e.g., new supplier).
  • Concept Shift: The physics changes (e.g., new material behavior at extreme heat).
  • Robustness: The model’s ability to maintain accuracy under these shifts.

24. Adversarial Perturbation Intuition

  • In high dimensions, very small, carefully chosen “nudges” can flip a model’s prediction.
  • Materials Context: Could a tiny bit of sensor noise make a “Safe” part look “Dangerous”?
  • Robust Training: Augmenting the training set with these “worst-case” perturbations.

25. Robust Optimization: Min-Max

  • \[ \min_{\mathbf{w}} \max_{\delta \in \Delta} J(\mathbf{w} + \delta) \]
  • We minimize the loss assuming an adversary is trying to maximize it.
  • Leads to extremely “flat” minima that are immune to noise (Sandfeld et al. 2024).

26. Data Augmentation for Robustness

  • Injecting noise, rotations, and physical transforms into the training loop.
  • Effectively broadens the model’s “experience,” making it less likely to be surprised by real-world variations.

27. Ensemble Robustness

  • Averaging 10 models is more robust than using 1.
  • Wisdom of the Crowd: Errors of individual models often cancel out, while the shared physical signal is reinforced.

28. Decision-Theoretic Abstention

  • The Reject Option: If the model’s uncertainty is too high, it should say “I don’t know; ask a human expert.”
  • This is the most critical safeguard for autonomous materials labs.

29. Selective Prediction

  • Coverage vs. Accuracy Tradeoff: I can be 100% accurate if I only predict the 10% of samples I’m most certain about.
  • Engineering Task: Find the operating point that balances safety and throughput.

30. Cost-Sensitive Thresholds

  • If a “False Negative” (missed crack) is 1000x more costly than a “False Positive” (manual re-check), we must shift our decision boundary accordingly.
  • Mathematics: Minimizing the Expected Risk, not maximizing accuracy.

31. Fairness and Subgroup Reliability

  • Does the model work equally well for all material classes?
  • Risk: A model might be 99% accurate on average but 0% accurate for rare, critical alloys.
  • Check: Perform subgroup analysis on the test set.

32. Invariant Predictors

  • A model is causally robust if its performance is invariant across different experimental environments.
  • If the model only works on “Tuesdays in Erlangen,” it has learned a spurious correlation, not physics.

33. Stress Testing Methodology

  • Beyond Cross-Validation: Actively try to “break” the model.
  • Feed it edge cases, extreme noise, and physically impossible inputs to see where the logic collapses.

34. Monitoring Post-Deployment

  • ML is not “Fire and Forget.”
  • Surveillance: Track the mean and variance of predictions in production. A sudden shift in the model’s behavior is often the first sign of a broken sensor (Neuer et al. 2024).

35. Human-in-the-Loop Safeguards

  • AI as an Augmenter, not a Replacer.
  • Providing “Evidence Packs” (Saliency + Uncertainty) to the engineer so they can make the final call.

36. Governance and Accountability

  • Who is responsible if the AI makes a wrong materials choice?
  • Model Cards: Standardized documentation of model limits and intended use cases.

37. Reproducibility Requirements (Final)

  • Fixed seeds, versioned data, and archived environments.
  • Scientific ML must be as reproducible as a wet-lab experiment.

38. Communication of Limits

  • “Our model is 95% accurate for alloys with <5% Cr, but should not be used for stainless steels.”
  • Transparency about blind spots is the hallmark of professional data science.

39. Synthesis: The Mathematical Pipeline

  • Data \(\to\) Representations (Unit 9-10) \(\to\) Probabilistic Inference (Unit 8, 11-12) \(\to\) Physical Constraints (Unit 13) \(\to\) Decision (Unit 13).
  • Every step is grounded in the linear algebra and calculus we learned at the start.

41. Exercise 1 scaffold: Calibration Audit

  • Take a pre-trained classifier.
  • Calculate its ECE and plot a Reliability Diagram.
  • Intentionally bias the model and see how the diagram changes.

42. Exercise 2 scaffold: Saliency vs. Perturbation

  • Generate saliency maps for an image classifier.
  • Perturb the “important” pixels and observe the drop in confidence.
  • Perturb “unimportant” pixels and see if the prediction remains stable.

43. Exercise 3 scaffold: PINN Toy Problem

  • Implement a 1D PINN to solve the equation \(u' = \cos(x)\) with \(u(0)=0\).
  • Minimize the residual loss and the boundary loss.
  • Compare the result to the analytical solution \(\sin(x)\).

44. Exercise 4 scaffold: Abstention Policy

  • Given a model’s predictive variance, define a threshold for “Rejecting” a sample.
  • Plot Accuracy vs. Coverage as you vary the threshold.

45. Final Project: Trust Report

  • Consolidate all your work into a “Model Card” extension.
  • Document the UQ, the physical consistency, and the known failure modes of your materials model.

46. Exam-oriented key statements

  1. PINNs embed physical residuals into the loss function.
  2. Saliency maps identify which features drive a prediction.
  3. Calibration ensures that probabilities reflect actual risk.
  4. OOD detection prevents model hallucinations on unseen data types.
  5. Causal models are required for making reliable physical interventions.
