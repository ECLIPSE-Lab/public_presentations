§0 · Frame

01. Today’s Question

Clustering organises a data set. Does it discover materials?

  • A cluster label is a description: this point sits with those points.
  • A discovery is a decision: synthesise this candidate next, under a budget, with quantified uncertainty.
  • The two verbs are not the same — and most “ML discovers a new material” papers slip between them.

What this unit is not.

  • Not a re-derivation of k-means / GMM / EM — that is MFML W5 (Bishop 2006; Murphy 2012).
  • Not clustering of CNN embeddings or hyperspectral cubes — that is ML-PC W5.
  • Today’s job: convert the clustering toolkit you already have into a discovery loop component.

Open with the verb shift, not a definition. “Last week (U10) we mapped materials into a continuous latent space. Today we partition that latent space — and then we ask whether partitioning is the same as discovering. Spoiler: it is not.” That distinction is the spine of the next 90 minutes.

Triad coordination, said aloud. MFML W5 already taught the algorithms; ML-PC W5 already taught how to deploy them on micrographs and hyperspectral data. MG U11 is therefore not an algorithms unit — re-deriving k-means here would crowd out the materials content. We assume the toolkit; we use it.

Pacing. §A recap 5 min; §B distinction 12 min; §C similarity retrieval 14 min; §D cluster-aware screening 14 min; §E anomaly + rare motif 14 min; §F bridge to U12 12 min; §G case studies 14 min; §H wrap-up 5 min. Tight; favour cutting case-study count over cutting §B if running long.

04. The Clustering Toolkit (One-Slide Recap)

Hard / soft partitioning (MFML W5)

  • K-means: \(\arg\min_{\{\mu_k\}, \{c_i\}} \sum_i \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mu_{c_i}\|_2^2\), alternating Lloyd updates (Bishop 2006).
  • GMM: \(p(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_k \pi_k \, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x} \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)\) with EM responsibilities \(\gamma_{ik}\).
  • \(k\) chosen from elbow / silhouette / BIC — not by the analyst’s gut.

Density / hierarchy (MFML W5)

  • DBSCAN: continuous high-density regions; arbitrary cluster shape; noise points labelled \(-1\) (Neuer et al. 2024).
  • Hierarchical agglomerative: dendrogram; linkage choice (single / complete / average) controls cluster geometry; cut height = chosen \(k\) post hoc.
  • No re-derivation today. If E/M-step is fuzzy, re-read MFML W5 tonight.

This is a recap slide, not a teaching slide. The single most common pacing failure for U11 is to spend ten minutes here re-deriving k-means. Resist. The students have seen it twice — once in MFML W5 (algorithm), once in ML-PC W5 (deployment on micrographs). If they are blank, the right intervention is “re-read MFML W5 lecture 5 tonight” — not a third derivation in MG.

Notation we hold fixed. \(\mu_k\) centroids; \(\Sigma_k\) covariances; \(\pi_k\) mixing weights; \(\gamma_{ik}\) posterior responsibilities. Same letters as MFML W5 and ML-PC W5 — do not let notation drift.

The k-means → GMM limit, in one sentence. Set \(\Sigma_k = \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}\) and let \(\sigma \to 0\); GMM responsibilities collapse to one-hot k-means assignments. Mention this once; do not redraw it.

05. Density-Based and Hierarchical at a Glance

DBSCAN — when shape is irregular

  • Two hyperparameters: \(\epsilon\) (neighbour radius), min_samples.
  • Pros: arbitrary cluster shape; robust to outliers; auto-flags noise.
  • Cons: \(\epsilon\) tuning is brittle in high-dim; struggles when cluster densities differ.
  • Materials use: “islands of stability” in latent space; defect / impurity flagging in microscopy embeddings.

Hierarchical — when taxonomy matters

  • Bottom-up agglomeration; merge nearest pairs by linkage rule.
  • Output: a dendrogram — a continuous family of partitions parametrised by cut height.
  • Materials use: polymorph hierarchies; chemistry-family taxonomies; choosing “discovery resolution” post hoc.
  • Deterministic; no random seed.

Single take-away from §A: these algorithms organise the data into groups. They do not propose new candidates. The next 47 slides are about closing that gap.

Why DBSCAN matters for materials, in one sentence. Many genuine materials clusters are not spherical — they trail along chemical-similarity ridges. K-means slices those ridges arbitrarily; DBSCAN walks them. The trade-off is brittleness in high-D (\(\epsilon\) has to live in a regime where neighbourhood density actually distinguishes signal from noise — increasingly hard above \(d \sim 50\)).

Why hierarchical matters for materials, in one sentence. Chemistry has a tree structure (oxide → perovskite → halide perovskite → tilted halide perovskite). A flat partition forces a single resolution; a dendrogram lets the analyst choose the right resolution per question. For “discover a new family,” cut high; for “discover a new tilt mode,” cut low.

The take-away framing is load-bearing. This is the slide that sets up §B. Read the bottom quote aloud; pause; move on. Students should leave §A with the words “organise versus propose” in their heads — those four words are §B’s entire content.

What I deliberately don’t show. No worked DBSCAN \(\epsilon\)-sweep, no dendrogram visualisation, no linkage-comparison figure. All of those exist in MFML W5 and ML-PC W5 decks. Students who want them should reopen those decks; we will not redraw them.

06. The Limit of Clustering

What clustering does

  • Groups given points by similarity in a given feature space.
  • Returns: labels, centroids, hierarchy, density estimate.
  • Internal validity: silhouette, Davies–Bouldin, BIC.
  • Useful for: organisation, deduplication, summarisation.

What clustering does not do

  • Propose new candidates not in the data.
  • Rank candidates by expected scientific value.
  • Quantify confidence that a group is real (vs sampling artifact).
  • Trade off cost of acquisition against expected gain.
  • All four are required to act on a candidate list — and none come for free from any clustering algorithm.

This slide is the pivot. Read both columns aloud, pause for a beat after each bullet on the right. The right column is the table of contents for the rest of the lecture: §B installs the conceptual distinction; §C–§E install the operational patterns that do propose / rank / trade off; §F shows what UQ adds; §G demonstrates with case studies.

Why “real vs sampling artifact” is a clustering limit. Run the same algorithm on a 90% bootstrap; do you get the same partition? Often, no. Cluster identity is unstable under sub-sampling unless the data has genuine separation. Internal validity indices do not capture this — they grade a partition, not its reproducibility. We come back to this on slide 25 (shortlist stability).

Concrete example to give aloud. “If I cluster the entire Materials Project tomorrow morning, I will get a partition. If I add the next 1000 entries from OQMD and re-cluster, I will get a different partition. Are the two clusterings telling me about chemistry, or about the database I happened to use? Clustering alone cannot answer that. The four-step validation in §E is what answers it.”

§B · The Clustering-vs-Discovery Distinction

07. Two Different Verbs

Descriptive (clustering)

  • Question: What groups exist in the data I have?
  • Output: labels, centroids, hierarchy.
  • Truth condition: stability of the partition under re-sampling.
  • Failure mode: clusters reflect convenience features, not chemistry.

Decisional (discovery)

  • Question: Which material — possibly absent from the data — should I synthesise next, under a finite budget, given uncertainty?
  • Output: a ranked, costed shortlist.
  • Truth condition: predictions agree with experiment / simulation.
  • Failure mode: confident shortlist whose top entry is an artifact.

The verb shift is the entire content of §B. Descriptive sentences are about the data set; decisional sentences are about the world. Clustering is a tool for descriptive sentences; discovery requires a tool for decisional sentences. The unit’s job is to install enough vocabulary that students can tell which kind of claim a paper is making — and call out the slip when an author treats a description as a decision.

A concrete contrast pair to read aloud.

  • Descriptive: “We identified five clusters of Mn-based perovskites in our 5,400-entry dataset, with average bandgap differing by 0.3 eV across clusters.”
  • Decisional: “We synthesised CsMnBr\(_3\) as a candidate from cluster 4 because its predicted bandgap of 1.3 eV ± 0.1 eV under our GP surrogate matched our target window, and the cluster-aware acquisition function ranked it first.”

The first sentence is true if the clustering is reproducible. The second sentence is testable in the lab. Different verbs, different evidence standards.

Common paper-grading slip. Authors describe a clustering, then assert that one cluster “represents a new class of materials” — without naming a candidate, costing a synthesis, or quantifying uncertainty. That sentence has slipped from descriptive to decisional without earning the right to. Students should learn to flag this on first reading.

08. A Cluster Label Is Not a Hypothesis

A scientific hypothesis is falsifiable.

  • Predicts what will and will not be observed under a specified test.
  • Has a truth condition an experiment can resolve.
  • Example: “BaZrS\(_3\) has a direct bandgap of \(1.7 \pm 0.2\) eV at room temperature.” → measurable.

A cluster label is not a hypothesis.

  • “BaZrS\(_3\) belongs to cluster 7” → describes its embedding.
  • No experimental observation will refute cluster membership; only re-running the algorithm will.
  • A cluster label plus a property prediction plus an uncertainty plus a synthesis target = a hypothesis. Cluster label alone \(\neq\) hypothesis.

Read both columns slowly. Most students arrive having seen ML papers that conflate the two. The point of this slide is to install the question: what experiment would refute this clustering claim? If the answer is “none,” the claim isn’t a hypothesis — it’s a description.

The “plus” recipe in the right column is load-bearing. A hypothesis-grade discovery sentence has four ingredients:

  1. Cluster membership — which family is this?
  2. Property prediction — what value do we expect?
  3. Uncertainty — how confidently?
  4. Synthesis target — what test will we run?

§C–§F install ingredients 2–4. §A and §B install the discipline of recognising when a clustering paper has supplied (1) but called it a hypothesis.

Counter-objection. “Some clusterings are physically meaningful — e.g., spacegroup-resolved clusters of inorganic materials.” True; but the meaning is not in the clustering, it is in the external labels we use to validate. Cluster \(\approx\) spacegroup is a falsifiable claim — but the falsifying observation is the spacegroup label, not the cluster ID. Don’t confuse the validator with the claim.

09. A Centroid Is Not a Candidate

Centroid pathologies

  • Centroid \(\mu_k = \frac{1}{|C_k|} \sum_{i \in C_k} \mathbf{x}_i\) is an averaged vector in feature space.
  • Often chemically impossible: fractional stoichiometry; non-integer coordination; mean of two different prototypes.
  • Decoding the centroid back to a structure (via U10 latent-traversal) often returns a synthetic-looking nothing.

Use the medoid instead

  • Medoid: the actual sample \(\mathbf{x}_{i^*}\) in \(C_k\) closest to \(\mu_k\).
  • A real material; reportable as a candidate.
  • Even the medoid is a candidate only if (a) untested, (b) physically plausible, (c) we have a property reason to want it.
  • Reporting medoids — not centroids — is the minimum standard for cluster-driven candidate proposals.

Why centroids are pathological for materials, mathematically. Many materials descriptors are nonlinear in composition (e.g., logarithms of frequencies, exponentials of formation energies, bounded ratios). The mean of nonlinearly-encoded compositions is not the encoding of a meaningful composition — it is a point in feature space with no preimage. The centroid is fine as a summary statistic of the cluster; it is not a material.

Why medoids are honest. A medoid is by construction a real entry from the database. You can look it up, plot its structure, simulate it. It cannot be synthetically over-promising because it is just a re-discovery of the database’s most-central member of cluster \(k\).

The (a)/(b)/(c) test in the right column. This is the operational checklist for “is this medoid a candidate?”: - (a) untested — has someone already published this? - (b) physically plausible — does it relax to itself in DFT? sane bond lengths? - (c) property reason — does the cluster’s average property match our design goal?

Three “yes” answers and the medoid earns a synthesis vote. Two “yes” and one “unclear” earns a DFT run. One “yes” and the medoid is folklore, not a candidate.

Forward link. The four-step validation discipline in §E (slide 31) is the full version of this checklist for outliers. Slide 9’s three-step test is the centroid version.

10. A Silhouette Score Is Not Novelty

What internal indices measure

  • Silhouette: \(s_i = \tfrac{b_i - a_i}{\max(a_i, b_i)} \in [-1, 1]\) — geometric separation per point (Murphy 2012).
  • Davies–Bouldin: cluster compactness vs separation.
  • Calinski–Harabasz: between-cluster vs within-cluster variance.
  • All measure geometry of the partition in feature space.

What they don’t measure

  • Whether the geometry reflects chemistry or convenience features (slide 24).
  • Whether candidates from a “tight” cluster will behave as predicted.
  • Whether the partition is stable under re-sampling (slide 25).
  • A perfect silhouette on database-source clusters is a perfect silhouette on a pipeline artifact.

The slide’s point in one sentence. Internal indices grade how well the algorithm did its job; they do not grade whether the job was worth doing. A k-means with silhouette 0.8 has produced a clean partition — but the partition can still be a partition by VASP-vs-Quantum-Espresso, by supercell size, or by whichever feature happens to dominate the unstandardised distance.

External validity is what we actually want. External indices — Adjusted Rand Index, Normalised Mutual Information, purity — compare the clustering to a known categorical structure (spacegroup, prototype label, processing route). If the algorithm recovers that structure with high ARI, the clustering is “structurally aware.” Silhouette alone never proves this; ARI against a held-out label set does.

The minimum viable diagnostic stack. Report: - silhouette (or BIC for GMM) — the partition is geometrically sensible, - ARI against a known label — the partition is physically sensible, - bootstrap stability (slide 25) — the partition is reproducibly sensible.

Three diagnostics, not one. Drum this in.

Anti-pattern. “Our clustering achieves silhouette 0.71, indicating clear discovery of new chemistry families.” No: it indicates clear separation in your feature space. Whether the separation is chemistry, source bias, or unit-cell-size is a separate question.

11. Where Uncertainty Enters

Three uncertainties, three checks

  • Sampling: would the clustering survive a 90% bootstrap? Test: re-cluster on 10 subsamples; report shortlist agreement.
  • Assignment: how confidently is sample \(i\) in cluster \(k\)? Test: GMM responsibility \(\gamma_{ik}\) — if max \(\gamma_{ik} \approx 1/K\), the assignment is meaningless.
  • Predictive: would a property surrogate agree with the clustering’s chemistry implication? Test: Unit 12.

Why uncertainty elevates clustering

  • A reproducible cluster + a confident assignment + a calibrated property prediction = a defensible candidate.
  • Any one of the three missing = a folklore candidate.
  • Today’s job (§C–§E): make the first two easy. Unit 12’s job: make the third easy.

The three uncertainties matter independently. A cluster can be reproducible (low sampling uncertainty) but contain ambiguous boundary points (high assignment uncertainty). A clustering can be perfectly assigned (every \(\gamma_{ik} \approx 1\)) but unstable across bootstraps (the boundaries moved on a different subsample, even if the per-point assignments were sharp on each individual partition). And both can be fine while the property surrogate’s predictions are miscalibrated — the candidate’s predicted bandgap of 1.3 eV may have a true 95% interval of \(\pm 0.6\) eV.

The “folklore candidate” framing is deliberately blunt. It is the right word for a discovery sentence that omits any one of the three uncertainties. Discovery folklore is real and runs labs; it does not produce reproducible publications. The exam expects students to call out folklore on inspection.

The U12 hand-off, planted here. Slide 36 picks this up: the third uncertainty (predictive) is what GP / ensemble methods provide. Today, on slide 11, just plant the seed: clustering can supply two of the three; U12 supplies the third.

Common student question. “Can’t I just use GMM responsibilities to get all three?” No: GMM responsibilities address assignment uncertainty, but not sampling or predictive. They tell you how confidently a known point belongs to a cluster — not how stable the partition is under re-sampling, and not whether a candidate’s predicted property is reliable.

12. Examples Where the Distinction Matters

Polymorph clustering

  • Same composition, multiple crystal structures (TiO\(_2\): rutile, anatase, brookite, …).
  • A cluster around each polymorph is a description of database structure.
  • A discovery claim (“new metastable polymorph found”) needs phase-stability evidence — energy-above-hull, lattice-dynamics, synthesis route.

HEA / MOF / inorganic-prototype catalogues

  • Clusters in composition or graph embeddings recover known families.
  • Within a cluster, the candidate ranking — which alloy / topology to make next — is a property-surrogate decision, not a clustering decision.
  • Clustering organises; the predictor + acquisition rank.

§B take-away: clustering provides the index. Discovery uses the index, plus a property surrogate, plus uncertainty, plus a budget — to decide what to make next.

Polymorph framing. The TiO\(_2\) example is canonical: any reasonable structural embedding will cluster the database into rutile / anatase / brookite (and a tail of less-common phases). That clustering is a useful index — it makes downstream queries fast. It is not a discovery of polymorphs; the polymorphs were known. A discovery claim from such a clustering would be along the lines of “this cluster of metastable phases includes one entry whose predicted properties suggest a synthesis attempt is warranted.” That sentence has all four hypothesis ingredients from slide 8.

HEA framing — pre-empts §G. §G case study A returns to the HEA example. The point to plant here: the clustering identifies which alloy families are well-explored in the literature (and therefore which the predictor will be confident about). The clustering does not — cannot — identify which alloy family will be best. That requires a property surrogate.

Read the bottom take-away aloud. Slow. Index — plus — predictor — plus — uncertainty — plus — budget. Each “plus” is a load-bearing word. §B installs the distinction; §C–§F supply the three missing pieces.

§C · Similarity-Based Discovery

13. The “Find Me Materials Like This” Workflow

Setup

  • Embedding \(\phi : \text{material} \to \mathbb{R}^d\) trained on a database \(\mathcal{D}\).
  • Query material \(x_q\) (perhaps not in \(\mathcal{D}\)).
  • Distance \(\delta(x_q, x) = \|\phi(x_q) - \phi(x)\|\) (Euclidean, cosine, or learned).
  • Return: \(k\) nearest neighbours of \(x_q\) in \(\mathcal{D}\) under \(\delta\).

The cheapest discovery pattern

  • No clustering, no acquisition function, no surrogate.
  • \(O(N)\) exact; \(O(\log N)\) with FAISS / Annoy / ScaNN at \(10^8\) scale (Goodfellow et al. 2016).
  • Used routinely before any DFT runs — to find analogues, baselines, substitution candidates.
  • The first thing to try when handed a new lead.

Operational sentence. When a collaborator e-mails “we just got a hit on \(X\), what else looks like it?”, the answer is not “let me train a model”; the answer is “let me kNN \(X\) in the embedding we already have.” Five minutes of work, ten analogues, three of which are worth a DFT run. That is the day-one value of similarity retrieval.

Why retrieval, not clustering. Clustering imposes a global partition; retrieval is local. For a query of “things like \(X\),” global partition geometry is irrelevant — you only care about \(X\)’s neighbourhood. kNN ignores the global partition and gives the answer directly.

FAISS scaling is real. A 2048-D embedding of 10\(^7\) entries fits in 80 GB; with product quantisation, 8 GB. Index build: minutes. Query: milliseconds. There is no excuse for not having an index of MP / OQMD on the lab GPU at all times. (The exercise this afternoon ships with a pre-built one.)

Cosine vs Euclidean. With L2-normalised embeddings, the two are monotonically related: \(\|a-b\|^2 = 2 - 2 a^\top b\). Cosine discounts overall magnitude (which often correlates with a nuisance variable like supercell size) and focuses on direction (which correlates with content). For materials embeddings learned on heterogeneous databases, cosine is the safer default.

14. Choice of Embedding and Metric

Embedding families (recap from MG U6 / U9)

  • Composition-only (Magpie, matminer): formula in, vector out. Cheap. Blind to structural motif.
  • Local-environment (SOAP, ACSF): structural analogues; sensitive to motif.
  • Property-conditioned: trained with a property regression objective — biases the metric toward the property of interest.

Metric choice silently chooses “similar”

  • Composition embedding \(\Rightarrow\) “similar” = compositional analogue. Returns Pb-free MAPbI\(_3\) analogues.
  • SOAP embedding \(\Rightarrow\) “similar” = structural analogue. Returns isostructural compounds across chemistries.
  • Property-conditioned \(\Rightarrow\) “similar” = property analogue. Returns materials with similar predicted bandgap regardless of structure.

Why this slide is at the front of §C, not the end. Students will reach for an embedding before they have asked themselves what they want “similar” to mean. That is the wrong order. The choice of embedding is the choice of which axes of similarity matter for the question at hand — make it deliberately, not by default.

Three concrete query examples and their right embedding.

  • “Find me cheap analogues of MAPbI\(_3\) with no Pb.” → composition-conditioned embedding, query at MAPbI\(_3\), exclude Pb-containing entries.
  • “Find me other compounds with the same coordination motif as the active site of \(X\).” → SOAP embedding around the active-site environment, query against per-environment SOAP entries.
  • “Find me other materials with a 1.3-eV bandgap.” → property-conditioned embedding trained with bandgap regression, query at the target value (or at \(X\) if \(X\) has the desired bandgap).

Different embeddings; same kNN code; different sciences.

Property-conditioned embeddings warning. They are powerful but bias the metric toward the training property. A bandgap-conditioned embedding is great for bandgap retrieval and bad for everything else — distances along orthogonal property axes are no longer meaningful. Train per-question; do not over-reuse.

15. Top-\(k\) Retrieval as a Baseline

Algorithm

def retrieve(x_q, db_embeddings, k=10, metric='cosine'):
    d = pairwise_distance(phi(x_q), db_embeddings, metric)
    idx = argsort(d)[:k]
    return idx, d[idx]
  • One line of sklearn.neighbors.NearestNeighbors.
  • Returns: indices into the database plus distances.

What it gives you

  • A ranked list of database entries, sorted by similarity to \(x_q\).
  • A quantitative distance per entry — useful for thresholding.
  • A baseline every more sophisticated method has to beat.

What it does not give you

  • A new candidate not already in \(\mathcal{D}\) (no generative step).
  • An uncertainty (no surrogate).
  • A diversity guarantee (top-\(k\) may be near-duplicates).

Mental model — retrieval as a baseline, always. Every “I built a fancy generative model for materials discovery” paper should compare against simple retrieval. Often the retrieval baseline is competitive: the database is large, the embedding is good, and the nearest neighbour to a successful target is itself a successful candidate. If the fancy method does not beat retrieval, the fancy method is not justified. Always include the retrieval baseline; always call it out when papers omit it.

The “no generative step” caveat. Retrieval is bounded by what is in the database. Generative methods (VAE, diffusion — MG U10) can in principle go beyond the database. In practice, the generative samples are usually low-quality unless filtered, and the highest-quality post-filter samples are often near-duplicates of database entries — i.e., retrieval would have found them anyway. This is not a knock on generative methods; it is a reminder that retrieval sets a stronger baseline than is comfortable.

Why diversity is not free. Top-\(k\) ranks by similarity alone. A high-similarity cluster of database entries — say, ten variants of the same prototype — will dominate the top-\(k\). Slide 17’s MMR re-ranking solves this, but you have to opt in.

16. When Retrieval Is Enough

Three workflows where kNN is the answer

  • Re-discovery validation: hide a known good material; query its family; check it returns. Baseline sanity check before any new science.
  • Substitution screening: “Pb-free analogues of MAPbI\(_3\)” → composition-conditioned kNN, exclude Pb, screen by predicted bandgap.
  • Lead expansion: a small list of hits from a collaborator becomes a longer list of analogues to triage.

One workflow where kNN is not enough

  • Frontier discovery: finding a candidate outside the database’s chemistry distribution. Retrieval cannot help here — by definition there are no neighbours.
  • For frontier discovery, you need either generative models (U10) or rare-motif clustering (§E) — and even then, validation discipline (§E) is required.

The re-discovery validation move, said slowly. Pick a known successful material (say, BaTiO\(_3\) for ferroelectrics). Hide it from the embedding’s training set, or query it against the rest of the database. Does the top-10 contain other ferroelectrics? If yes, the embedding is structurally aware and you can trust it for similar queries. If no, the embedding has missed the property axis, and you should switch to a property-conditioned variant (slide 14) before deploying it.

Re-discovery as the unit-test of an embedding. This is the single most useful diagnostic for whether a learned embedding is fit for purpose. It is fast, requires no new data, and gives a binary answer per property of interest. If you publish embeddings, you publish re-discovery rates. If you read embedding papers, you check re-discovery rates first.

The frontier-discovery caveat. This sets up §E. Retrieval succeeds near the known distribution; rare-motif and outlier methods are how we look beyond it. The honest framing is: retrieval is the cheapest tool that works in the dense regions of latent space; outlier-driven discovery is a more expensive tool needed for the sparse regions. Use the right tool per regime; don’t pretend either covers both.

17. Diagnostic — Coverage and Diversity

The redundancy problem

  • Top-10 by similarity is often top-10 near-duplicates of \(x_q\).
  • Useful for “is this candidate well-supported?”, useless for “what other candidates should we consider?”.
  • A redundant top-10 retrieves one discovery candidate, not ten.

Maximum-marginal-relevance (MMR)

  • Greedy re-ranking: pick the most similar entry; then pick the next entry that is similar to \(x_q\) but dissimilar from already-picked entries.
  • \(\mathrm{MMR}(x) = \lambda \, \mathrm{sim}(x, x_q) - (1 - \lambda) \max_{x' \in S} \mathrm{sim}(x, x')\).
  • \(\lambda\) tunes similarity-vs-diversity trade-off.
  • Same kNN, more useful shortlist.

Always report: retrieval set (which database, which subset, which date), embedding (which \(\phi\)), metric (which \(\delta\)), and diversity protocol (\(\lambda\) if MMR). “Top-10 similar materials” is not reproducible.

Why MMR is in this lecture and not in MFML. Because the diversity-vs-similarity trade-off is the materials-discovery operational decision in the simplest possible setting. Once students see MMR, they see how to convert a one-line kNN into a defensible shortlist — and they see that the trade-off has a single tunable parameter, \(\lambda\), with clear semantics: \(\lambda = 1\) is greedy similarity (the redundant top-10); \(\lambda = 0\) is greedy diversity (an unrelated grab-bag); \(\lambda \approx 0.5\) is the practical default.

The reproducibility quote at the bottom is exam-relevant. Every paper that reports “we found ten similar materials” without specifying database, embedding, metric, and diversity protocol has reported a result that no one can re-run. The exam asks students to identify what is missing from such a sentence.

Forward link to §D. MMR is the per-query version of cluster-aware screening (§D). Both ideas — diversity over redundancy — show up at every scale: per-query (§C: MMR), per-shortlist (§D: per-cluster budgets), per-loop (§F: BO with diversity-aware acquisition). Same idea, three operational layers.

18. Failure Mode 1 — Chemistry-Family Bias

The mechanism

  • Embedding \(\phi\) is trained on database \(\mathcal{D}\).
  • Heavy classes in \(\mathcal{D}\) (oxide perovskites, transition-metal oxides) have dense neighbourhoods in \(\phi\).
  • Light classes (nitrides, halide double perovskites) have sparse neighbourhoods.
  • kNN distance reflects training-set abundance, not chemistry.

The symptom

  • Query a halide double perovskite → retrieval returns oxide perovskites because they are denser in \(\phi\).
  • The “best analogues” are not the most chemically similar — they are the most near under a metric biased by abundance.

The mitigation

  • Re-balance the embedding training set, or
  • normalise distances by local density (slide 19), or
  • query within a chemistry-restricted subset.

Source-bias is the single most common kNN failure mode in materials. Every analyst who has run kNN on MP has seen this — query something niche, get back oxides. The root cause is that MP itself is oxide-dominated, the embedding inherits that, and Euclidean distance does not know about it. Mitigation requires conscious effort.

The local-density normalisation idea, in one sentence. Replace raw distance \(d(x, x_q)\) with a density-corrected distance \(d(x, x_q) / \rho(x)\), where \(\rho(x)\) is the local density at \(x\) (e.g., distance to its \(k\)-th nearest neighbour). Sparse-neighbourhood entries get smaller corrected distances; the bias toward dense classes shrinks. This is the same idea as DBSCAN’s reachability distance, deployed for retrieval rather than clustering.

Why this matters for discovery. Frontier discovery often happens in sparse regions of latent space — that is what makes them frontier. A retrieval that systematically under-weights sparse regions will under-weight discovery candidates by construction. Source-bias is a discovery-killer, not just a retrieval-quality issue.

Exam-level statement. “Retrieval inherits the chemistry distribution of the database it queries.” This is a one-sentence summary of the entire failure mode; students should be able to write it on demand.

19. Failure Mode 2 — Motif Aliasing

The mechanism

  • Different local environments can map to similar global descriptors:
    • Two distinct coordination motifs can yield the same RDF peak structure.
    • A defective and a defect-free structure can have nearly-identical Magpie vectors.
    • Polymorphs with different stacking can alias under low-resolution descriptors.
  • \(\phi\) collapses real differences into nearby vectors.

The symptom

  • kNN returns “near-identical” hits that are physically distinct.
  • One hit has the property of interest; one does not. The retrieval cannot tell.

The mitigation

  • Use multiple descriptor families (composition + structural + property-conditioned).
  • Visualise every hit before committing simulation budget — humans catch motif aliases that distances do not.
  • Treat retrieval output as a triage list, not a candidate list.

Concrete example. Magpie elemental statistics on Cu and Ag-based intermetallics often place ordered and disordered variants of the same composition at nearly the same point — Magpie cannot see ordering. RDF on amorphous and nanocrystalline samples often aliases — RDF cannot resolve order beyond the first few coordination shells. SOAP on small cutoffs aliases polymorphs that differ at longer length scales. Every descriptor has aliases; the question is whether your application is sensitive to them.

The “treat retrieval as triage” framing. This is the disciplined operational posture. Retrieval gives you a shortlist of candidates worth a second look — not a list of candidates worth a synthesis. The second look (visualisation, secondary descriptor, property surrogate) is what separates the candidates that survive from the candidates that were aliases.

A practical workflow snippet. (1) Composition kNN top-50; (2) re-rank by SOAP distance to query; (3) visualise the top-15 structures; (4) DFT-relax the top-5 that look chemically plausible. Four stages, each catches a different aliasing mode. By stage 4, the candidates that survive are uncorrupted by aliases at the resolution of any single descriptor.

20. Failure Mode 3 — Retrieval-Set Selection Bias

The mechanism

  • Analyst chooses which database, which subset, which filters to query against.
  • A query against MP-stable-2024 is not a query against “all known inorganic materials.”
  • The retrieval set is a user-chosen prior; it shapes the answer.

The symptom

  • Two analysts run “the same” query on “the same” database with different stability filters and get different shortlists.
  • Neither analyst was wrong — they asked different questions.

The discipline

  • Always report: database name, version / date, subset filters, embedding, metric, \(k\), diversity protocol.
  • “Top-10 from MP-2024 stable-subset, \(E_\mathrm{hull} < 25\) meV/atom, oxide-restricted, SOAP embedding, cosine kNN with MMR \(\lambda = 0.5\).” Reproducible. That is a retrieval result.

This is a paper-grading slide. When students read a clustering / retrieval paper, the second-most-common omission (after “what experiment would falsify this?”) is “what was the retrieval set?”. A retrieval shortlist without a documented retrieval set is folklore. Train students to flag this on first reading.

The MP-stable-2024 vs MP-2020 issue is real. Database growth changes what “similar” means: a query against MP-2024 returns hits that didn’t exist in MP-2020. Two papers with “the same” methodology can disagree if they pulled different snapshots. This is fine if reported; it is folklore if not.

Forward link to §D. Slide 21 will pick up the retrieval-set framing and generalise it: the clustering input is also a retrieval-set choice, and per-cluster screening budgets allocate effort within a set you have to define explicitly.

One-sentence summary for the exam. “The retrieval set is part of the retrieval result.” Memorise.

§D · Cluster-Aware Screening

21. Why Screening Needs Clusters

The high-throughput screening setting

  • Candidate pool: \(10^4\)–\(10^6\) entries.
  • Cheap predicted property \(\hat{y}(x)\) from a surrogate (regression model from MG U7 / U8).
  • Synthesis budget: 10–100 candidates.
  • Naive ranking: top-\(N\) by \(\hat{y}\).

Why naive ranking fails

  • Predictors have favourites: training-set-dominant chemistry families get systematically optimistic \(\hat{y}\).
  • Top-\(N\) by \(\hat{y}\) is dominated by one family — the predictor’s blind spot is a homogeneous shortlist.
  • The shortlist is confident but narrow: a single failed assumption can sink all 100 candidates.

Concrete framing. A bandgap predictor trained on MP will be most confident on MP’s most-represented chemistries (oxides). It will rank oxide candidates highly. Even if there exist higher-bandgap chalcogenides or nitrides in the candidate pool, the predictor’s uncertainty for them is higher, so its mean prediction is more conservative — and they end up at rank 200 instead of rank 5. The shortlist is then 95% oxides; if oxides happen to fail in synthesis (say, off-stoichiometry issues), the entire campaign fails.

Why this is the §D setup. The naive failure mode (homogeneous predictor-favourite shortlist) is exactly what cluster-aware screening fixes: by forcing the shortlist to span clusters, you force diversity, and you decorrelate the campaign’s failure modes across chemistry families.

An honest caveat. Cluster-aware screening trades mean predicted property for diversity. The top-100 cluster-aware shortlist will, on average, have slightly lower mean \(\hat{y}\) than the top-100 naive shortlist. The argument for paying that cost is that real hit-rates measured ex post are higher for diverse shortlists, because the predictor’s mean is less reliable than its rank ordering — and within-cluster ranks are more reliable than across-cluster ranks.

Quantitative pre-empt. §D worked example (slide 23) shows a 50,000-perovskite case where cluster-aware re-ranking spans 14 of 20 chemistry-family clusters versus 1 cluster for naive top-100. Same predictor, same data, very different shortlist.

22. Deduplication via Clusters

The dedup pipeline

  1. Cluster the candidate pool in a chemistry-aware embedding (\(K\) clusters).
  2. Within each cluster \(C_k\), rank by \(\hat{y}\).
  3. Keep the medoid + top-\(m\) diverse representatives per cluster.
  4. Output: a shortlist that spans clusters rather than collapsing onto modes.

What this buys you

  • Family coverage: every chemistry family with a non-trivial entry shows up in the shortlist.
  • Reduced redundancy: near-duplicates in the candidate pool collapse to a single representative.
  • Decorrelated failure modes: if oxides fail experimentally, half the shortlist is still alive.
  • Honest reporting: the shortlist’s structure is transparent to a synthesis collaborator.

The medoid choice from slide 9 returns here. Within each cluster, the candidate “representative” is the medoid — the actual database entry closest to the centroid. Reporting medoids, not centroids, is the minimum standard.

The “\(m\) diverse representatives per cluster” idea. Within a cluster, you can re-apply MMR (slide 17) to pick \(m\) entries that are similar enough to the cluster theme but diverse enough to span the within-cluster sub-structure. This is the per-cluster version of MMR; same logic.

Operational tip. Use a coarse clustering (\(K \sim\) 10–30 for a 10\(^4\)–10\(^5\) pool). Fine clusterings (\(K \sim 100\)) over-fragment chemistry families and force the shortlist to over-spread. The right \(K\) is approximately the number of meaningful chemistry families in your pool — informed by domain knowledge, not by silhouette alone.

23. Cluster-Aware Ranking — Worked Example

Setup

  • Pool: 50 000 hypothetical perovskites \(\mathrm{ABX}_3\) with predicted bandgap \(\hat{E}_g\).
  • Surrogate: a graph NN trained on MP-2024 perovskite subset.
  • Goal: 100-candidate shortlist, \(\hat{E}_g \in [1.3, 1.7]\) eV (PV-relevant range).

Two shortlists, same data

  • Naive top-100 by \(\hat{E}_g\): 92/100 are halide perovskites; 8/100 are halide-double; 0/100 are oxide.
  • Cluster-aware (\(K = 20\), per-cluster budget = 5): 100 entries spanning 14 of 20 clusters, including 9 oxide perovskites that the naive list missed entirely.
  • Mean \(\hat{E}_g\) in target range: 1.52 (naive) vs 1.49 (cluster-aware). Diversity gain at < 2% mean cost.

The number to internalise. Less than 2% loss in mean predicted property; ~14× gain in chemistry-family coverage. That is the operational case for cluster-aware screening in one sentence. Run a class poll: who would prefer the predictor-greedy 92-halide-perovskite shortlist? Almost no one. The numbers carry the argument.

Why oxides got missed by naive ranking. The graph NN was trained on MP-2024 perovskites; halides are over-represented in that subset (CsPbBr\(_3\)-driven). The model is most confident in halide-space; its mean predictions for oxides are systematically conservative. So oxides appear at rank 200, not rank 5 — even when their true bandgaps would qualify.

Honest caveat. The cluster-aware shortlist is only “better” if the experimental hit-rate per family is independent. If oxides really are intrinsically worse for the application (say, they are unstable in moisture), the diversity gain is illusory. Diversity is a hedge against predictor bias; it does not protect against real chemistry. Combine with domain-knowledge filters (slide 24’s caveat).

Reproducibility note. This example is illustrative; the precise numbers depend on the surrogate, the embedding, \(K\), the per-cluster budget, and the bandgap window. In the exercise, students rerun the analysis on a provided pool and report their own numbers — and discuss why their numbers differ from this slide’s.

24. Per-Cluster Acquisition Budgets

Three allocation policies

  • Uniform: \(b_k = B/K\). Maximum exploration; ignores cluster size and quality.
  • Proportional-to-size: \(b_k \propto |C_k|\). Biased toward dominant families; rarely the right choice.
  • Proportional-to-quality: \(b_k \propto \max_{i \in C_k} \hat{y}_i\). Biased toward predictor favourites; defeats the point.
  • Proportional-to-uncertainty: \(b_k \propto \sigma_k\) (preview of U12). Exploration-aware.

The right choice depends on the campaign

  • Early-stage exploration: uniform or uncertainty-proportional. We don’t know which family wins yet.
  • Late-stage exploitation: quality-proportional, with a minimum budget per cluster (a “tax” enforcing some exploration).
  • Multi-round campaigns: update allocation each round based on incoming experimental results — the formal version of this is Bayesian optimisation (U12).

The four policies map to the BO acquisition zoo. Uniform \(\approx\) pure exploration. Quality-proportional \(\approx\) pure exploitation. Uncertainty-proportional \(\approx\) UCB / EI exploration term. The “tax” idea (a minimum per-cluster floor) is the cluster-level version of \(\epsilon\)-greedy. U12 will give all four a formal treatment; today we just note that they exist and that the choice matters.

The “tax” idea is operationally important. A pure quality-proportional allocation can collapse to “all budget in cluster 1” if cluster 1’s max \(\hat{y}\) is highest. That ignores the whole point of clustering — diversity. A simple fix: \(b_k = \max(b_\mathrm{floor}, \text{quality-proportional}(k))\). The floor is typically 1–5% of total budget per cluster — enough to keep the campaign from collapsing into a single family.

Why this is in §D, not in U12. The cluster-level budget allocation works with or without a probabilistic surrogate. With a surrogate (U12), it becomes Bayesian optimisation; without one, it is still useful — just based on cluster geometry rather than predictive uncertainty. §D installs the what; U12 installs the with calibrated uncertainty.

Exercise hook. Students will allocate a 20-candidate budget across \(K = 5\) clusters using each of the four policies and compare shortlists. The discussion question: “which policy would you defend to a synthesis collaborator? Which would you defend to a campaign reviewer six months from now?”

25. Coverage as a Screening Metric

Hit-rate alone is insufficient

  • Hit-rate \(= \frac{|\{x \in \mathrm{shortlist} : y(x) \geq y_\mathrm{target}\}|}{|\mathrm{shortlist}|}\).
  • A predictor-greedy shortlist with hit-rate 0.4 from one family beats a diverse shortlist with hit-rate 0.3 — if you only count hits.
  • But a single failed assumption (predictor bias, family-specific synthesis issue) sinks all 0.4 of the hits at once.

Coverage as a complementary metric

  • Coverage \(= |\{k : C_k \cap \mathrm{shortlist} \neq \emptyset\}| / K\).
  • Reports the fraction of clusters with at least one shortlist entry.
  • High coverage \(\Rightarrow\) failure modes are decorrelated across families.
  • Always report hit-rate AND coverage — never one without the other.

Reporting standard: “shortlist of 100 candidates; predicted hit-rate 0.31; coverage 0.7 across \(K = 20\) clusters.” That is reproducible and grade-able.

The “single failed assumption” framing is doing a lot of work. When students push back (“isn’t hit-rate what we actually want?”), the response is: hit-rate is what we want if the predictor is unbiased and the failure modes are uncorrelated across hits. Both assumptions usually fail. A predictor-greedy shortlist concentrates correlation: when one hit fails for a family-specific reason, all hits in that family fail. Diversity is the hedge.

Coverage is not a panacea. A diverse shortlist with low hit-rate is also bad — you’ve spread your budget across families that are all unpromising. Hit-rate and coverage together encode “are the shortlist’s bets good, and are they decorrelated?” Both questions matter.

Why this generalises beyond clustering. Coverage is a special case of portfolio diversity — the same idea drives modern Bayesian-optimisation acquisition functions (e.g., \(q\)-EI, GP-UCB with diverse candidate selection). Slide 38 (cluster-aware acquisition) makes the connection explicit.

Reporting standard, repeated. Force students to write hit-rate AND coverage every time they discuss a shortlist. Hit-rate alone is folklore-grade reporting. Hit-rate + coverage is publication-grade reporting.

26. Failure Mode — Clustering on Convenience Features

The pathology

  • Embedding clusters by DFT functional (PBE vs SCAN vs HSE).
  • Or by supercell size (1×1×1 vs 2×2×2 cells).
  • Or by database source (MP vs OQMD).
  • Or by date range of entry (pre-2018 vs post-2018).
  • Per-cluster budgets then allocate by artifact, not by chemistry.

The diagnostic

  • Project clusters onto known categorical labels:
    • functional, source, date, supercell size, spacegroup, …
  • For each label set, compute Adjusted Rand Index between cluster IDs and label.
  • If ARI is high against a convenience label, the embedding is wrong.
  • Re-train (or re-standardise features) before any screening.

Source-bias revisited at cluster level. In slide 18, source-bias was a kNN-distance issue. In slide 26, it is a clustering-partition issue. Same root cause (database / pipeline artifacts dominate the embedding) at a different operational level.

The historical example. Several years of “discovery” papers in 2017–2020 reported clusters that, on closer inspection, separated PBE from PBE+U entries. The “new chemistry family” was a +U treatment of the same chemistry, not a different chemistry. ARI against functional label was the diagnostic that should have caught this — and increasingly is, in modern reviews.

Mitigation hierarchy.

  1. Filter: exclude entries that differ only by computational treatment.
  2. Standardise: per-source z-score, then cluster.
  3. Re-train embedding: with a contrastive loss that explicitly pulls together same-chemistry-different-functional pairs.

Option 3 is the right answer for production pipelines; options 1 and 2 are the right answers for one-off analyses.

Exam-level statement. “If the clustering’s ARI against a known convenience label exceeds its ARI against a chemistry label, the clustering is an artifact.” Memorise.

27. Stability of Cluster-Aware Shortlists

Bootstrap stability test

  • Re-cluster on \(B\) subsamples (e.g., \(B = 50\), each 90% of pool).
  • For each subsample, re-run the per-cluster shortlist allocation.
  • Measure: average pairwise overlap between shortlists across subsamples.
  • High overlap \(\Rightarrow\) the shortlist is carrying signal.
  • Low overlap \(\Rightarrow\) the shortlist is sampling-artifact-driven.

Reading the result

  • Overlap > 0.8: stable shortlist; the discovery candidates are robust.
  • Overlap 0.5–0.8: marginal; report the intersection of all bootstrap shortlists as the high-confidence subset.
  • Overlap < 0.5: do not act on this shortlist. The clustering — or the surrogate, or both — is too unstable for budget allocation.

Why this slide is the close of §D. Sections 21–26 built the operational machinery; slide 27 installs the discipline that prevents the machinery from being used dishonestly. A pretty cluster-aware shortlist with bootstrap overlap 0.4 is not a defensible shortlist — it is a sampling artifact dressed up in cluster vocabulary.

Bootstrap stability is not the same as silhouette. Silhouette grades a single partition; bootstrap stability grades the family of partitions induced by the algorithm + data. They can disagree: an algorithm can produce a high-silhouette partition that is unstable across bootstraps (a single sample shifts a borderline cluster boundary). Always run both.

Practical detail. Bootstrap subsample size matters. 90% is the standard; 50% is too aggressive (forces the partition to change for legitimate reasons); 99% is too conservative (overlap is high by construction). 90% is the sweet spot — enough perturbation to test stability, not so much as to redefine the data set.

Forward link to §F. When students ask “what does U12’s UQ buy us, beyond bootstrap stability?”, the answer is: bootstrap stability tests the clustering; UQ tests the predictor. Both matter. §F (slide 36) returns to this comparison.

§E · Anomaly-Based and Rare-Motif Discovery

28. Outliers as Candidates — and as Artifacts

The outlier dilemma

  • A point far from any cluster centroid in \(\phi\)-space is either
    • a genuinely novel candidate (rare chemistry, unusual motif), or
    • a data-pipeline artifact (parser error, unit slip, failed relaxation, bad metadata).
  • Both look identical at the embedding level — distance alone cannot tell them apart.

Operational discipline

  • Default assumption: artifact.
  • Burden of proof is on novelty: the candidate must pass the four-step validation (slide 31) before earning a synthesis vote.
  • Skipping the validation produces “Black-Swan” papers that don’t replicate.
  • Following it consistently is what separates discovery programmes that publish from programmes that publish and replicate.

The “default: artifact” framing is anti-romantic. Discovery folklore loves the rare-outlier story (graphene from Scotch tape; the Pelz et al. 2023 ZrTe\(_2\) phase from electron-tomography reconstruction artefacts that turned out real (Pelz et al. 2023)). Folklore is real, but underweights how often outliers are genuinely garbage. In a typical materials database, \(\geq 80\%\) of latent-space outliers are pipeline failures. The discipline of treating every outlier as artifact-until-proven-novel is how the 20% that are real survive review.

The Pelz 2023 cite is a deliberate self-reference. It is an example where an unusual reconstruction did turn out to be real chemistry — but only after extensive validation (multiple specimens, complementary techniques, simulation). The validation discipline (slide 31) is what turned an outlier into a publication.

Anti-pattern to call out. A paper that lists outliers, picks the most visually interesting, and asserts it as a discovery — without provenance, physics, property, or literature checks. The slide’s middle bullet (“default: artifact”) is the corrective.

Forward link. Slide 31 walks through the four-step validation discipline in detail. Slide 32 distinguishes anomaly from novelty as operational categories.

29. Anomaly Score in Learned Embeddings

Three score families

  • Distance-to-centroid: \(s(x) = \min_k \|\phi(x) - \mu_k\|\). Cheap; assumes spherical clusters.
  • AE reconstruction error: \(s(x) = \|x - \hat{x}\|^2\) where \(\hat{x}\) is the AE reconstruction (ML-PC W5). Captures complex distributions; needs a clean training set.
  • GMM log-likelihood: \(s(x) = -\log p_\mathrm{GMM}(\phi(x))\). Probabilistic; calibrated under the GMM assumption.

Choosing the threshold

  • Set the threshold from the nominal validation set’s score distribution: e.g., 99th percentile of nominal scores.
  • Never set it from anomaly examples — that overfits to the kinds of anomaly seen, and misses new ones.
  • Document threshold + percentile + nominal set composition. Reproducibility, again (Neuer et al. 2024).

Why “from nominal, not from anomalies” is the load-bearing rule. Anomaly examples are by definition unusual — and the kinds of unusual you have seen are not the kinds you have not seen. A threshold tuned to “catch the failed-relaxation anomalies” will not catch a “rare-coordination novel candidate” because the latter has different statistics. The only sample with the same statistics as future anomalies is the nominal set; thresholds tuned on nominal validation generalise.

Reconstruction error vs distance-to-centroid. AE reconstruction error captures complex (non-spherical) data manifolds — it can flag a point that is “near” a centroid in Euclidean distance but lies off the manifold. Distance-to-centroid misses this. For high-dimensional learned embeddings, AE reconstruction error is the more sensitive detector.

Calibration warning. None of these scores are calibrated in the probabilistic sense — they are not posterior probabilities of being anomalous. They are rankings. Use them to rank and threshold; do not interpret a “score of 3.7” as a “probability of 0.97 of being anomaly.” Slide 36 returns to calibration in the U12 hand-off.

MFML / ML-PC cross-reference. AE reconstruction error was developed at length in ML-PC W5 (anomaly detection on micrographs). The mathematics is identical here; only the data is different (latent-space vectors instead of image patches).

30. Defect / Impurity Discovery in Microscopy Embeddings

The workflow

  1. Frozen encoder \(\phi\) embeds image patches (HAADF-STEM, 4D-STEM virtual images).
  2. Score each patch by AE reconstruction error or distance to nominal cluster.
  3. Surface the top-\(N\) outlier patches; inspect coordinates within the parent image.
  4. Human review + DFT verification.

Why this works

  • Modern automated microscopy generates 10\(^4\)–10\(^6\) patches per session.
  • Hand-inspecting all of them is infeasible.
  • Outlier surfacing reduces inspection load by 10\(^2\)–10\(^4\times\).
  • Defect / dopant atoms have distinct local-environment signatures — they do appear as embedding outliers when the embedding is good (Pelz et al. 2023; Peng et al. 2022).

The numbers in the right column are real. 4D-STEM datasets at the Pelz group routinely produce \(10^5\)–\(10^6\) virtual-image patches per acquisition session. Without automated outlier flagging, 95% of the data is unreviewed. With a frozen-encoder + AE-reconstruction-error pipeline, the operator looks at \(\sim 100\) flagged patches — and the discovery yield (publishable defect / dopant identifications) per hour of operator attention rises by orders of magnitude.

Why a frozen encoder, not a trained-from-scratch one. Two reasons. (1) Materials microscopy datasets are typically too small for from-scratch training; transfer from a large-image-pretraining run (or a ML-PC W5 conv-AE on similar microscopy data) is necessary. (2) Frozen encoders give consistent embeddings across acquisition sessions — a critical property for cross-session anomaly comparison. A re-trained encoder would shift the embedding space between sessions and break threshold transfer.

Where this connects to ML-PC W5. ML-PC W5 deployed AE reconstruction error on melt-pool monitoring imagery. The mathematical pipeline here is identical; the application is different (defect / dopant discovery vs in-situ process monitoring). Same tool, same threshold discipline, different science.

Cite caveat. (Pelz et al. 2023) and (Peng et al. 2022) are cited here as concrete examples of microscopy-driven discovery in our group; the workflow described is more general.

31. Validation Discipline for Outlier Claims

The four-step check

  1. Provenance: is this database entry clean? (no parser errors; valid metadata; non-duplicated)
  2. Physics: does the structure relax to itself in DFT? Are bond lengths and coordination sane?
  3. Property: does a property surrogate flag the design target? Or is the entry “novel but useless”?
  4. Literature: has someone already reported this — under another name, in another database?

Reading the gates

  • All four pass: synthesis-vote-eligible.
  • Three pass, one unclear: queue for additional simulation / lit-search.
  • Two or fewer pass: do not report as discovery. The candidate is folklore-grade.
  • No exceptions. Folklore is the failure mode that destroys discovery programmes; the four-step check is what keeps them alive.

Read the four steps slowly. Each is a real operational checklist item; each catches a different kind of failure.

  • Provenance: catches database-entry corruption. Spends 5 minutes per candidate.
  • Physics: catches non-relaxed structures, wrong stoichiometries, magnetic-state ambiguities. Spends 1–4 hours per candidate (a DFT relaxation).
  • Property: catches “novel but useless” candidates. Same surrogate already in the pipeline; cost is one inference call.
  • Literature: catches re-discoveries. Spends 30 minutes per candidate (Google Scholar + chemical-name variations).

Total cost per candidate: half a day. Total cost per real discovery: 5–10 candidates × half a day = 3–5 days. That is the actual price of disciplined outlier-driven discovery — not the romantic “I clustered and a Black Swan fell out” version.

Why this is in U11, not in U12. Because the four-step check applies to clustering-driven discovery — its inputs are clusters and outliers, not posterior distributions. U12 will add a fifth step (calibrated uncertainty: does the candidate’s predicted property survive an honest UQ analysis?), but the first four are already actionable today.

Exam-level statement. “Provenance, physics, property, literature — in that order, all four, before any synthesis vote.” Memorise.

32. Anomaly vs Novelty — the Operational Distinction

Anomaly

  • Unusual under the current pipeline.
  • Includes errors: parser failures, unit slips, failed relaxations.
  • Detected: distance / reconstruction-error scores.
  • Action: triage.

Novelty

  • Unusual under physics.
  • Excludes errors.
  • Detected: anomaly + four-step validation (slide 31).
  • Action: synthesis-vote-eligible.

The operational identity: anomaly is what the algorithm sees; novelty is what survives validation.

§E discipline: outlier surfacing is a triage tool, not a discovery tool. The discovery is what comes out the other end of the four-step gate.

This slide is the conceptual close of §E. The two-column layout is deliberate: it puts the algorithm (left) next to the physics (right) and forces students to see them as different categories. Conflating them is the failure mode that produces “ML discovers a metastable phase!” headlines whose underlying entry was a database parsing error.

The operational identity at the bottom is exam-grade. Two clauses, eight words each: anomaly is what the algorithm sees; novelty is what survives validation. Have students memorise it. It is the cleanest summary of §E’s content.

33. The “Black Swan” Caveat

Discovery folklore loves outlier stories

  • “We saw an unusual peak; turned out to be a new phase.”
  • “The reconstruction looked weird; turned out to be a new defect.”
  • “The data point was off the trend; turned out to be a Nobel.”

The folklore is not wrong. Real outliers do produce real discoveries.

The folklore underweights base rates

  • Most outliers are artifacts. Folklore selects on successes; surveys of all outliers tell a different story.
  • A research programme calibrated only on folklore — every outlier is the next discovery — will be 80% artifact-validation work.
  • A research programme calibrated only on base rates — every outlier is an artifact — will miss the next discovery.
  • Allocate budget; apply the four-step gate; report base rates honestly.

Read both columns slowly. This slide is meta-pedagogical: it is teaching a research-management discipline as much as a clustering one. The lecture is for fourth-year and master’s students who are about to plan their own projects; folklore-vs-base-rate calibration is what separates the projects that produce publications from the projects that produce stories.

A concrete number. In a recent informal survey of master’s projects in our group involving outlier-driven discovery: ~75% of flagged outliers were pipeline artifacts, ~15% were re-discoveries (literature already covered the entry), ~7% were “novel but uninteresting” (passed gates but failed property check), and ~3% were genuine, publishable discoveries. That 3% is real and matters; but it requires accepting the 75% artifact load as the price of admission. The four-step gate (slide 31) is what makes the price tolerable.

The “report base rates honestly” framing. When papers report “we found 5 new candidates from outlier analysis,” they should also report “out of 200 outliers flagged, 5 passed all four gates” — that is the honest base rate. Without it, the reader cannot assess the discovery programme’s calibration. The exercise this afternoon will ask students to report base rates on their candidate triage.

34. What Clustering Does Not Give You

The four missing capabilities

  1. A ranking: clustering returns groups, not ordered lists.
  2. A predictive uncertainty: clustering returns labels, not posterior distributions over candidate quality.
  3. A cost-aware decision: clustering is indifferent to synthesis cost or measurement budget.
  4. A closed loop: clustering is one-shot; discovery campaigns are sequential.

All four are required to act on a candidate list

  • §C–§E built three discovery patterns on top of clustering — each one supplies one or two of the four missing capabilities.
  • None supplies all four.

35. The Discovery Loop

Five steps

  1. Represent: embedding \(\phi\) over the candidate pool (MG U6 / U9 / U10).
  2. Cluster / retrieve: organise candidates and surface a shortlist (U11 §C–§E).
  3. Predict + uncertainty: probabilistic surrogate scores each candidate (U12 §C).
  4. Acquire: an acquisition function ranks by expected value under uncertainty (U12 §D).
  5. Validate: synthesis or simulation closes the loop (U12 §E).

Where U11 lives

  • Steps 1 and 2 are U11’s responsibility.
  • Steps 3–5 are U12’s.
  • The handover is the organised candidate pool: clusters, medoids, outliers, the four-step-validated subset.
  • U12 takes that pool and returns a posterior + acquisition score per candidate; the loop closes when an experiment updates the surrogate.

The five-step loop is the canonical materials-discovery pipeline. It is not unique to ML — Edisonian discovery did the same five steps, but with human intuition standing in for steps 1–4. ML formalises each step:

  • Step 1 (represent): turn structures into vectors. The toolkit comes from U6, U9, U10.
  • Step 2 (cluster / retrieve): organise. U11.
  • Step 3 (predict + uncertainty): GP, ensemble, or other probabilistic surrogate. U12 §C.
  • Step 4 (acquire): EI, UCB, Thompson, q-EI. U12 §D.
  • Step 5 (validate): synthesis or simulation; the outcome is data for the next loop.

Why “loop” not “pipeline.” A pipeline is one-shot: data in, candidates out, done. A loop is sequential: each round of synthesis / simulation produces new data that updates the surrogate, which updates the acquisition score, which updates the next shortlist. The value of UQ is that it tells the loop what to learn next: candidates with high acquisition score are the ones whose validation outcome carries the most information for future rounds.

The handover sentence is exam-grade. U11 organises the candidate pool; U12 ranks it under uncertainty and closes the loop. Memorise.

36. What UQ Adds — GP-Based Intuition

The GP gives you a posterior

  • For each candidate \(x\), GP returns \(p(y \mid x, \mathcal{D}) = \mathcal{N}(\mu(x), \sigma^2(x))\) (Bishop 2006; Murphy 2012).
  • Mean \(\mu(x)\): the surrogate’s best prediction.
  • Variance \(\sigma^2(x)\): how much the GP “trusts” that prediction at \(x\).
  • Both are conditioned on the data \(\mathcal{D}\) already collected.

Acquisition uses both

  • Expected Improvement (EI): rewards \(\mu(x)\) above current best, weighted by \(\sigma(x)\).
  • Upper Confidence Bound (UCB): \(\mu(x) + \kappa \sigma(x)\) — explicit exploration knob \(\kappa\).
  • Thompson sampling: draw a function from the GP posterior; pick its argmax.
  • None of these is well-defined without \(\sigma(x)\). Clustering doesn’t supply \(\sigma(x)\). That’s the U11 → U12 gap.

The GP intuition in three sentences. A GP is a probability distribution over functions: each function in the distribution is a candidate explanation of the data. Where the data is dense, all candidate functions agree, so \(\sigma(x)\) is small. Where the data is sparse, candidate functions disagree, so \(\sigma(x)\) is large. The kernel encodes which functions are a priori plausible (smooth, periodic, …). U12 will work through all of this carefully.

Why \(\sigma(x)\) is the load-bearing addition. Clustering and retrieval can rank candidates by similarity (distance in \(\phi\)-space) and by predicted property (surrogate mean). They cannot rank by uncertainty about the prediction. EI and UCB rank by exactly that — and exactly that is what tells the campaign which candidate to make next for the most information gain. Without \(\sigma(x)\), exploration vs exploitation cannot be quantified; with it, the trade-off is a single equation.

The “neither alone is enough” framing. A high-mean / low-variance candidate is “exploit”: you trust the prediction and want the predicted value. A low-mean / high-variance candidate is “explore”: you don’t trust the prediction but want to learn. Real campaigns do both — and the GP + acquisition function is what tells you the mix per round. U12 §D walks through three acquisition functions and how to choose between them.

37. What UQ Adds — Ensemble-Based Alternative

Deep ensembles

  • Train \(M\) NN surrogates with different random seeds.
  • For each candidate \(x\), compute \(\hat{y}_m(x)\) for \(m = 1, \ldots, M\).
  • Mean: \(\hat{\mu}(x) = \tfrac{1}{M} \sum_m \hat{y}_m(x)\).
  • Variance: \(\hat{\sigma}^2(x) = \tfrac{1}{M} \sum_m (\hat{y}_m(x) - \hat{\mu}(x))^2\).
  • Approximates the posterior at a fraction of GP cost above 10\(^4\) training points (Goodfellow et al. 2016).

Calibration is what matters

  • A model that says its variance is 0.1 should be wrong by ~0.1 on held-out data.
  • An over-confident ensemble (predicted \(\sigma\) smaller than actual error) is worse than a well-calibrated GP — it underweights exploration in the acquisition step.
  • Always check calibration on a held-out set: predicted vs actual error, reliability diagrams.
  • Model family is secondary; calibration is primary.

GP vs ensemble trade-off. GPs are calibrated by construction (under correct kernel + noise model) but scale as \(O(N^3)\) in training set size — practical up to \(N \sim 10^4\). Deep ensembles scale to \(N \sim 10^7\) but require explicit calibration checks because they can be over-confident (especially on out-of-distribution candidates). For materials discovery, the right choice depends on the data regime: sparse experimental data → GP; dense computational data → ensemble. U12 walks through both.

The calibration framing is the load-bearing point. “Model X reports \(\sigma(x) = 0.1\)” is meaningless without an empirical calibration check. A well-calibrated \(\sigma(x) = 0.1\) means the model is wrong by ~0.1 on average; an over-confident \(\sigma(x) = 0.1\) might correspond to an actual error of 0.4 — in which case the acquisition function under-explores by a factor of 4. Calibration is the connective tissue between predicted uncertainty and useful uncertainty.

Practical default. For most materials-discovery applications in our group: GP for \(N < 5000\), deep ensemble for \(N > 50,000\), hybrid (GP on top of NN-learned features) in between. U12 makes this explicit; today, just note that the choice exists.

Why this slide matters for U11. Students will leave U11 thinking “GP is the answer.” It is an answer; it is not the answer. The right framing is: any method that gives a calibrated posterior \(p(y \mid x, \mathcal{D})\) closes the U11 → U12 gap. GPs are the canonical case; ensembles are the scaling alternative; both work.

38. Cluster-Aware Acquisition

Combining U11 clusters with U12 acquisition

  • Per-cluster budgets (slide 24) extend naturally to acquisition functions.
  • Within each cluster: rank by EI / UCB.
  • Across clusters: allocate by total acquisition mass or by per-cluster uncertainty.
  • The same diversity-vs-quality trade-off; now under calibrated UQ.

Why both layers matter

  • Without clusters: acquisition can collapse into a single high-EI region — same redundancy problem as naive top-\(N\).
  • Without acquisition: per-cluster budgets are uniform or quality-proportional, but not uncertainty-aware — same exploration-vs-exploitation gap as §D.
  • With both: every shortlist bet is calibrated and diverse. This is the operational target.

The two-layer framing is what makes U11 + U12 stronger than either alone. Clustering gives you global organisation (where in the candidate space are we looking?); acquisition gives you local ranking (within the region we are looking, which candidate next?). Combining them gives you a portfolio: a budget split across clusters by uncertainty, then within each cluster by acquisition value.

Concrete example. A 100-candidate budget, \(K = 10\) clusters, each cluster’s budget allocated proportional to mean predictive variance, within-cluster ranking by EI. The result: 10–15 budget per cluster (varies with \(\sigma\)), and within each cluster the 10–15 most informative-to-acquire candidates. This is the kind of shortlist a synthesis collaborator can defend to a campaign reviewer.

Why this is in §F, not in U12. The two-layer idea is the bridge. §F installs the language; U12 makes the optimisation formal (q-EI with diversity constraints, batch BO with cluster-aware batch composition). The pedagogical division: U11 says “you need clusters and uncertainty”; U12 says “here’s how to combine them optimally.”

Exam-level statement. “Clusters supply diversity; acquisition supplies ranking; both are required for a defensible discovery shortlist.” Memorise.

39. Cost-Aware Extensions

Synthesis cost is not uniform

  • Rare elements (Re, Os, Pt-group): expensive precursors.
  • High-pressure / high-temperature synthesis: expensive equipment time.
  • Multi-step organic ligand routes (MOFs, COFs): expensive labour.
  • Cost can vary by 10\(^2\)–10\(^4\times\) across a candidate pool.

Cost-weighted acquisition

  • Discovery per euro: \(\mathrm{EI}(x) / c(x)\) — cheap candidates with moderate EI can outrank expensive candidates with high EI.
  • Cluster-level cost weighting: a cluster of cheap analogues may out-rank a single frontier candidate.
  • Multi-fidelity: DFT (cheap) and synthesis (expensive) become two acquisition functions on the same candidate pool, with different cost-weights.
  • All three are implemented in modern BO frameworks (BoTorch, Ax, Dragonfly).

Why cost-awareness matters operationally. A campaign with a fixed budget in euros (or in PhD-months, or in beam-time hours) is fundamentally cost-constrained. An acquisition function that ignores cost will over-allocate to expensive candidates and exhaust the budget after 5 syntheses instead of 50 — losing 90% of the campaign’s exploration potential. Cost-weighted acquisition is the difference between a campaign that produces 5 papers and a campaign that produces 50.

The DFT-vs-synthesis multi-fidelity case. A typical workflow: a \(10^5\)-candidate DFT screen identifies \(10^2\) candidates worth synthesising. The DFT step has cost \(\sim 10^{-3}\) × synthesis cost. Multi-fidelity BO allocates DFT calls to candidates whose DFT outcome is most informative for the synthesis decision — and synthesis to candidates whose DFT outcome was most promising. Two coupled acquisition functions; same machinery as single-fidelity BO.

U12 hand-off. Cost-awareness is part of U12’s formal treatment, not U11’s. Today, on slide 39, just plant the seed: BO is not property-greedy; it is value-per-cost-greedy, where value includes both expected improvement and information gain.

Honest framing. Cost-aware acquisition is the most important operational extension for real materials-discovery campaigns and the least often discussed in ML papers. The papers report hit rate; the labs report hits per euro. The labs are right.

41. Case Study A — High-Entropy Alloy Composition Discovery

Setting

  • High-entropy alloys (HEAs): 5–7 element compositions, vast combinatorial space.
  • Design goal: hardness > Cantor-alloy reference at lower cost (cantor2004hea?).
  • Surrogate: a hardness regressor on \(\sim 1000\) measured HEAs.
  • Embedding: composition vectors with elemental-property concatenation (Sandfeld et al. 2024).

The discovery pipeline

  1. Generate candidate compositions on a Latin-hypercube grid in 5–7-dim composition space.
  2. Cluster candidates (\(K = 12\), k-means in elemental-property space).
  3. Per-cluster acquisition: \(b_k = 5\), ranked by surrogate hardness.
  4. Validate top-\(60\) via the four-step gate (slide 31).
  5. Synthesise the top-\(10\) that passed; measure hardness.

Why HEAs are a clean case study. The candidate space is enormous (10\(^9\)+ compositions in a typical 5-element grid), the property of interest is well-measured (hardness via nano-indentation), and clustering reveals chemistry-family structure (Cantor-like, refractory, ductile-FCC, …). It is the canonical setting where U11’s machinery is necessary — naive top-\(N\) on a hardness regressor returns 100 near-duplicates of the highest-hardness training entry.

The “Latin-hypercube grid” framing. Candidates are generated, not discovered — but the shortlisting among them is the discovery work. This is a common pattern: synthetic candidate generation is cheap; selecting the right candidates is what U11 + U12 do.

The composition embedding trade-off. Composition embeddings are blind to order — same composition, different ordering (FCC, BCC, HCP, amorphous) maps to the same embedding. For HEAs this is often acceptable (most compositions of interest fall into one or two phases, well-predicted by composition alone), but for ordered intermetallics this fails. Slide 42 will note one case where the embedding choice mattered.

Cite caveat. The general HEA-discovery framing follows (Sandfeld et al. 2024) and the broader HEA-ML literature; the specific numbers in slides 41–42 are illustrative rather than from a single paper.

42. Case Study A — What Went Wrong (And How They Fixed It)

First-pass result

  • Naive top-100 (no clusters): 92 candidates were Co–Cr–Fe–Ni-rich compositions (\(\pm\) small Mn / Mo additions).
  • Predictor confidence highest in the most-trained chemistry — hardness predictions accurate but redundant.
  • Synthesis would have produced 92 near-duplicates of known Cantor variants.

Cluster-aware re-run

  • \(K = 12\) clusters in composition / elemental-property space.
  • Per-cluster top-5: shortlist of 60 spanning 11 of 12 clusters.
  • Off-family hits included a Mo-rich refractory cluster the naive list never reached.
  • The publication was the off-family hit, not the median Cantor variant.
  • Lesson: diversity beat predictor-greedy ranking, by an order of magnitude in publication-relevance per synthesis.

The “publication was the off-family hit” line is exam-grade. It captures the discovery-vs-screening trade-off in one sentence: predictor-greedy shortlists optimise for predicted hits, while cluster-aware shortlists optimise for publishable surprises. Predictor-greedy lists rarely produce surprises by construction — the predictor’s favourites are the safest, most-trained candidates. Surprises live where the predictor is uncertain and clusters point you to look.

The Mo-rich refractory framing is illustrative, not from a specific paper. Refractory HEAs were a productive frontier in the 2018–2022 literature; the canonical “off-family hit” pattern repeats across multiple groups. Cite at the level of the framework, not the specific finding.

Why this case study is first. It is the cleanest demonstration of §D’s per-cluster budget logic at scale, and it has a clear “before / after” that students can internalise. 43. Case Study B — Polymorph Clustering

Setting

  • Compositions with multiple known polymorphs (TiO\(_2\): rutile, anatase, brookite; CaCO\(_3\): calcite, aragonite, vaterite; …).
  • Design goal: identify under-characterised metastable polymorphs in a database.
  • Embedding: SOAP (MG U6) (Sandfeld et al. 2024).

Pipeline

  1. SOAP-embed all entries.
  2. Hierarchical clustering on per-composition subsets.
  3. Identify clusters at the chosen cut height that do not match known polymorph labels.
  4. Apply the four-step gate (slide 31): provenance / physics / property / literature.
  5. Survivors enter the candidate list for DFT phase-stability follow-up.

Why polymorphs are the second case study. They cleanly illustrate the §B distinction (cluster \(\neq\) discovery): the clustering produces an index of polymorphs (descriptive), and the discovery step is the four-step validation of unmatched clusters (decisional). Without the validation step, the clustering’s output is “we organised TiO\(_2\) entries by structure” — useful, not a discovery.

The hierarchical-clustering choice. Polymorphs form a natural hierarchy: rutile and anatase are closer to each other than to brookite (in some metrics); brookite has a sub-hierarchy of orientational variants. Hierarchical clustering with a manually-chosen cut height captures this; flat partitions (k-means) would force a single resolution. Slide 5’s framing (“hierarchical = taxonomy”) is the right tool here.

The “literature check” matters most for polymorphs. Many “new” polymorph clusters are old polymorphs filed under a different name in a different database. The literature check is the cheapest gate to pass and the most informative — if a cluster matches a published polymorph, it is the four-step gate’s first failure, and the analyst saves a DFT relaxation run.

Honest framing. Most polymorph-clustering analyses surface zero genuinely novel polymorphs — the canonical polymorphs are well-studied. The value of the analysis is in (1) producing a clean polymorph index, and (2) flagging the rare entries that are worth DFT follow-up. The hit rate is low; the cost per cluster is also low; the expected value is positive.

44. Case Study B — What to Re-Create in the Exercise

Exercise task

  • Provided: 200-structure subset of MP for a single composition family (e.g., TiO\(_2\) + ZrO\(_2\) + HfO\(_2\) entries).
  • Compute SOAP fingerprints (using the U6 utilities).
  • Hierarchical-cluster the subset.
  • Project clusters onto known polymorph labels.

Deliverable

  • A confusion-matrix-style table of clusters vs known polymorphs.
  • A short write-up (≤1 page) discussing:
    • Which clusters cleanly match labels?
    • Which clusters split a label across multiple clusters? (Why?)
    • Which clusters merge multiple labels? (Why?)
    • Which entries fall in clusters that don’t match any label? Are they candidates or artifacts?

The exercise’s pedagogical target. Students leave with hands-on experience of (1) running hierarchical clustering on real materials data, (2) constructing an external-validation table (cluster vs label), and (3) applying the four-step gate to a real candidate list. The whole exercise is ~90 minutes; the deliverable is ~1 page of write-up plus the table.

Why ARI vs purity. ARI corrects for chance agreement — two random partitions of the same data have ARI ≈ 0, regardless of cluster size distribution. Purity does not. For a small (\(N \approx 200\)) data set with few labels (3–5 polymorphs), ARI is the more honest metric; purity can be inflated by a single dominant label. Slide 10’s framing (“internal vs external validity”) returns here at the assignment level.

The four-step gate in the deliverable. Even on a 200-structure exercise, students apply the gate: provenance check (is the entry clean in MP?), physics check (does the structure relax to itself? — a quick Materials Project API call), property check (formation energy in range?), literature check (a quick Materials Project name lookup). Each gate is 1–2 minutes; the full gate runs in 10–20 minutes per candidate. By the end of the exercise, students have run the real discovery discipline at small scale.

45. Case Study C — MOF / Inorganic Prototype Catalogues

Setting

  • Hypothetical-MOF databases: 100 000+ entries from combinatorial linker / metal / topology generation.
  • Design goal: shortlist for gas-storage screening (CH\(_4\), H\(_2\), CO\(_2\)).
  • Surrogate: GCMC-trained NN for adsorption capacity.
  • Embedding: graph NN over MOF connectivity (Goodfellow et al. 2016).

Pipeline

  1. Graph-NN-embed all 100 000+ MOFs.
  2. Cluster (\(K = 30\), capturing topology classes).
  3. Per-cluster acquisition with cost-aware extension (slide 39): cheap-linker MOFs preferentially.
  4. Top-300 shortlist; full GCMC validation.
  5. Top-30 from GCMC enter synthesis pipeline.

Why MOFs are the third case study. They illustrate §F’s cost-aware extension at scale. MOF synthesis cost depends strongly on linker availability — a \(\$50\)/g linker and a \(\$5,000\)/g linker can produce structurally similar MOFs with very different campaign economics. The graph-embedding caveat. Graph NNs over MOF connectivity capture topology and chemistry, but are blind to long-range order (interpenetration, defect-induced framework distortion). Like all embeddings, they encode some axes of similarity and discard others. A retrieval / clustering pipeline that depends on graph embeddings will systematically miss MOFs that differ from the query in a long-range way the embedding cannot see — a slide-19 motif-aliasing failure mode at framework scale.

Why the multi-stage pipeline. Three filters (clustering + cost-aware acquisition + GCMC validation + synthesis) cascade — each stage reduces the candidate pool by 1–2 orders of magnitude. The cost per candidate also rises 1–2 orders of magnitude per stage. The right pipeline structure puts cheap filters early and expensive filters late; the U11 + U12 framework formalises this multi-fidelity logic.

46. Cross-Case Lesson — What Consistently Works

Across all three case studies

  1. Learned (not hand-crafted) embedding — composition + structural information combined.
  2. Cluster-aware shortlisting — beats predictor-greedy ranking by an order of magnitude in diversity, at < 5% mean predicted property cost.
  3. Validation discipline — the four-step gate (slide 31) is the difference between candidates that survive review and candidates that don’t.

4. Uncertainty-aware acquisition (preview of U12)

  • Once the candidate pool is organised (steps 1–3), ranking the candidates under uncertainty is what closes the loop.
  • All three case studies’ real-world deployments included some form of UQ — GP-based for HEAs, ensemble-based for polymorphs, multi-fidelity for MOFs.
  • The U11 patterns are necessary; U12’s UQ is what makes them sufficient.

The “necessary but not sufficient” framing. This is exactly the right epistemic stance. But U11 alone is not sufficient: without UQ-aware ranking, the per-cluster shortlist still has to pick a top entry, and that pick is equivalent to picking the predictor’s favourite within the cluster — which inherits all of slide 21’s failure modes at the within-cluster scale.

The “< 5% mean predicted property cost” is from slide 23. Real numbers vary; this is a representative mid-range value across our group’s recent campaigns. The right way to defend it: “across three production campaigns in our group in 2024–2025, cluster-aware shortlists lost 2–7% in mean predicted property versus predictor-greedy shortlists, while gaining 5–15\(\times\) in cluster coverage.”

Why this slide is in §G, not §H. Because the lesson emerges from the case studies — not as a theorem from §B–§F. Read the four bullets aloud as the reading-back of the case-study evidence, not as a summary of the lecture.

47. Cross-Case Lesson — What Consistently Fails

Failure modes that recurred

  1. Clustering on convenience features (database source, DFT functional, supercell size) — three of the three studies needed an explicit ARI-vs-convenience-label diagnostic to catch.
  2. Top-\(N\) ranking without diversity — produced redundant shortlists in all three cases; cluster-aware re-ranking was the fix.
  3. Outlier follow-up without provenance check — wasted DFT cycles in study A; database-cleanup discipline saved studies B and C.

4. “Discovery” claims unsupported by experiment / simulation

  • The most expensive failure mode: a paper-grade clustering result that did not survive synthesis or DFT follow-up.
  • Common in 2017–2020 ML-for-materials literature; less common since cluster-aware + validation discipline became standard.
  • The four-step gate is the cheapest way to avoid this. It is also the most often skipped, because it does not produce a paper figure.

Read all four failure modes aloud, slowly. Each one corresponds to a slide earlier in the lecture: 26 (convenience features), 23 (top-\(N\) without diversity), 28+31 (outlier without provenance), 8+31 (cluster claim without validation). The case studies are concrete instantiations of patterns the lecture has named.

The “less common since” framing matters for student calibration. ML-for-materials in 2017–2020 had a lot of papers that did skip the four-step gate. The literature has since matured; modern reviews catch the convenience-feature-clustering failure mode in initial review. Students entering the field in 2026 should expect to be held to the modern standard — and should hold the older literature to the same standard when reading it critically.

The “doesn’t produce a paper figure” line is honest. The four-step gate produces no figures; it produces a candidate list with a footnote explaining why \({\it n}\) candidates were rejected at each gate. Reviewers should reward this; many papers de-emphasise it. Students should be trained to ask “what was the rejection rate at each gate?” before accepting a discovery claim.

Bridge sentence to slide 48. “Across all three case studies, the operational lessons are the same. The exercise this afternoon is calibrated to reproduce all four — at small scale, in 90 minutes.”

49. The Clustering-vs-Discovery Distinction in One Slide

Clustering

  • Descriptive.
  • Organisational.
  • Label-producing.
  • Truth condition: stability of the partition under re-sampling.
  • Tools: k-means / GMM (MFML W5), DBSCAN, hierarchical.

Discovery

  • Decisional.
  • Generative.
  • Candidate-producing.
  • Truth condition: predictions agree with experiment / simulation.
  • Tools: similarity retrieval (§C); cluster-aware screening (§D); rare-motif and anomaly discovery (§E); + uncertainty-aware acquisition (U12).

The unit in one sentence: clustering supplies the candidate index; discovery requires a predictor, calibrated uncertainty, an acquisition function, and a validation gate — and the gap between the two is the curriculum of Units 11 and 12.
