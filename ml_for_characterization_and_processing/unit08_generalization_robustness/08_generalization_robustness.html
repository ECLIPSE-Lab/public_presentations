01. Learning Outcomes

After this unit, you will be able to:

  1. Explain the bias-variance tradeoff and its implications for model selection
  1. Apply cross-validation strategies appropriate for materials science data
  1. Compare regularization techniques (L1, L2, Dropout, Early Stopping)
  1. Define process windows using ML predictions and sensitivity analysis
  1. Design a robust model selection workflow for engineering applications

Welcome to Unit 8. This unit is about moving from “my model works on the training set” to “my model is reliable in production.” We cover the foundational theory of generalization, the practical tools of cross-validation and regularization, and the engineering concept of process windows — tying ML model quality to real-world manufacturing robustness. This is arguably the most important unit for anyone who wants to deploy ML in an industrial setting.

Part 1: Why Models Fail

Generalization, Bias, and Variance

In Part 1 we establish the theoretical foundation: why does a model that performs perfectly on training data fail on new data? This is the generalization problem, and understanding it is essential before we can fix it.

02. The Generalization Challenge

From the Lab Bench to the Production Floor

  • Training error: How well the model fits the data it has seen
  • Generalization error: How well the model performs on unseen data
  • These are not the same thing — and confusing them is the #1 mistake in applied ML

Materials Example:

A model trained on 50 tensile test specimens from a single batch predicts yield strength perfectly — but fails on specimens from a different powder lot, a different machine, or a different humidity level.

The Generalization Gap

The difference between training error and test error is the generalization gap. Our entire goal in model selection is to minimize this gap while keeping both errors low.

This is the central problem of machine learning. In materials science it is particularly acute because data is expensive to collect, datasets are small, and the operating conditions in production can differ substantially from laboratory conditions. A model that memorizes 50 training samples is useless if it cannot generalize to the 51st. We need to understand why this happens and what to do about it. The generalization gap is influenced by model complexity, dataset size, and the similarity between training and deployment distributions.

03. Underfitting vs Overfitting

Underfitting

  • Model is too simple
  • High bias
  • Misses the underlying pattern
  • Example: Fitting a line to a nonlinear stress-strain curve

Good Fit

  • Model captures the true relationship
  • Balanced bias and variance
  • Generalizes well to new data

Overfitting

  • Model is too complex
  • High variance
  • Memorizes noise
  • Example: Degree-20 polynomial on 15 data points

This visual comparison is one of the most important concepts in all of machine learning. The underfitting model (e.g., a straight line) cannot capture the curvature in the data — it has high bias. The overfitting model (e.g., a degree-20 polynomial) passes through every data point including measurement noise — it has high variance, meaning that a slightly different training set would produce a wildly different model. The “just right” model captures the true underlying relationship. In materials science, the true relationship is governed by physics, and our goal is to discover that physics — not the noise in our measurement equipment.

04. The Bias-Variance Tradeoff

The Fundamental Decomposition

For a model \(\hat{f}(x)\) trained on data \(\mathcal{D}\), the expected prediction error at a point \(x\) is:

\[\text{EPE}(x) = \underbrace{\text{Bias}^2[\hat{f}(x)]}_{\text{systematic error}} + \underbrace{\text{Var}[\hat{f}(x)]}_{\text{sensitivity to training data}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2_\varepsilon}_{\text{irreducible noise}}\]

  • As complexity increases: bias decreases, variance increases
  • The optimal model sits at the minimum of total error
  • The irreducible noise \(\sigma^2_\varepsilon\) sets a floor — no model can beat it

This is the bias-variance tradeoff, one of the most important theoretical results in statistical learning. Bias is the systematic error — the error you would get even if you had infinite training data. It comes from the model being too restrictive. Variance is the sensitivity of your model to the particular training set you happened to collect. It comes from the model being too flexible. The irreducible noise sigma-squared-epsilon represents the inherent randomness in the process — measurement noise, uncontrolled variables, stochastic processes. No model, no matter how good, can eliminate this term. The optimal model complexity is at the bottom of the U-shaped total error curve.

05. Bias-Variance Decomposition: Mathematical Detail

Deriving the Decomposition

Let \(y = f(x) + \varepsilon\) where \(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon] = 0\) and \(\text{Var}[\varepsilon] = \sigma^2_\varepsilon\).

\[\mathbb{E}_\mathcal{D}\left[(y - \hat{f}(x))^2\right] = \mathbb{E}_\mathcal{D}\left[(f(x) + \varepsilon - \hat{f}(x))^2\right]\]

Expanding and using \(\bar{f}(x) = \mathbb{E}_\mathcal{D}[\hat{f}(x)]\):

\[= \underbrace{(f(x) - \bar{f}(x))^2}_{\text{Bias}^2} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_\mathcal{D}\left[(\hat{f}(x) - \bar{f}(x))^2\right]}_{\text{Variance}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2_\varepsilon}_{\text{Noise}}\]

Term Meaning Reduced by
\(\text{Bias}^2\) Model assumptions miss the truth More flexible model
\(\text{Variance}\) Model changes with different training data More data, regularization
\(\sigma^2_\varepsilon\) Inherent measurement noise Better instruments (not ML!)

Let me walk you through the math. We start with the true data-generating process: y equals f of x plus noise epsilon. The expected squared error of our prediction f-hat, taken over all possible training sets D, can be decomposed into three terms. The bias squared term is the squared difference between the true function and the average prediction of our model over all possible training sets. The variance term measures how much f-hat fluctuates around its mean. The noise term is irreducible. The key insight is that bias is reduced by making the model more flexible, but this increases variance. More data reduces variance without affecting bias. Regularization reduces variance at the cost of slightly increasing bias — and this is usually a good trade. Better instruments reduce noise, which is outside the scope of ML.

06. Polynomial Regression Example

Complexity vs Generalization (McClarren 2021)

Setup: \(n = 20\) noisy samples from \(f(x) = \sin(2\pi x)\)

Polynomial Degree Training MSE Test MSE Diagnosis
1 0.42 0.45 Underfitting (high bias)
3 0.08 0.09 Good fit
9 0.001 0.35 Overfitting (high variance)
15 \(\approx 0\) 12.7 Severe overfitting

  • Degree 3 has the best test error — not degree 15!
  • Training error always decreases with complexity — but test error does not

This is a classic demonstration from McClarren Chapter 2. We generate 20 noisy points from a sine curve and fit polynomials of increasing degree. The degree-1 fit is a straight line — clearly too simple. The degree-3 fit captures the sinusoidal shape nicely. The degree-9 fit starts oscillating wildly between data points, and degree-15 is even worse. Notice the training MSE drops to nearly zero for high-degree polynomials — the model passes through every point. But the test MSE explodes. This is the hallmark of overfitting: training error goes down but test error goes up. The model is memorizing noise rather than learning the underlying sine function. This principle applies directly to materials ML — if you have 50 data points and train a neural network with 10,000 parameters, you will almost certainly overfit.

07. Think About This…

A Materials Scientist’s Dilemma

You are building a model to predict melt pool depth in laser powder bed fusion from 5 process parameters (laser power, scan speed, hatch spacing, layer thickness, preheat temperature).

You have 40 experimental measurements.

A colleague suggests using a neural network with 3 hidden layers of 128 neurons each.

Question 1: How many parameters does this network have?

→ Approximately \(5 \times 128 + 128 \times 128 + 128 \times 128 + 128 \times 1 \approx 33{,}000\) parameters

Question 2: What will happen when you train it on 40 data points?

→ The model will perfectly memorize all 40 points (training error ≈ 0) but fail catastrophically on new data. You have 33,000 knobs to fit 40 numbers — the model has 825× more capacity than data!

What should you do instead?

→ Start with a simpler model (linear regression, low-degree polynomial, small network with regularization). Use cross-validation to verify generalization.

This is a very common mistake in applied ML for materials science. Researchers see impressive results from deep learning in computer vision where millions of images are available, and they assume the same architectures will work for tabular process data with 40 to 100 samples. The rule of thumb is that you need at least 5 to 10 samples per model parameter to avoid overfitting in the absence of strong regularization. With 40 data points, you should start with models that have fewer than 10 free parameters — linear regression, polynomial regression of low degree, or a very small neural network with strong regularization. Give students a moment to think before revealing the answers.

08. Summary: Part 1

Why Models Fail — Key Takeaways

  • Generalization — performance on unseen data — is the only metric that matters for deployment
  • Bias (underfitting) and Variance (overfitting) are in tension: reducing one increases the other
  • Total Error = Bias² + Variance + Irreducible Noise
  • More complex ≠ better — the optimal model balances complexity against available data
  • In materials science with small datasets, overfitting is the dominant failure mode

Let me summarize Part 1. The generalization problem is at the heart of applied machine learning. In materials science, where data is expensive and datasets are small, overfitting is by far the more common failure mode. Researchers often use models that are far too complex for the amount of data they have. The bias-variance decomposition gives us a theoretical framework for understanding this, and in the next parts we will learn practical tools to address it: cross-validation for diagnosis, and regularization for treatment.

Part 2: Robust Validation Strategies

Cross-Validation and Data Splitting

Now that we understand why models fail, we need tools to detect failure. The fundamental tool is cross-validation — a family of techniques for estimating generalization error using only the data we have. This is especially critical in materials science where collecting additional test data may require weeks of experiments.

09. The Problem with Holdout Validation

A Single Split Is Not Enough

Simple holdout: Split data into 80% training / 20% test

  • Train on 80%, evaluate on 20%
  • Report the test error as “model performance”

Problems for materials data:

  1. With \(n = 50\) samples, the test set has only 10 samples — high variance in the estimate
  2. Results depend on which 10 samples end up in the test set
  3. We “waste” 20% of our precious data — we cannot train on it

Demonstration: Random splits of a 40-sample dataset:

Split seed Test MSE
42 0.12
17 0.31
99 0.08
7 0.24

Which is the “true” performance? None of them are reliable individually.

The simplest validation strategy is the holdout method: split your data into a training set and a test set. This is fine when you have millions of samples, but in materials science you often have 30 to 100 samples. With 50 samples and an 80/20 split, your test set has only 10 points. The estimated error has very high variance — repeat the experiment with a different random split and you get a very different number. This is demonstrated in the table: four different random splits give test MSEs ranging from 0.08 to 0.31. We need a more stable method, and that method is cross-validation.

10. K-Fold Cross-Validation

The Standard Approach (Sandfeld et al. 2024)

Algorithm:

  1. Randomly partition data into \(k\) equally-sized folds
  2. For \(i = 1, \ldots, k\):
    • Hold out fold \(i\) as the validation set
    • Train on the remaining \(k-1\) folds
    • Record validation error \(e_i\)
  3. Report: \(\text{CV}_k = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^k e_i \pm \text{SE}\)

Benefits:

  • Every sample is used for both training and validation
  • The average over \(k\) folds is much more stable than a single holdout
  • Standard error gives a confidence interval on performance

Typical choice: \(k = 5\) or \(k = 10\)

K-fold cross-validation solves the instability of the holdout method. We split the data into k equal parts called folds. We train k separate models, each time holding out a different fold for validation. Then we average the k validation errors. This gives a much more stable estimate of generalization performance because every data point gets to be in the validation set exactly once. The standard choice is k equals 5 or k equals 10. Five-fold CV uses 80% of data for training in each round, while 10-fold uses 90%. With small datasets, 10-fold or even leave-one-out CV is preferred to maximize training set size.

12. Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation

When Every Sample Counts

LOOCV is K-Fold CV where \(k = n\) (number of samples):

  • Each “fold” contains exactly one sample
  • Train on \(n-1\) samples, test on 1
  • Repeat \(n\) times

Advantages:

  • Maximum possible training set size (\(n-1\))
  • Deterministic — no randomness in the split
  • Nearly unbiased estimate of generalization error

Disadvantages:

  • Computationally expensive (\(n\) models to train)
  • High variance in the estimate (single-sample folds)
  • Folds are highly correlated (they share \(n-2\) samples)
When to use LOOCV

LOOCV is most appropriate when \(n < 30\) and training a single model is fast (e.g., linear regression, small random forests). For neural networks on small datasets, use 5- or 10-fold CV instead.

Leave-one-out cross-validation is the extreme case where k equals n. You train n separate models, each time leaving out a single sample for testing. This maximizes the training set size, which is critical for small datasets. It is also deterministic — unlike k-fold where the results depend on the random fold assignment. However, LOOCV has high variance because each test set contains only one point, and the n training sets are almost identical to each other, making the error estimates highly correlated. For materials science with very small datasets, say under 30 samples, LOOCV is often the best choice as long as the model trains quickly. For neural networks, use 5- or 10-fold CV.

13. Stratified Cross-Validation

Handling Imbalanced Materials Data

Problem: In classification tasks, some classes may be rare.

  • Example: 95% “Good” parts, 5% “Defective” parts
  • A random fold might contain zero defective samples → unreliable evaluation

Stratified K-Fold CV:

  • Each fold preserves the class distribution of the full dataset
  • If 5% of samples are defective, each fold also has ~5% defective samples
from sklearn.model_selection import StratifiedKFold

skf = StratifiedKFold(n_splits=5, shuffle=True, random_state=42)
for train_idx, val_idx in skf.split(X, y_class):
    # Each fold has the same proportion of each class
    X_train, X_val = X[train_idx], X[val_idx]
    y_train, y_val = y_class[train_idx], y_class[val_idx]

Materials examples requiring stratification:

  • Defect detection (rare defect classes)
  • Phase classification (minority phases)
  • Pass/fail quality classification (high yield processes)

Stratified cross-validation is essential when you have imbalanced class distributions. In materials science, this is extremely common. Quality control datasets might have 95% good parts and 5% defective parts. If you randomly split into 5 folds, some folds might end up with zero defective samples, making the evaluation on that fold meaningless. Stratified K-Fold ensures each fold has the same proportion of each class as the full dataset. In scikit-learn, you just replace KFold with StratifiedKFold and pass the class labels to the split method. Always use stratified CV for classification problems with imbalanced classes.

14. Grouped Cross-Validation

Preventing Spatial and Batch Leakage

Problem: In materials data, samples are often correlated within groups:

  • Multiple measurements from the same specimen
  • Images from different regions of the same wafer
  • Tests on parts from the same powder batch

Data leakage: If correlated samples appear in both train and test sets, the model “sees” the answer.

from sklearn.model_selection import GroupKFold

# group_labels: e.g., specimen ID or batch number
gkf = GroupKFold(n_splits=5)
for train_idx, val_idx in gkf.split(X, y, groups=group_labels):
    # All samples from a given specimen stay together
    X_train, X_val = X[train_idx], X[val_idx]
Critical for Materials Science!

Ignoring group structure can give overly optimistic CV scores. Your model appears to generalize — but it is actually just recognizing which specimen a sample came from, not learning the underlying physics.

Grouped cross-validation is absolutely critical for materials science and is one of the most commonly violated principles. If you take 10 measurements from each of 5 specimens, you have 50 data points. But these are not 50 independent samples — the measurements within each specimen are highly correlated. If sample A appears in both your training and test set (different measurements from the same specimen), the model can effectively memorize specimen-level features rather than learning general relationships. GroupKFold ensures that all measurements from a given specimen, batch, or experiment stay in the same fold. This gives you an honest estimate of how well the model will perform on a truly new specimen. Always ask yourself: what is the unit of independence in my data? Group your CV folds accordingly.

15. Nested Cross-Validation

Unbiased Model Selection + Performance Estimation

The problem with standard CV for hyperparameter tuning:

If you use CV to select hyperparameters, the CV score is optimistically biased — you have selected the hyperparameters that happen to score best on these particular folds.

  • Inner loop: tunes hyperparameters via CV on training data
  • Outer loop: estimates generalization error on truly unseen data
  • Computationally expensive but gives an unbiased performance estimate

Nested cross-validation separates two concerns that are often incorrectly conflated. The inner loop performs hyperparameter tuning using cross-validation on the training portion of the data. The outer loop estimates the generalization performance of the entire model selection procedure on data that was never used for any purpose. Without nested CV, you run the risk of reporting an optimistically biased performance estimate because you selected the hyperparameters that happened to work best on your CV folds. With nested CV, the outer test folds are never touched during model selection. This is the gold standard for model evaluation in the small-data regime typical of materials science. It is computationally expensive — if you have 5 outer folds and 5 inner folds, you train 25 models for each hyperparameter configuration — but it gives you an honest answer.

16. Think About This…

Choosing the Right Validation Strategy

Scenario: You have collected hardness measurements from 8 different steel specimens. For each specimen, you measured hardness at 20 different locations, giving you 160 total measurements. You want to predict hardness from composition and processing parameters.

A colleague uses standard 10-fold CV and reports \(R^2 = 0.95\).

Is this result trustworthy?

Answer: Almost certainly no. Standard K-Fold will randomly distribute the 20 measurements from each specimen across folds. The model can learn specimen-specific patterns (spatial correlations, local microstructure) rather than the composition→hardness relationship.

What should be done instead?

→ Use GroupKFold with groups = specimen_id, giving 8 groups. This tests whether the model can predict hardness for an entirely new specimen it has never seen — which is the real question.

The \(R^2\) will likely drop significantly — but it will be honest.

This is a very realistic scenario. Give students a moment to think about it before revealing the answer. The key insight is that 160 measurements from 8 specimens does not give you 160 independent samples — you have 8 independent samples, each measured 20 times. If you use standard K-Fold, measurements from the same specimen will appear in both training and validation sets. The model can exploit spatial autocorrelation within each specimen to achieve high apparent accuracy without actually learning the composition-to-hardness relationship. GroupKFold with specimen_id as the group variable forces the model to generalize to entirely new specimens. The R-squared will almost certainly be much lower, but it honestly reflects the model’s ability to predict hardness for a new specimen based on its composition and processing.

17. Summary: Part 2

Robust Validation — Key Takeaways

  • Holdout validation is unstable for small datasets — use K-Fold CV
  • LOOCV maximizes training data when \(n < 30\) and models train quickly
  • Stratified CV preserves class balance for imbalanced classification problems
  • Grouped CV prevents data leakage from correlated samples — essential in materials science
  • Nested CV gives unbiased performance estimates when tuning hyperparameters
Scenario Recommended CV
Small balanced dataset (\(n < 30\)) LOOCV
Medium dataset, no groups 5- or 10-Fold
Imbalanced classes Stratified K-Fold
Grouped/correlated data GroupKFold
Hyperparameter tuning + evaluation Nested CV

This table is your reference guide for choosing a cross-validation strategy. The most common mistake in materials science is ignoring group structure. Always ask: what is my unit of independence? If you measured the same specimen multiple times, or tested parts from the same batch, those measurements are not independent. Use GroupKFold. For classification with imbalanced classes, use Stratified K-Fold. For hyperparameter tuning, use nested CV if you can afford the computational cost, or at minimum separate your data into a development set (for CV-based tuning) and a final test set (never touched during tuning).

Part 3: Regularization Techniques

Taming Model Complexity

Part 3 addresses the treatment for overfitting: regularization. We will cover a range of techniques from classical L1 and L2 penalties to modern methods like dropout, batch normalization, and data augmentation. The common thread is that all regularization techniques constrain the model’s capacity, preventing it from memorizing noise.

18. Why Regularize?

Constraining the Solution Space

Core idea: Add a penalty for model complexity to the loss function.

\[J_\text{regularized} = \underbrace{\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w})}_{\text{data fit}} + \underbrace{\lambda \, \Omega(\mathbf{w})}_{\text{complexity penalty}}\]

Without regularization (\(\lambda = 0\)):

  • Model is free to use arbitrarily large weights
  • Learns noise, overfits

With regularization (\(\lambda > 0\)):

  • Large weights are penalized
  • Model is forced to find simpler solutions
  • Better generalization at the cost of slightly worse training fit

The hyperparameter \(\lambda\) controls the tradeoff:

  • \(\lambda\) too small → still overfitting
  • \(\lambda\) too large → underfitting (penalty dominates, weights → 0)
  • Select \(\lambda\) via cross-validation

Regularization is the most important practical tool for preventing overfitting. The idea is simple: we add a penalty term to the loss function that discourages complex models. The penalty is controlled by a hyperparameter lambda. When lambda is zero, we have no regularization and the model is free to overfit. When lambda is very large, the penalty dominates and the model underfits because all weights are driven toward zero. The optimal lambda is in between, and we find it using cross-validation. Different choices of the penalty function Omega give different types of regularization with different properties, as we will see in the next slides.

19. L2 Regularization (Ridge Regression)

Shrinking Weights Toward Zero

\[J_\text{Ridge} = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^p w_j^2 = \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|_2^2 + \lambda \|\mathbf{w}\|_2^2\]

Closed-form solution:

\[\hat{\mathbf{w}}_\text{Ridge} = (\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X} + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y}\]

Properties:

  • Shrinks all weights proportionally toward zero
  • Never sets weights exactly to zero — keeps all features
  • Stabilizes ill-conditioned problems (adds \(\lambda\) to diagonal)
  • Also called Tikhonov regularization in engineering/physics

Geometric interpretation:

The L2 constraint defines a circle (sphere in higher dimensions). The regularized solution is where the loss contours first touch this circle.

L2 regularization, also known as Ridge regression or Tikhonov regularization, penalizes the sum of squared weights. This encourages small weights but does not force any weight to be exactly zero. The closed-form solution shows that Ridge regression adds lambda times the identity matrix to X-transpose-X before inverting. This has a beautiful numerical property: even if X-transpose-X is singular or nearly singular (which happens when features are correlated), adding lambda-I makes the matrix well-conditioned and invertible. The geometric picture is that the L2 penalty constrains the weights to lie within a circle (or hypersphere). The optimal solution is where the elliptical contours of the loss function first touch this circle. Note that the circle is smooth, so the intersection point generically will not be on a coordinate axis — meaning no weight is exactly zero.

20. L1 Regularization (Lasso)

Automatic Feature Selection Through Sparsity

\[J_\text{Lasso} = \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|_2^2 + \lambda \|\mathbf{w}\|_1 = \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|_2^2 + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^p |w_j|\]

Properties:

  • Drives many weights to exactly zero
  • Performs automatic feature selection
  • Solution is sparse: only a subset of features are “active”
  • No closed-form solution — requires iterative optimization

Geometric interpretation:

The L1 constraint defines a diamond (cross-polytope). The corners of the diamond lie on coordinate axes — so the loss contours often touch at a corner, setting one or more weights to exactly zero.

Materials application: With 100 candidate process parameters, Lasso can identify the 5-10 that truly matter — interpretable and physically meaningful.

L1 regularization, known as the Lasso, penalizes the sum of absolute values of weights. The crucial difference from L2 is that L1 produces sparse solutions — many weights become exactly zero. This happens because of the geometry: the L1 constraint region is a diamond shape, and its corners lie on the coordinate axes. The elliptical loss contours are likely to first touch the diamond at a corner, which corresponds to one or more weights being exactly zero. This is tremendously useful in materials science where you might have many candidate features but only a few are physically relevant. The Lasso automatically identifies which features matter. However, when features are correlated, Lasso tends to pick one arbitrarily and zero out the others, which can be misleading.

22. Elastic Net: The Best of Both Worlds

\[J_\text{ElasticNet} = \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|_2^2 + \lambda_1 \|\mathbf{w}\|_1 + \lambda_2 \|\mathbf{w}\|_2^2\]

Or equivalently with mixing parameter \(\alpha \in [0, 1]\):

\[J = \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|_2^2 + \lambda \left[\alpha \|\mathbf{w}\|_1 + (1 - \alpha) \|\mathbf{w}\|_2^2\right]\]

  • \(\alpha = 1\): Pure Lasso
  • \(\alpha = 0\): Pure Ridge
  • \(0 < \alpha < 1\): Elastic Net

Why Elastic Net?

  • Handles correlated features better than Lasso (groups of correlated features are selected together)
  • Still produces sparse solutions (unlike pure Ridge)
  • Particularly useful in materials science where process parameters are often correlated (e.g., power and energy density)
from sklearn.linear_model import ElasticNet
model = ElasticNet(alpha=0.1, l1_ratio=0.5)  # l1_ratio = alpha
model.fit(X_train, y_train)
print(f"Nonzero features: {(model.coef_ != 0).sum()} / {len(model.coef_)}")

Elastic Net combines L1 and L2 regularization and addresses the main weakness of Lasso. When features are correlated — which is extremely common in materials science because process parameters are physically related — Lasso tends to arbitrarily pick one feature from a correlated group and zero out the others. Elastic Net selects groups of correlated features together, giving a more stable and interpretable result. The mixing parameter alpha controls the balance between L1 sparsity and L2 stability. In practice, alpha equals 0.5 is a good starting point, and you can tune it via cross-validation. The scikit-learn implementation uses l1_ratio for the mixing parameter.

23. Dropout for Neural Networks

Randomly Disabling Neurons During Training (Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville 2016)

Idea: During each training step, randomly set each neuron’s output to zero with probability \(p\) (typically \(p = 0.2\) to \(0.5\)).

Why it works:

  • Prevents co-adaptation — neurons cannot rely on specific other neurons
  • Each training step trains a different “sub-network”
  • Effectively an ensemble of \(2^n\) sub-networks (where \(n\) = number of neurons)
  • At test time: use all neurons but scale outputs by \((1-p)\)
import torch.nn as nn

model = nn.Sequential(
    nn.Linear(10, 128),
    nn.ReLU(),
    nn.Dropout(p=0.3),    # Drop 30% of neurons
    nn.Linear(128, 64),
    nn.ReLU(),
    nn.Dropout(p=0.3),
    nn.Linear(64, 1)
)

Dropout is the most widely used regularization technique for neural networks. During training, each neuron is randomly set to zero with probability p. This forces the network to learn redundant representations — no single neuron can become a “critical path” because it might be dropped. The result is more robust features that generalize better. Mathematically, dropout can be interpreted as training an exponential ensemble of sub-networks that share weights. At test time, we use all neurons but scale their outputs by 1 minus p to account for the fact that more neurons are active than during training. Typical dropout rates are 0.2 to 0.5. Higher dropout provides more regularization but requires training for longer.

24. Batch Normalization

Stabilizing Training Through Normalization

Batch Normalization normalizes the inputs to each layer using mini-batch statistics:

\[\hat{x}_i = \frac{x_i - \mu_\text{batch}}{\sqrt{\sigma^2_\text{batch} + \epsilon}} \qquad y_i = \gamma \hat{x}_i + \beta\]

where \(\gamma\) (scale) and \(\beta\) (shift) are learned parameters.

Benefits:

  • Stabilizes training — reduces sensitivity to weight initialization
  • Allows higher learning rates → faster convergence
  • Acts as a mild regularizer (batch statistics add noise)
  • Reduces the need for dropout in some architectures

Caution for small batch sizes:

In materials science, batch sizes may be very small (e.g., 4-8 samples). Batch normalization becomes unreliable because the batch statistics are noisy. Consider Layer Normalization or Group Normalization instead.

Batch normalization normalizes the activations at each layer by subtracting the batch mean and dividing by the batch standard deviation. The learnable parameters gamma and beta allow the network to undo the normalization if that is optimal. Batch normalization has a secondary regularization effect because the batch statistics inject noise into the training process — each sample’s normalized value depends on which other samples are in the same mini-batch. This acts as a mild regularizer. However, there is an important caveat for materials science applications: batch normalization requires reasonably large batch sizes to estimate the mean and variance accurately. With very small batches, the estimates are noisy and can destabilize training. In these cases, use layer normalization or group normalization, which compute statistics over features rather than over the batch.

25. Early Stopping

The Simplest and Most Effective Regularizer

Principle:

  • Training loss always decreases as training continues
  • Validation loss decreases then increases (overfitting begins)
  • Stop training at the minimum of validation loss

Implementation:

from sklearn.neural_network import MLPRegressor

# Or in PyTorch with a patience-based callback:
best_val_loss = float('inf')
patience, wait = 10, 0
for epoch in range(1000):
    train_loss = train_one_epoch(model, train_loader)
    val_loss = evaluate(model, val_loader)
    if val_loss < best_val_loss:
        best_val_loss = val_loss
        save_checkpoint(model)  # Save the best model
        wait = 0
    else:
        wait += 1
        if wait >= patience:
            break  # Stop training
  • Patience parameter: how many epochs to wait for improvement before stopping
  • Acts as an implicit constraint on model complexity — limits effective number of parameters

Early stopping is arguably the most important regularization technique in practice. The idea is simple: monitor the validation loss during training. Initially, both training and validation loss decrease. At some point, the model begins to overfit — training loss continues to decrease but validation loss starts to increase. We stop training at the epoch where validation loss was lowest. The patience parameter prevents us from stopping too early due to random fluctuations — we wait for a certain number of epochs without improvement before concluding that the model is overfitting. Early stopping is elegant because it requires no modification to the model architecture or loss function. It can be combined with other regularization techniques. Bishop showed that for linear models, early stopping is mathematically equivalent to L2 regularization with a specific lambda value determined by the number of training steps.

26. Data Augmentation as Regularization

Manufacturing More Training Data

Idea: Apply domain-appropriate transformations to existing data to create new training samples.

For micrograph images:

  • Rotation (90°, 180°, 270° for isotropic materials)
  • Flipping (horizontal, vertical)
  • Random cropping
  • Brightness/contrast variation (simulating imaging conditions)
  • Adding Gaussian noise (simulating detector noise)

For tabular process data:

  • Adding small Gaussian noise: \(\tilde{x}_j = x_j + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_j^2)\)
  • Mixup: \(\tilde{x} = \alpha x_a + (1-\alpha) x_b\), \(\tilde{y} = \alpha y_a + (1-\alpha) y_b\)
  • SMOTE for imbalanced classification (generates synthetic minority samples)
Domain Knowledge Matters

Only apply augmentations that preserve the physical meaning of the data. Rotating a micrograph of a columnar grain structure by 45° changes the physics — don’t do it! Always validate augmentations against domain knowledge.

Data augmentation creates additional training samples by applying transformations that should not change the label. For images, this includes rotations, flips, and noise addition. For tabular data, you can add small Gaussian noise to inputs or use mixup, which creates virtual samples by linearly interpolating between existing ones. The key principle is that augmentations must be physically valid. Rotating a micrograph by 90 degrees is valid for an isotropic material but not for a material with a preferred orientation. Adding noise is valid if the noise level is consistent with your measurement uncertainty. Data augmentation acts as regularization because it increases the effective dataset size and teaches the model to be invariant to the augmented transformations. This reduces variance without increasing bias, as long as the augmentations are physically correct.

27. Ensemble Methods: Bagging and Random Forests

Reducing Variance Through Averaging

Bagging (Bootstrap Aggregating):

  1. Draw \(B\) bootstrap samples from the training data (sample with replacement)
  2. Train a separate model on each bootstrap sample
  3. Average the predictions: \(\hat{f}_\text{bag}(x) = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B \hat{f}_b(x)\)

Why it works (variance reduction):

If each model has variance \(\sigma^2\) and correlation \(\rho\):

\[\text{Var}\left[\hat{f}_\text{bag}\right] = \rho \sigma^2 + \frac{1-\rho}{B}\sigma^2\]

As \(B \to \infty\), variance approaches \(\rho \sigma^2\) — reduced by factor \((1-\rho)\)!

Random Forests = Bagged decision trees with additional randomization:

  • At each split, only a random subset of features is considered
  • This decorrelates the trees (reduces \(\rho\)), further reducing variance
  • Extremely effective for tabular materials data with minimal tuning

Ensemble methods reduce variance by averaging multiple models. Bagging trains each model on a bootstrap sample — a random sample drawn with replacement from the training data. The variance of the average is always less than or equal to the variance of the individual models, with equality only when the models are perfectly correlated. Random Forests go further by randomizing the feature subset at each tree split, which decorrelates the individual trees and amplifies the variance reduction. Random Forests are one of the most reliable out-of-the-box methods for tabular data in materials science. They handle nonlinear relationships, require minimal hyperparameter tuning, provide built-in feature importance estimates, and naturally resist overfitting as the number of trees increases. If you are unsure what model to start with for a tabular materials dataset, Random Forests are an excellent default choice.

Part 4: Process Robustness and Sensitivity

From Model Quality to Process Quality

In Part 4 we shift perspective. So far we have focused on making ML models robust — ensuring they generalize well. Now we use ML models to analyze the robustness of manufacturing processes. This connects ML directly to quality engineering concepts that are critical for industrial deployment.

29. What Is Process Robustness?

Taguchi’s Quality Philosophy Meets ML

Robust process: A process whose output quality is insensitive to uncontrollable variation (noise).

Sources of noise in manufacturing:

  • Raw material variation (powder particle size, composition fluctuations)
  • Environmental factors (temperature, humidity)
  • Equipment drift (laser power decay, nozzle wear)
  • Operator-to-operator variation

Key insight: A process operating at the peak of a narrow performance hill is fragile. A process operating on a plateau of acceptable performance is robust — even if the peak value is slightly lower.

\[\text{Robustness} \propto \frac{1}{\left\|\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f(\mathbf{x})\right\|} \bigg|_{\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}_\text{operating}}\]

Low gradient magnitude = robust operating point.

Process robustness is a concept from quality engineering, originally developed by Genichi Taguchi. The idea is that in a real factory, you cannot perfectly control every process parameter. There will always be variation — powder batches differ, lasers age, humidity changes. A robust process is one that delivers acceptable quality despite this variation. Mathematically, robustness is related to the gradient of the quality function with respect to the process parameters. If the gradient is large, small perturbations in the inputs cause large changes in output — the process is fragile. If the gradient is small, the process is robust. ML models give us the ability to estimate this gradient everywhere in the process parameter space, enabling us to find robust operating points systematically.

30. Sensitivity Analysis: Local Methods

How Much Does Each Input Matter?

Local sensitivity (partial derivatives):

\[S_i = \frac{\partial \hat{f}}{\partial x_i}\bigg|_{\mathbf{x}_0}\]

Measures the rate of change of the prediction with respect to input \(x_i\) at operating point \(\mathbf{x}_0\).

Normalized sensitivity (elasticity):

\[S_i^\text{norm} = \frac{\partial \hat{f}}{\partial x_i} \cdot \frac{x_i}{\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}_0)}\]

“A 1% change in \(x_i\) causes an \(S_i^\text{norm}\)% change in the output.”

One-at-a-time (OAT) sensitivity:

  • Vary one parameter while holding all others fixed
  • Plot output vs each parameter → sensitivity curves
  • Simple but misses interactions between parameters

For neural networks: Use automatic differentiation (backpropagation) to compute \(\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \hat{f}\) efficiently.

Local sensitivity analysis asks: if I change input x_i by a small amount, how much does the output change? This is simply the partial derivative of the model with respect to each input. The normalized version, called elasticity, gives the percentage change in output per percentage change in input, which is more interpretable when inputs have different units. One-at-a-time analysis varies each parameter independently, which is simple but misses interaction effects. For neural networks, automatic differentiation computes all partial derivatives efficiently using backpropagation. This is computationally cheap because it reuses the same computational graph used for training.

31. Global Sensitivity: Sobol Indices

Decomposing Variance Across the Entire Parameter Space

Limitation of local methods: They only tell you about sensitivity at one operating point. The sensitivity may change dramatically across the parameter space.

Sobol indices decompose the total output variance into contributions from each input (and their interactions):

\[\text{Var}[Y] = \sum_i V_i + \sum_{i<j} V_{ij} + \cdots + V_{1,2,\ldots,p}\]

First-order Sobol index for input \(x_i\):

\[S_i = \frac{V_i}{\text{Var}[Y]} = \frac{\text{Var}_{x_i}\left[\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}_{\sim i}}[Y \mid x_i]\right]}{\text{Var}[Y]}\]

Fraction of total variance explained by \(x_i\) alone (ignoring interactions).

Total-effect index: \(S_{T_i} = 1 - \frac{\text{Var}_{\mathbf{x}_{\sim i}}\left[\mathbb{E}_{x_i}[Y \mid \mathbf{x}_{\sim i}]\right]}{\text{Var}[Y]}\)

Includes all interactions involving \(x_i\).

Sobol indices provide a global view of sensitivity. Unlike local partial derivatives, Sobol indices average over the entire input space and naturally account for interactions between parameters. The first-order index S_i measures the fraction of output variance attributable to input x_i alone. The total-effect index S_Ti includes all interactions between x_i and other inputs. If S_i and S_Ti are very different for a given input, that input is involved in significant interactions. Computing Sobol indices requires many model evaluations — typically thousands — but this is feasible when the model is an ML surrogate rather than a physical simulation. This is one of the key advantages of having an ML model: sensitivity analysis that would be prohibitively expensive with a physics simulation becomes routine.

32. SHAP Values for Feature Importance

Fair Attribution of Predictions (Neuer et al. 2024)

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) assigns each feature a contribution to each individual prediction:

\[\hat{f}(x) = \phi_0 + \sum_{j=1}^p \phi_j(x)\]

where \(\phi_0 = \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}]\) and \(\phi_j(x)\) is the SHAP value for feature \(j\) at input \(x\).

Based on game theory: The Shapley value is the unique attribution that satisfies:

  • Efficiency: contributions sum to the prediction
  • Symmetry: equal features get equal credit
  • Null: irrelevant features get zero credit

Visualization:

  • Beeswarm plot: One dot per sample, colored by feature value, positioned by SHAP value
  • Dependence plot: SHAP value of one feature vs its value, colored by an interacting feature
import shap
explainer = shap.TreeExplainer(rf_model)  # or KernelExplainer for any model
shap_values = explainer.shap_values(X_test)
shap.summary_plot(shap_values, X_test)

SHAP values are a powerful tool for understanding which features drive individual predictions, not just average feature importance. They are based on the Shapley value from cooperative game theory, which is the unique way to fairly distribute a total payoff among players. In our case, the players are the features and the payoff is the prediction. SHAP values satisfy three natural axioms: efficiency (contributions sum to the prediction), symmetry (equally important features get equal credit), and the null axiom (useless features get zero credit). The beeswarm plot shows the distribution of SHAP values for each feature across all test samples, giving both the importance and direction of each feature’s effect. We will cover SHAP in more detail in Unit 14 on explainability, but it is introduced here because it is closely related to sensitivity analysis and feature importance.

33. Process Windows: Definition

The Safe Operating Region

A process window is the region in parameter space where:

  1. The target property meets its specification (e.g., density > 99.5%)
  2. The process is robust to expected noise levels
  3. The model prediction is confident (low uncertainty)

Formally:

\[\mathcal{W} = \left\{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^p : \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}) \in [y_\text{min}, y_\text{max}] \;\wedge\; \|\nabla \hat{f}(\mathbf{x})\| < \tau \right\}\]

where \([y_\text{min}, y_\text{max}]\) is the specification range and \(\tau\) is the sensitivity threshold.

In additive manufacturing: The process window is the region in (Power, Speed) space that avoids both lack-of-fusion (insufficient melting) and keyhole (excessive vaporization) porosity.

The process window concept bridges ML model outputs and engineering decisions. It is the region in parameter space where the process delivers acceptable results reliably. The formal definition has two conditions: the predicted property must be within specification, and the gradient must be small enough that expected noise in the process parameters does not push the output outside specification. Visualizing process windows typically involves plotting a heatmap of the predicted property in a 2D slice of parameter space, with contour lines showing the specification boundaries. The process window is the area between these contours where the gradient is also below the sensitivity threshold. In practice, for additive manufacturing, the process window in power-speed space must avoid lack-of-fusion at low energy input and keyhole porosity at high energy input.

35. Robust Optimization vs Peak Optimization

Choosing Where to Operate

Peak Optimization

  • Finds the maximum of the predicted property
  • Operating point: \(\mathbf{x}^* = \arg\max_\mathbf{x} \hat{f}(\mathbf{x})\)
  • Risk: Small deviations cause large quality drops
  • Good for: laboratory research

Robust Optimization

  • Finds a plateau where the property is good AND stable
  • \(\mathbf{x}^* = \arg\max_\mathbf{x}\left[\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}) - \kappa \|\nabla \hat{f}(\mathbf{x})\|\right]\)
  • Benefit: Tolerates process noise
  • Good for: production/manufacturing
The Engineering Tradeoff

In production, a 5% lower peak performance with 10× better robustness is almost always the right choice. A process that produces 98% density every time is more valuable than one that produces 99.5% density half the time and 95% density the other half.

This distinction between peak optimization and robust optimization is crucial for translating ML results into production. In research, we often optimize for the best possible result under ideal conditions. In production, we need the best reliable result under realistic variation. The robust optimization objective includes a penalty for the gradient magnitude, weighted by a parameter kappa. This favors operating points on broad plateaus rather than narrow peaks. The callout box makes an important practical point: consistent performance at slightly lower levels is almost always more valuable than occasional peak performance with high variability. This is a fundamental principle of quality engineering that applies directly to ML-guided process optimization.

36. Case Study: LPBF Keyhole Porosity Avoidance

Laser Powder Bed Fusion Process Window

Problem: In LPBF additive manufacturing, porosity arises from two mechanisms:

  • Lack-of-fusion (too little energy): unmolten powder, large irregular pores
  • Keyhole (too much energy): vapor depression traps gas, spherical pores

ML approach:

  • Inputs: Laser power \(P\) [W], Scan speed \(v\) [mm/s], Layer thickness \(t\) [µm], Hatch spacing \(h\) [µm]
  • Output: Relative density [%] or porosity classification
  • Model: Random Forest trained on ~200 single-track experiments

Results:

  • The ML model identifies a band of safe parameters between two failure modes
  • Sensitivity analysis reveals that \(P/v\) (linear energy density) is the dominant parameter
  • The robust operating point is at the center of the safe band, not at the edge

This case study illustrates the full workflow. In laser powder bed fusion, there are two competing failure modes: lack-of-fusion porosity occurs when energy input is too low, and keyhole porosity occurs when energy input is too high. The process window is the band between these two failure modes in power-speed space. An ML model trained on experimental data can map out this window much more efficiently than a full design of experiments. Sensitivity analysis using the trained model reveals that the ratio of power to speed, which is the linear energy density, is the dominant parameter controlling porosity. The robust operating point is at the center of the safe band where the model is least sensitive to fluctuations in both power and speed. This is a canonical example of how ML enables systematic process window identification and robust operating point selection.

This flowchart summarizes the model selection workflow we recommend. Start by clearly defining the problem and success criteria. Perform exploratory data analysis with domain knowledge. Establish a simple baseline — often just predicting the mean or using linear regression. Then try two to three model families and evaluate them using cross-validation with appropriate grouping. If performance is not acceptable, diagnose whether the problem is high bias or high variance, and act accordingly. Once you have an acceptable model, tune its hyperparameters using nested CV. Finally, evaluate on a held-out test set that has never been used for any decision, and deploy. After deployment, continue monitoring the model’s performance to detect distribution shift. Always start simple and increase complexity only when justified by the data.

39. Hyperparameter Tuning Strategies

Grid Search, Random Search, and Bayesian Optimization

Grid Search

  • Evaluate all combinations on a fixed grid
  • \(k^p\) evaluations for \(p\) parameters with \(k\) values each
  • Exhaustive but scales poorly
  • Only feasible for 1-2 hyperparameters
from sklearn.model_selection import GridSearchCV
param_grid = {'alpha': [0.01, 0.1, 1, 10]}
gs = GridSearchCV(Ridge(), param_grid, cv=5)

Random Search

  • Sample hyperparameters from distributions
  • More efficient than grid for \(p > 2\) (McClarren 2021)
  • Covers more of the space per evaluation
  • Often finds good solutions faster
from sklearn.model_selection import RandomizedSearchCV
from scipy.stats import loguniform
param_dist = {'alpha': loguniform(1e-3, 1e2)}
rs = RandomizedSearchCV(Ridge(), param_dist,
                        n_iter=50, cv=5)

Bayesian Optimization

  • Builds a surrogate model of CV score vs hyperparameters
  • Selects next point to maximize expected improvement
  • Most sample-efficient for expensive models
  • Tools: Optuna, scikit-optimize
import optuna
def objective(trial):
    alpha = trial.suggest_loguniform('alpha', 1e-3, 1e2)
    model = Ridge(alpha=alpha)
    return cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=5).mean()
study = optuna.create_study(direction='maximize')
study.optimize(objective, n_trials=50)

Three main strategies for hyperparameter tuning. Grid search evaluates every combination on a predefined grid — it works well for one or two hyperparameters but scales exponentially. Random search samples from distributions and is more efficient because it explores more unique values per evaluation. Bergstra and Bengio showed that random search finds good hyperparameters in fewer evaluations than grid search when some hyperparameters are more important than others, which is usually the case. Bayesian optimization is the most sophisticated approach — it builds a probabilistic model of how the CV score depends on hyperparameters and uses this to intelligently choose the next configuration to evaluate. Optuna is a popular Python library for Bayesian hyperparameter optimization. For most materials science applications, random search with 50 to 100 iterations is a good default choice.

40. Learning Curves: Diagnosis Tool

Identifying Bias vs Variance from Data

Learning curve: Plot training error and validation error as a function of training set size.

High Bias (Underfitting)

  • Training error increases as data grows
  • Validation error decreases but plateaus high
  • Both converge to a large gap from target
  • Fix: More complex model, better features

High Variance (Overfitting)

  • Training error remains low
  • Validation error remains high
  • Large gap between training and validation
  • Fix: More data, regularization, simpler model
from sklearn.model_selection import learning_curve
train_sizes, train_scores, val_scores = learning_curve(
    model, X, y, train_sizes=np.linspace(0.1, 1.0, 10),
    cv=5, scoring='neg_mean_squared_error'
)

Learning curves are one of the most useful diagnostic tools in machine learning. By plotting training and validation error as a function of training set size, you can distinguish between high bias and high variance problems. In the high bias case, both errors converge to a high value — adding more data will not help because the model is too simple. You need a more complex model or better features. In the high variance case, training error is low but validation error is high — there is a large gap. Adding more data will help because it gives the model more information to generalize from. Regularization and model simplification also help. Scikit-learn provides a convenient learning_curve function that automates this analysis. Always plot learning curves before deciding whether to collect more data or change your model.

41. When to Collect More Data vs Improve the Model

A Decision Framework

Observation Diagnosis Action
High train error, high val error High bias More complex model or better features
Low train error, high val error High variance More data or regularization
Low train error, low val error, large gap to target Irreducible noise Better measurements or new features
Good CV but poor production performance Distribution shift Collect production-representative data

Collecting more data helps when:

  • You have high variance (overfitting)
  • Your current data does not cover the deployment distribution
  • You need to characterize rare events (defects, failures)

Improving the model helps when:

  • You have high bias (underfitting)
  • Domain knowledge suggests important features are missing
  • The relationship is highly nonlinear and you are using a linear model
Practical Rule of Thumb

If doubling your training data improves CV score by less than 5%, you have reached the point of diminishing returns for data collection. Focus on feature engineering or model architecture instead.

This decision framework helps practitioners decide where to invest their effort. In materials science, data collection is expensive — each new experiment may cost days of work and thousands of euros in materials and machine time. So the decision between collecting more data and improving the model is a real economic question. The learning curve is your diagnostic tool: if the learning curve shows high variance, more data will help. If it shows high bias, more data will not help — you need a better model. If both errors are low but still above your target, the bottleneck may be irreducible noise from measurement uncertainty, and you need better instruments or new types of measurements. If your model performs well in cross-validation but poorly in production, you likely have a distribution shift problem and need to collect data that is more representative of production conditions.

42. Think About This…

Regularization in Practice

You are training a neural network to predict fatigue life from 12 processing and microstructure features. Your dataset has \(n = 80\) samples.

The network has 2 hidden layers with 64 neurons each (~5,000 parameters).

You apply no regularization. After training:

  • Training \(R^2 = 0.99\)
  • 5-Fold CV \(R^2 = 0.35\)

Question: What regularization strategies would you combine to improve this?

Recommended combination:

  1. Reduce network size → 2 layers of 16 neurons (~300 parameters)
  2. Add Dropout (\(p = 0.3\)) after each hidden layer
  3. Early Stopping with patience = 15 epochs
  4. L2 weight decay (\(\lambda = 10^{-3}\)) in the optimizer
  5. Use data augmentation (small Gaussian noise on inputs)
  6. Tune all regularization hyperparameters via cross-validation

This engagement slide asks students to think about regularization in a realistic scenario. With 80 samples and 5,000 parameters, the model has 60 times more capacity than data — severe overfitting is guaranteed. The training R-squared of 0.99 confirms this. The CV R-squared of 0.35 shows the model barely generalizes. The recommended approach combines multiple regularization strategies: first, drastically reduce the network size to bring the parameter count closer to the data count. Then add dropout, early stopping, and L2 weight decay for additional regularization. Data augmentation with Gaussian noise effectively increases the dataset size. All regularization hyperparameters should be tuned via cross-validation, not set arbitrarily. The resulting model will have worse training R-squared but much better CV R-squared.

43. Domain-Informed Regularization

Using Physics to Constrain ML Models

Beyond generic regularization: Use domain knowledge to impose physically meaningful constraints.

Examples in materials science:

Constraint Implementation
Output must be positive (density, strength) Use ReLU or softplus output activation
Monotonic relationship (more energy → more melting) Constrained optimization or monotonic networks
Symmetry (isotropic material → rotation invariance) Equivariant architectures or augmentation
Conservation laws (mass, energy) Physics-informed loss terms
Known scaling laws (\(\sigma \propto d^{-1/2}\) Hall-Petch) Feature engineering: include \(d^{-1/2}\) as input
Physics-Informed ML

Domain-informed regularization is a form of inductive bias — it encodes prior knowledge about the problem into the model structure. This is especially powerful for small datasets where data alone cannot distinguish between physically plausible and implausible solutions. We will explore this further in Units 12-13.

Generic regularization techniques like L2 and dropout do not use any knowledge about the problem — they simply penalize complexity. Domain-informed regularization goes further by encoding physical knowledge into the model. If you know the output must be positive, use an appropriate activation function. If you know a relationship is monotonic, enforce this constraint. If you know the material is isotropic, use rotation-invariant features. If you know a scaling law from physics, include the appropriate transformed features. These constraints reduce the space of possible solutions, effectively acting as strong regularization. This is particularly valuable for small datasets where generic regularization alone may not prevent the model from learning unphysical relationships. We will explore physics-informed ML in more depth in later units.

44. Industrial Deployment Considerations

From Notebook to Production

Model validation is not the end — it is the beginning.

Deployment checklist:

  1. Distribution monitoring: Track input distributions; alert when production data differs from training data
  2. Performance monitoring: Continuously compare predictions to ground truth when available
  3. Model versioning: Track which model version produced which predictions
  4. Fallback strategy: What happens when the model fails? (default to conservative parameters)
  5. Retraining policy: When and how to update the model with new data

Common failure modes in production:

  • Covariate shift: New powder supplier → different particle size distribution
  • Concept drift: Machine aging → relationship between parameters and output changes
  • Out-of-distribution inputs: Operator enters parameter values outside training range

Best practice: Define an applicability domain — the region of input space where the model has been validated. Flag predictions outside this domain.

Deploying an ML model in production is fundamentally different from running it in a Jupyter notebook. The training data represents the world as it was during data collection. The production environment changes over time — new materials, equipment wear, different operators. Distribution monitoring detects when the inputs to the model have shifted away from the training distribution. Performance monitoring tracks whether the model’s predictions remain accurate. Fallback strategies define what happens when the model is uncertain or detects an anomaly — typically defaulting to conservative, well-tested parameters. Retraining policies determine when to update the model with new data. The applicability domain is a critical concept: the model should only be trusted within the region of input space where it has been validated, and predictions outside this region should be flagged for human review.

46. Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Lessons Learned

Pitfall Consequence Prevention
Using test data for model selection Optimistic performance estimate Nested CV or separate holdout
Ignoring group structure Data leakage, inflated \(R^2\) GroupKFold
No baseline model Cannot judge if complex model is worthwhile Always start with linear/mean
Over-engineering with deep learning Overfitting, uninterpretable Start simple, increase complexity if needed
Reporting training accuracy Meaningless metric Report CV or test accuracy only
Not checking learning curves Wrong diagnosis Always plot learning curves

The golden rule of applied ML:

“If your result seems too good to be true, it probably is. Check for data leakage.”

This slide summarizes the most common mistakes in applied ML for materials science. Each of these pitfalls is something I have seen repeatedly in published papers and student projects. The most dangerous is data leakage from ignoring group structure, because it produces results that look excellent but do not reflect real-world performance. Another common mistake is jumping to deep learning without establishing a baseline — you need to know what a simple model achieves before you can judge whether a complex model is justified. The golden rule is worth memorizing: if your R-squared is 0.99 on a materials dataset with 50 samples, you almost certainly have a bug, data leakage, or overfitting. Always be skeptical of results that seem too good.

48. Summary & Key Takeaways

1. Generalization is Everything

Models must perform on unseen data. The bias-variance tradeoff governs this.

2. Validate Rigorously

Use cross-validation with appropriate grouping and stratification. Never evaluate on training data.

3. Regularize Intelligently

Combine explicit penalties (L1/L2), implicit methods (early stopping, augmentation), and architectural approaches (dropout) — tuned via CV.

4. Think About Robustness

Optimize for stability, not just peak performance. Use sensitivity analysis to understand which parameters matter.

5. Start Simple, Increase Complexity Only When Justified

Linear models → Random Forests → Neural Networks. Always compare to a baseline.

These five takeaways capture the essence of this unit. Generalization is the only metric that matters for deployment. Cross-validation with appropriate grouping gives honest performance estimates. Regularization prevents overfitting, and the choice of regularization technique depends on the model type and the data. Robustness considerations transform ML from a prediction tool into a decision-support tool for manufacturing. And the principle of parsimony — start simple, add complexity only when the data justifies it — prevents most of the common pitfalls in applied ML.
