02. Why attention for materials — long-range correlations exceed CNN receptive fields

  • A diffraction pattern, a STEM image, an EELS line scan, an LPBF layer stack — all contain structure that spans the entire field of view.
  • The Bragg discs of a 4D-STEM pattern are geometrically constrained pairs: positions on opposite sides of the central beam carry coupled information (lattice vectors).
  • A CNN with 3×3 kernels needs ~\(L/2\) layers to connect two pixels \(L\) apart. On a 256×256 pattern that is 128 layers for global context
  • A transformer mixes all positions in one layer, at the cost of \(\mathcal{O}(L^2)\) in sequence length.
  • For micrographs we trade pixels for patches (ViT) to make \(L\) manageable.

Warning

“Long-range” is not a buzzword on materials data. EELS edges span hundreds of channels; LPBF microstructure depends on the previous five layers. If your model cannot see that far in one layer, it cannot learn the physics.

Materials hook. I want students to feel why the 3×3 conv hammer hits a ceiling on diffraction and on layered builds. Walk through the Bragg-pair example explicitly — pick a 256×256 CBED pattern, point at two discs 200 px apart, count the conv layers needed.

War story. The receptive-field argument is the slide to come back to in §C when we justify why transformers beat CNNs for diffraction — it is not aesthetic, it is geometric (Friedel pairs are exactly antipodal).

1080Ti budget. Even a 16×16-patch ViT on 256×256 has L=256, well inside what a 1080Ti runs with SDPA at batch 32.

Cross-reference. MFML W10 derives the receptive-field bound formally.

03. The inductive-bias triangle — CNN vs GNN vs transformer

Model Built-in assumption Pays off when Materials home
CNN Locality + translation equivariance Signal is local, lots of structure, less data Micrograph texture, single-disc tasks
GNN Relational, given graph structure You know the graph (atoms↔︎bonds) Crystal/molecule property (small data)
Transformer Everything can attend to everything (minimal bias) Relationship is long-range/unknown and data/pretraining Diffraction, multimodal, sequences
  • A transformer has the weakest inductive bias — that is its power (learns the relation) and its curse (needs data to learn it).
  • Choosing a model is choosing how much physics to hard-code vs learn.

Note

This triangle is the thread of the entire unit — every decision slide (§B (the §B synthesis slide), §C (the “why transformers beat CNNs for diffraction” slide), §E (the decision-guide slide)) is one corner of it.

The conceptual spine. Introduce the triangle early so every later decision slide is “which corner?”. The deep point: weak inductive bias = flexibility traded for data hunger. CNNs hard-code locality, GNNs hard-code the bond graph, transformers hard-code almost nothing.

Materials hook. Materials data is small, so hard-coded physics (CNN locality, GNN graph) is often the better deal — unless the relationship is genuinely long-range/unknown, which is the transformer’s niche.

Cross-reference. Forward to the §B synthesis slide (GNN vs crystal transformer), the §C “why transformers beat CNNs for diffraction” slide, and the §E data-efficiency / decision-guide slides (the honest comparison). MFML W10 for the formal capacity argument.

04. Refresher: scaled dot-product attention — the formula and the cost

\[ \mathrm{Attn}(Q, K, V) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V \]

  • \(Q, K, V \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d_k}\) — queries, keys, values; one row per token.
  • \(QK^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L}\) — pairwise similarity; this is the memory bottleneck.
  • \(\sqrt{d_k}\) scaling keeps softmax saturation in check at high \(d_k\).
  • Multi-head: run \(h\) attention blocks of dim \(d_k / h\) in parallel, concat; cheap diversity.

Complexity:

  • Compute: \(\mathcal{O}(L^2 \, d_k)\).
  • Memory (naive): \(\mathcal{O}(L^2)\) to materialise the attention matrix.
  • At \(L = 1024\), \(L^2 = 10^6\) entries per head per layer — fits, but tight on 11 GB.
  • This is why Flash Attention (later) matters: same math, \(\mathcal{O}(L)\) memory.

Note

Reference: (Vaswani et al. 2017). Derivation, gradient flow, why pre-norm beats post-norm — all in MFML W10.

Mechanism. \(QK^\top\) is what couples every token to every other token — which is exactly what we wanted for diffraction patterns.

1080Ti budget. A naive attention on L=4096 needs 16M entries × batch × heads — blows past 11 GB instantly. This number motivates the Flash Attention slides.

Cross-reference. MFML W10: refer students there for the full per-head, per-layer FLOP and memory count and for the positional-encoding zoo. Do not re-derive softmax gradients here.

05. Positional encodings for materials — the materials-specific crux

Attention is permutation-equivariant: without position information it is a deep set. For language you add 1-D sinusoids. Materials axes are not a sentence:

  • Micrograph / diffraction (2-D): token at \((0,0)\) is the central beam, token at \((7,7)\) is a high-\(k\) disc. Need 2-D encodings (learned, 2-D sinusoidal, or 2-D RoPE).
  • Crystal (3-D, periodic): the lattice is infinite and periodic. A naive absolute position is meaningless — position must be periodic-invariant (fractional coordinates, lattice-aware relative encodings). §B is built on this.
  • Spectrum (1-D, physical axis): the energy/2θ axis has physical units. The encoding should respect that an EELS edge at 532 eV is a fixed absolute location, not a free token order.
  • Process sequence (1-D, causal): LPBF layer index is causal and physically ordered — relative/causal encodings, not bidirectional absolute.

Warning

Get the positional encoding wrong and the transformer silently learns a physics-free permutation-invariant map. This is the single most common materials-transformer bug — and it is invisible until you ablate.

[FIGURE: four mini-panels — 2-D grid over a CBED pattern; periodic lattice with translation arrows; 1-D energy axis with an EELS edge marked; causal layer-index ladder for LPBF — each labelled with its required positional-encoding family]

Why this slide is new. The old deck folded this into an anti-pattern bullet. It is not an anti-pattern — it is the materials-specific design decision and deserves a full slide. MFML W10 covers the encoding zoo; what it does not do is map each family onto a materials modality. That mapping is the value added here.

Mechanism. Permutation-equivariance is a feature for sets, a bug for diffraction. The fix is not “add positions” generically — it is “add the right positional structure for this physical axis”.

Materials hook. The periodic-crystal case is the deepest: you literally cannot write down an absolute position for an infinite crystal. That impossibility is what motivates Matformer / Crystalformer / ComFormer in §B — flag it forward now.

Anti-pattern + diagnostic. Diagnostic for “did I get positions right?”: ablate the positional embedding and watch accuracy. On diffraction it should collapse. If it does not, your task was not using geometry and a transformer was the wrong tool.

Cross-reference. ML-PC u09 MAE-on-spectra also has to token an energy axis — same issue, revisited in §C.

06. Vision Transformer (ViT) in five lines

  1. Patchify the image into \(P\times P\) patches (e.g. 16×16). Each patch becomes one token. A 224×224 image gives \(L = 196\).
  2. Linear embed each flattened patch into a \(d\)-dimensional vector.
  3. Prepend a learnable [CLS] token; add positional embeddings (one per slot, including CLS).
  4. Stack \(N\) transformer encoder blocks (LayerNorm → MHA → MLP → residual).
  5. The final state of [CLS] is the image representation; classify with a linear head (Dosovitskiy et al. 2021).

Diagram in words: image → grid of patches → flatten → linear projection → token sequence → [CLS] + pos. emb. → \(N \times\) encoder → take [CLS] → MLP head.

Warning

ViT was originally trained on JFT-300M. Pretraining matters: on small materials datasets, a ViT trained from scratch will overfit catastrophically — pretrain (MAE / DINO) or use a pretrained backbone. See the data-efficiency slide in §E.

Mechanism. ViT is just “the 2017 encoder applied to image patches” — the only new ideas vs. NLP are patchify and 2-D positions (previous slide).

Materials hook. The patch size is the lever — full slide next. ViT-Tiny (d=192, 12 layers, 3 heads) is the workhorse we use throughout this deck.

Cross-reference. MFML W10 for the encoder-block internals; ML-PC u09 for MAE pretraining of exactly this encoder; §E for the honest “when NOT to use a ViT” slide.

Pre-empt the question. “Why [CLS] and not global average pooling?” — both work; [CLS] is the original and what pretrained checkpoints expose. GAP is a fine drop-in and sometimes more robust on small data.

07. Tokenisation choices for micrographs, diffraction, and spectra

The token is the inductive bias. What you choose to call one token decides what the model can and cannot mix in one layer.

  • Micrograph (HRTEM/SEM): square patches. Smaller \(P\) → more tokens → finer features but \(L^2\) cost. \(P{=}16\) on 256² → \(L{=}256\).
  • Diffraction (CBED/SAED): patches break disc geometry if a Bragg disc straddles a patch boundary. Options: smaller patches, overlapping patches, or detect discs first and token per disc.
  • Spectrum (EELS/XAS/XRD): token along the energy / 2θ axis — fixed-width windows, or peak-centred tokens. The axis is physical; do not shuffle it.
  • 4D-STEM: two valid choices — token the detector (one sequence per scan position) or token the scan grid (one token per probe position). They answer different questions.

Note

There is no universal patch. Choosing tokens is a modelling decision with a physics consequence — make it explicitly, then ablate it.

[FIGURE: same physical datum tokenised three ways — square patches over a micrograph; per-disc tokens over a CBED pattern; window tokens over an EELS edge]

Mechanism + materials hook. This is the slide that distinguishes a materials ML course from a generic DL course. The patch grid is borrowed from natural images where it is arbitrary; on diffraction it is not arbitrary because disc geometry carries the lattice.

War story. A common failure: 16×16 patches on a CBED pattern where discs are ~10 px — discs land on patch seams, the model never sees a whole disc, accuracy plateaus far below a disc-detector + transformer pipeline. Diagnostic: visualise patch boundaries over real discs before you train.

Cross-reference. Forward to §C 4D-STEM ViT and “why transformers beat CNNs for diffraction”; ML-PC u09 for spectral tokenisation revisited with MAE.

Pacing. Keep tight — the payoff lands in §C; here just plant the three options.

08. Flash Attention — the kernel that makes long sequences tractable

  • Same mathematics as scaled dot-product attention; different IO pattern.
  • Naive attention writes the full \(L\times L\) matrix to HBM, then reads it back for softmax and the \(\cdot V\) multiply. Memory-bound.
  • Flash Attention tiles the computation, keeps the running softmax statistics in SRAM, and never materialises the full matrix in HBM (Dao et al. 2022).

Net effect:

  • \(\mathcal{O}(L)\) memory at fixed head size.
  • 2–10× wall-clock speedup at long \(L\).
  • Exact, not approximate — same gradients.

Practical PyTorch:

import torch.nn.functional as F

# auto-dispatches to the best available kernel
y = F.scaled_dot_product_attention(
        q, k, v,
        attn_mask=None,
        dropout_p=0.0,
        is_causal=False,
)

Mechanism. The whole trick is IO-awareness: never write the \(L\times L\) matrix to slow HBM; recompute it tile-by-tile in fast SRAM with an online-softmax accumulator.

1080Ti budget. This is the single piece of plumbing that unlocks training a 4D-STEM ViT on a 1080Ti at all. Without SDPA, batch 8 on L=1024 OOMs; with SDPA, batch 32 fits. The Pascal caveat is the next slide — do not pre-empt it here.

Cross-reference. MFML W10 derives the complexity; here we care only that the math is unchanged and the memory is now linear.

10. Scaling alternatives — MoE, Mamba, SSMs (names to recognise)

  • Mixture of Experts (MoE) — replace the MLP block with \(E\) parallel MLPs and a router that sends each token to the top-\(k\) experts. Sparse compute: \(E\)× parameters, only \(k\)× FLOPs. Useful for a large model on a small inference budget.
  • Mamba / structured state-space models (SSMs) — recurrent formulation with \(\mathcal{O}(L)\) compute and constant memory; competitive with transformers on long sequences (Gu and Dao 2023). Cross-reference the ML-PC u07 time-series deck, where the Mamba/SSM slide lives.
  • For our 1080Ti / lab-scale budget, neither is needed at typical materials sequence lengths (\(L \lesssim 4096\) with SDPA).
  • They become relevant for whole-build LPBF sequences (\(L \sim 10^5\) layers × patches) or multi-spectrum joint models.

Note

Pick the right tool. A vanilla ViT + SDPA solves 90 % of materials tasks at our scale. MoE / Mamba are for the long tail.

Pacing. Deliberately short — students should know the names so they recognise them in papers; they should not leave thinking they need a 70B MoE to do strain mapping.

Cross-reference. ML-PC u07 (time-series) introduced SSMs as a sequence-model alternative; this is a pointer, not a re-teach. The whole-build LPBF sequence reappears in §D process monitoring.

11. The periodicity problem — naive attention breaks on infinite crystals

  • A crystal is defined by a unit cell + lattice vectors, repeated infinitely. The “input” has no finite token set and no absolute coordinate origin.
  • A finite molecular transformer (token = atom, absolute/relative 3-D position) does not transfer: which periodic image of an atom do you tokenise? All of them?
  • Property prediction must be invariant to: choice of unit cell, lattice translations, and (for many properties) rotations/reflections.

Warning

Tokenise “the atoms in one unit cell” with absolute positions and you have silently broken translational invariance — the model can read off the (arbitrary) cell origin. This is the crystal analogue of the §A positional-encoding bug.

[FIGURE: a 2-D crystal with its unit cell highlighted; the same atom shown in several periodic images; arrow to “which image is the token?”]

Mechanism + materials hook. This is the conceptual hinge of §B. Make the impossibility concrete: there is no finite list of atoms and no canonical origin, so the entire finite-transformer playbook needs reworking.

Pre-empt the question. “Can’t I just take a big supercell and use a normal transformer?” — you can, but you have re-introduced an arbitrary boundary and lost exact periodic invariance; the three papers exist precisely to avoid that hack.

Cross-reference. §A positional-encoding slide flagged this forward; ML-PC u04 graph reps for the GNN framing that the next slides build on.

19. 4D-STEM ViT for rapid microstructure inference (Je et al. 2026)

  • 4D-STEM: at each scan position \((x,y)\) the detector records a 2-D diffraction pattern \(I(k_x,k_y)\) — a 4-D tensor, GB-to-TB scale.
  • Case study (preprint): a transformer ingests the diffraction patterns and infers microstructure (phase / orientation / strain-type labels) per scan position, far faster than iterative template matching or disc fitting.

What it bought over the CNN/classical baseline:

  • One-layer global mixing of antipodal discs → orientation/phase inference without an explicit template library.
  • Rapid inference: amortised forward pass replaces per-pattern optimisation across millions of patterns.

Warning

Marked as a preprint — treat the specific numbers as unverified. The architectural argument (global disc coupling) is sound regardless.

What the paper did (preprint). Data: 4D-STEM scans; task: per-scan-position microstructure inference (phase/orientation). Baseline: template matching / disc-fitting pipelines that optimise per pattern. What the transformer bought: amortised inference (one forward pass vs. per-pattern optimisation across millions of patterns) plus library-free orientation/phase via global disc coupling.

Materials hook. This upgrades the old generic “ViT on 4D-STEM” slide into a real, citable case study. Orientation mapping today is template matching; strain mapping is disc fitting; this replaces both with a learned amortised map.

Anti-pattern + diagnostic. A single 4D-STEM scan (e.g. 256×256 positions × 256×256 detector) does not fit in 11 GB — train on randomly-sampled scan-position minibatches. Diagnostic for overfitting: hold out whole scans, not random positions (positions within a scan are correlated).

20. 4D-STEM ViT — the concrete pipeline & MAE pretext

Pipeline (diagram in words): 4D-STEM scan → sample scan positions → per-position diffraction pattern (256×256) → patchify (\(P{=}16\), \(L{=}256\)) → 2-D positional embedding (§A) → ViT-Tiny encoder → per-position head (phase / orientation / strain-type).

MAE pretext (the materials answer to data hunger):

  • Mask 75 % of diffraction patches; reconstruct; no labels — the unlabelled 4D-STEM stack is the pretraining corpus (ML-PC u09).
  • Then freeze/lightly-finetune on the 50–500 labelled scan positions you can afford.

Note

Practical recipe: ViT-Tiny (\(d{=}192\), 12 layers), patch 16, 256×256, MAE-pretrained on unlabelled 4D-STEM, finetuned on labels.

Mechanism + materials hook. This is the “how would I actually build it” slide that pays off the 4D-STEM ViT case-study slide and the §A tokenisation/positional content. The MAE pretext is the bridge to ML-PC u09 (callback to the §A recap slide: the u09 MAE encoder was exactly this).

1080Ti budget. A single scan does not fit in VRAM; sample scan-position minibatches. Batch 32 fp16 with SDPA on ViT-Tiny is the calibrated workhorse setting.

Cross-reference. ML-PC u09 (MAE), §A slides 04/06, §E the pretrain→finetune recipe slide recipe and the exercise slide exercise instantiate this at toy scale.

22. Self-supervised ViT foundation models for EM (Sauter and Brockhauser 2024)

  • Idea: DINOv2-style self-supervised pretraining of a ViT on millions of unlabelled micrographs → a general-purpose EM feature extractor (“foundation model for advanced image analysis”, arXiv:2402.18286).
  • No labels needed for pretraining; downstream tasks (classification, segmentation, retrieval) use a light head on frozen or lightly-tuned features.

What it bought:

  • Strong few-label downstream performance — the materials-lab regime (50–500 labels) is exactly where this pays off.
  • A reusable backbone across instruments / specimens, mitigating the per-lab data scarcity problem.

Note

This is the materials answer to “ViTs are data-hungry”: pretrain self-supervised on the unlabelled mountain you already have.

Mechanism. DINOv2-style self-distillation: a student ViT matches a teacher’s representation under augmentations, no labels. The micrograph archive every EM lab already has becomes the pretraining corpus.

Materials hook + cross-reference. This is the direct bridge between ML-PC u06 (DINOv2) and u09 (SSL on spectra) — same recipe, microscopy modality. It is also the answer to the §E “data-efficiency reality” slide.

What the paper did. Pretrain on millions of unlabelled EM images; evaluate few-label downstream. The headline is sample-efficiency on downstream tasks, not a single accuracy number — frame it that way.

23. DiffractGPT — XRD pattern → structure as sequence generation (Choudhary 2024)

  • DiffractGPT (J. Phys. Chem. Lett. 2024): a generative pretrained transformer that maps an X-ray diffraction pattern → atomic structure, framed as sequence generation (pattern in, structure tokens out).
  • Inverts the usual forward problem: instead of predicting a pattern from a structure, it generates the structure that explains the pattern.

What it bought:

  • Amortised structure solution: a forward generation pass instead of iterative search / Rietveld refinement seeding.
  • A genuinely generative use of transformers in characterization — the §C counterpart to discriminative ViTs.

Note

Pattern → structure is an inverse problem; DiffractGPT treats structure determination as conditional sequence generation.

Mechanism. Autoregressive decoder conditioned on the diffraction pattern, emitting a tokenised structure (composition / lattice / sites). This is the generative-transformer slide of the unit — contrast with the discriminative ViTs around it.

What the paper did. Data: XRD patterns paired with known structures; task: conditional structure generation; baseline: classical search / refinement seeding. What it bought: amortised candidate generation that can seed or replace iterative solution.

Cross-reference. ML-PC u08 (inverse problems) — pattern→structure is an inverse problem; this is its transformer instantiation. Pre-empt: “is the generated structure physically valid?” — generation gives candidates; physical validation/refinement still follows.

24. ViT / multimodal transformers for XRD phase ID (Simonnet et al. 2025)

  • Vision Transformers for XRD analysis (HAL preprint, 2024): treat the 1-D (or 2-D) diffraction pattern as a ViT input for phase identification / classification.
  • Multimodal variant: fuse the pattern with elemental priors (known chemistry) as extra tokens — chemistry constrains the phase search.

What it bought over a 1-D CNN:

  • Global peak-relationship modelling: phase ID depends on the set of peak positions and their ratios — a long-range relationship across the 2θ axis.
  • Easy multimodal fusion: elemental composition enters as additional tokens, not a bolted-on branch.

Note

Phase ID is a relational task across the whole pattern — exactly the receptive-field argument from the receptive-field slide, on a 1-D axis.

Mechanism + materials hook. Phase identification is not local: it is the joint pattern of peak positions/intensities. A transformer mixes all peaks in one layer; multimodal tokenisation lets elemental priors (which dramatically prune the phase space) enter as tokens.

What the paper did. Pattern (± elemental prior) → phase label; baseline 1-D CNN. The transformer’s gain is relational modelling + clean multimodal fusion.

Cross-reference. Mirrors the receptive-field slide’s receptive-field argument on the 2θ axis; ML-PC u09 for spectral modelling; the §C synthesis slide ties diffraction reasoning together.

25. Transformers for spectra — EELS / XAS (token the energy axis)

  • Spectral transformer: token the energy-loss / absorption axis (fixed-width windows or peak-centred tokens), encode with absolute physical-axis positions (§A), classify or regress.
  • Tasks: edge identification, oxidation-state / coordination regression, fine-structure (ELNES/XANES) interpretation.

Why a transformer over a 1-D CNN:

  • Fine-structure correlations span hundreds of channels — pre/post-edge relationships are long-range on the energy axis.
  • Self-attention couples the edge onset and the EXAFS oscillations in one layer.

Warning

The energy axis is physical: 532 eV is the O-K edge whether or not it is token #17. Do not shuffle tokens; use absolute-axis positional encoding.

Mechanism + materials hook. Pre-edge, white-line, EXAFS oscillations are long-range relationships on a physical axis — the §A energy-axis positional-encoding case, applied.

Cross-reference. Direct callback to ML-PC u09 MAE-on-spectra: same modality, MAE is the pretext, this is the supervised head. State explicitly that the u09 MAE encoder is a spectral transformer — we have now opened that box (callback to the §A recap slide).

26. Multimodal & cross-modal transformers for characterization

  • Real materials problems rarely give you one modality. Attention is a natural fusion mechanism: concatenate token sequences from different modalities and let cross-attention align them.
  • Examples: image + diffraction (HRTEM + FFT/CBED), spectrum + composition prior (the XRD phase-ID slide XRD + elements), micrograph + process metadata (AM frame + machine parameters).

Why attention beats a late-fusion concat:

  • Tokens from each modality keep their own positional structure (§A); cross-attention learns which tokens of modality B explain modality A.
  • Missing-modality robustness: a transformer trained with modality dropout degrades gracefully when one stream is absent at deployment.

Warning

Multimodal fusion multiplies \(L\) — apply the the volumetric cross-attention slide lesson: cross-attention with separate \(K,V\) per modality, not one giant self-attention over the concatenation.

[FIGURE: two token streams (micrograph patches; spectrum windows) feeding a cross-attention block, with a fused [CLS] readout]

Mechanism + materials hook. This generalises the the XRD phase-ID slide multimodal XRD example into the unit’s fusion principle: attention is the cheapest correct way to combine heterogeneous materials signals because each modality retains its own positional encoding.

War story. Late-fusion (train two CNNs, average logits) throws away cross-modal alignment; the gain of attention fusion is precisely learning which spectral channel explains which image region. Modality-dropout training is the practical robustness trick.

Cross-reference. The μSAM slide (XRD + elemental priors), the volumetric cross-attention slide (cost control), §A (per-modality positional structure), ML-PC u06 (multimodal transfer).

27. Volumetric & serial-section cross-attention (tomography / stacks)

  • Setting: serial-section EM, FIB-SEM stacks, electron tomography — the signal at slice \(z\) depends on slices \(z\pm k\) (continuity of structure through the volume).
  • Architecture: per-slice ViT encoder → cross-attention over the slice axis (\(Q\) from slice \(z\); \(K,V\) from neighbours \(z-k..z+k\)).

Why cross-attention, not concatenation:

  • Concatenating slices along the token axis multiplies \(L\) by the stack depth → \(L^2\) blows up by depth².
  • Cross-attention with separate \(K,V\) keeps cost linear in stack depth.

Warning

This is the reframed “long-stack” slide: the same trick that handles LPBF layer history (§D) handles tomography slice continuity. One mechanism, two materials problems.

Mechanism. Cross-attention over the depth axis is the original 2017 encoder–decoder construct (MFML W10 — reference, do not re-derive). The cost argument is the load-bearing content: separate \(K,V\) keeps it linear in depth where concatenation is quadratic.

Materials hook. Reframes the old generic “LPBF long-stack” slide for tomography, making it characterization-side; §D reuses the identical mechanism for processing-side layer history. Calling out the shared mechanism is the pedagogical point.

Cross-reference. Forward to §D AM monitoring; MFML W10 for cross-attention derivation.

28. Why transformers beat CNNs for diffraction — the synthesis slide

  • A 2-D diffraction pattern’s information is geometrically long-range by construction:
    • Friedel pairs \(I(\mathbf{k}) \leftrightarrow I(-\mathbf{k})\) are exactly antipodal across the central beam.
    • Lattice vectors are read from disc pairs on opposite sides of the pattern.
  • A CNN needs \(\sim L/2\) stacked 3×3 layers to couple antipodal discs; a transformer couples them in layer 1.
  • This is not an aesthetic preference — it is the receptive-field argument of the receptive-field slide, instantiated on the specific geometry of diffraction.
  • It is also why the §B crystal case is analogous: long-range periodic structure ↔︎ long-range reciprocal-space structure.

Note

Use a transformer on diffraction when the task depends on antipodal/long-range disc relationships (orientation, strain, phase). For a purely local task (single-disc intensity), a CNN is fine and cheaper.

The keystone slide of §C. Friedel symmetry makes “long-range” exact and geometric, not heuristic — antipodal pixels are physically coupled. This is the rigorous version of the receptive-field slide’s hand-wave.

Anti-pattern. Do not reach for a transformer on a local diffraction task (e.g. integrate one disc) — a CNN is cheaper and just as good. The transformer earns its cost only when the relationship is genuinely long-range.

Cross-reference. The receptive-field slide (receptive field), §B (analogous long-range periodic structure), next slide (microscopy anti-patterns).

29. Microscopy-transformer anti-patterns

  • Data hunger. ViT from scratch on 500 micrographs → catastrophic overfit; a ResNet18 beats it. Fix: SSL/MAE pretraining (the EM foundation-model slide) or a pretrained backbone.
  • Positional embeddings on diffraction. Forgetting (or shuffling) them deletes the disc geometry — the §A bug, EM edition. Diagnostic: ablate; accuracy must crash.
  • Dose / SNR mismatch. Train on high-dose, deploy on low-dose (beam-sensitive specimens) → silent failure. Fix: dose-augment, or include low-dose data.
  • Patch boundaries through discs. (the tokenisation slide) — discs straddling patch seams cap accuracy.

Warning

The dose anti-pattern is the sneaky one: the model is confident and wrong on the low-dose beam-sensitive sample you actually care about. Always validate at deployment dose.

Anti-pattern + diagnostic, all materials-specific. Each is a real lab failure: (1) from-scratch ViT on a small SEM set; (2) shuffled tokens as “augmentation”; (3) high-dose-trained model deployed on a dose-limited MOF/biological/battery sample; (4) the the tokenisation slide patch-seam trap.

War story. Dose mismatch: a strain-mapping ViT trained on robust inorganic data, deployed on a dose-sensitive battery cathode at 1/10 the dose — confidently wrong, no error flag. UQ (ML-PC u11) is the safety net.

Cross-reference. §A positional encodings, the tokenisation slide tokenisation, ML-PC u11 (UQ) as the deployment-safety follow-up.

30. Attention maps as a diagnostic — what did the model look at?

  • Unlike a CNN, a transformer exposes attention weights: for a prediction you can ask which patches/discs/channels the [CLS] token attended to.
  • Attention rollout composes the per-layer attention into a single saliency map over the input.

Why this matters in a materials lab:

  • A correct orientation-mapping ViT should attend to the Bragg discs, not the vacuum or the beam stop. If it attends to detector artefacts, you have a data leak, not a model.
  • It is a physics sanity check, not a full explanation — attention ≠ causal attribution (a known caveat).

Warning

Use attention maps to catch shortcuts (the model classifying phase from a detector hot-pixel), not to claim mechanistic understanding. This is exactly when to keep nn.MultiheadAttention (the practical-recipe (SDPA) slide) — you need the weights.

[FIGURE: a CBED pattern with an attention-rollout heatmap overlaid — high weight on Bragg discs, low on vacuum; alongside a failure case attending to a detector seam]

Mechanism + war story. Attention rollout is the practical interpretability tool for materials ViTs. The canonical save: a “great accuracy” orientation model that was actually reading a detector defect correlated with sample tilt — the attention map made the shortcut obvious; held-out accuracy on a clean detector then collapsed.

Anti-pattern + diagnostic. Caveat the students: attention is not a faithful explanation (well-documented). Use it as a shortcut-detector, not a proof.

Cross-reference. The §C recap slide (nn.MultiheadAttention is the right tool when you need weights), the microscopy anti-patterns slide (data-leak anti-patterns), ML-PC u11 (UQ as the complementary safety net).

31. Transformers for atomic-resolution image restoration & atom finding

  • Setting: low-dose / beam-sensitive HRTEM/STEM — noisy, drift-distorted images where atoms must still be localised.
  • Transformer use: denoising/restoration and atom-column detection with attention over the whole field (atom columns are a periodic, long-range arrangement — the §C receptive-field argument on real-space images).

Why attention over a denoising CNN:

  • Lattice periodicity is a global prior: a transformer can enforce “atoms lie on a consistent lattice” across the whole image in one layer; a CNN only locally.
  • Pairs naturally with SSL pretraining (the EM foundation-model slide) — unlabelled noisy stacks are abundant.

Note

Real-space periodicity is long-range too — the diffraction argument (the “why transformers beat CNNs for diffraction” slide) has a direct-space twin.

Mechanism + materials hook. Closes the loop with the “why transformers beat CNNs for diffraction” slide: periodicity is long-range whether you are in reciprocal space (diffraction) or real space (atomic-resolution images). The global lattice prior is exactly what a transformer’s one-layer mixing supplies and a denoising CNN cannot.

Anti-pattern + diagnostic. Dose mismatch (the microscopy anti-patterns slide) bites hardest here — a restoration model trained at high dose hallucinates atoms at low dose. Diagnostic: validate at deployment dose and check atom counts against an independent measurement.

Cross-reference. The XRD phase-ID slide (reciprocal-space twin), the EM foundation-model slide (SSL pretraining), the microscopy anti-patterns slide (dose anti-pattern), ML-PC u09 (denoising/AE lineage).

35. Melt-pool sequence modelling — the layer-history case

  • The physics: melt-pool morphology / angle at layer \(\ell\) depends on the thermal history of layers \(\ell-1, \ell-2, \ldots, \ell-k\) — an overhang two layers below changes local cooling and thus porosity.
  • The model: per-layer (or per-frame) encoder → cross-attention over the layer/time axis (reuse the volumetric cross-attention slide’s mechanism on the processing side).

What attention buys over a per-layer CNN:

  • A per-layer CNN is memoryless — it cannot represent “this defect was caused by an overhang 3 layers ago”.
  • Layer-axis attention makes the hidden thermal-history variable visible to the model; predicts porosity / lack-of-fusion before it is buried.

Warning

Whole-build sequences are long (\(L\sim10^5\)). This is the genuine long-tail where Mamba/SSM (the scaling-alternatives (Mamba/SSM) slide, ML-PC u07) competes with attention — do not force dense attention over an entire build.

Mechanism + materials hook. This is the dedicated case slide promised by the outline (it absorbs the cancelled W7 supplementary on melt-pool-angle sequences). The killer argument: the per-layer CNN is memoryless and thermal history is the hidden variable in LPBF — attention is the inductive bias that exposes it.

Anti-pattern + diagnostic. Truncating a 5000-layer build to a 32-layer window discards long-range thermal history. Diagnostic: if porosity correlates with features >k layers back, your window is too short — switch to SSM.

Cross-reference. The DiffractGPT slide (cross-attention cost), the scaling-alternatives (Mamba/SSM) slide + ML-PC u07 (long-build → Mamba/SSM), ML-PC u11 (a porosity call without a confidence band is not a deliverable).

38. Processing anti-patterns

  • Deployment drift. Train on machine A / powder lot 1 / season; deploy on machine B / lot 2. The melt-pool statistics moved; the model did not. Monitor input distribution; retrain on drift.
  • Label scarcity. Defect labels in AM are expensive (CT-scan ground truth). A from-scratch transformer starves. Fix: pretrain on unlabelled in-situ video; few-label finetune.
  • Sequence length denial. Truncating a whole build to 32 layers loses long-range thermal history. If you need the full build, use SSM/Mamba (the scaling-alternatives (Mamba/SSM) slide).
  • No uncertainty on a quality call. A scrap/keep decision without a confidence band is not a deliverable.

Warning

Diagnostic for drift: track a held-out current-production validation stream, not just the original test set. A model that was accurate last quarter can be silently wrong this quarter.

Anti-pattern + diagnostic. Processing is where drift bites hardest — powder lots, machine recalibration, seasonal humidity all move the input distribution. The diagnostic (a live current-production validation stream) is the deployable safeguard.

39. Practical recipe — nn.MultiheadAttention vs F.scaled_dot_product_attention

Old-style (nn.MultiheadAttention):

import torch.nn as nn

mha = nn.MultiheadAttention(
        embed_dim=192,
        num_heads=3,
        batch_first=True,
)
y, attn_w = mha(q, k, v)
# pre-PT 2.0: materialises L×L
  • Returns attention weights for free.
  • Materialises the \(L\times L\) matrix on older PyTorch.
  • Use only when you actually need the weights (e.g. attention-rollout visualisation).

New-style (F.scaled_dot_product_attention):

import torch.nn.functional as F

y = F.scaled_dot_product_attention(
        q, k, v, is_causal=False,
)
# auto-dispatches to Flash / mem-eff / math
  • No attention weights returned (by design).
  • \(\mathcal{O}(L)\) memory; 2–10× faster on Ampere+.
  • Default choice for all new code.

Note

nn.MultiheadAttention is not deprecated — it is the wrong default. Use it only when you need the weights.

Materials hook. This is the slide students copy-paste. The one nuance: keep nn.MultiheadAttention in your toolbox specifically for attention-rollout visualisations (interpreting which Bragg discs the model attended to) — there the weights are the deliverable.

Cross-reference. The Flash-Attention slide (Pascal caveat) for what “2–10×” really means on a 1080Ti; the bench-it slide for the bench numbers.

41. Data-efficiency reality — when NOT to use a transformer (the honest slide)

  • ViTs have weak inductive bias vs CNNs. With ~\(10^3\) labels and no pretraining, a ResNet18 beats a from-scratch ViT — every time.
  • A tuned GNN beats a crystal transformer on a few-thousand-structure dataset (the §B synthesis slide).
  • For a local task (single Bragg disc, one peak), a CNN/1-D CNN is cheaper and just as accurate (the “why transformers beat CNNs for diffraction” slide).

Use a transformer when: the relationship is genuinely long-range/relational and you have either lots of labels or a pretraining corpus (labelled or unlabelled).

Warning

“We used a transformer” is not a result. The result is the transformer beat a tuned CNN/GNN baseline on a fair split — and on small materials data that often does not happen without pretraining.

The honest slide of the unit. Every prior part argued for transformers; this one draws the boundary. The recurring lab failure is the from-scratch ViT on 500 images that loses to ResNet18 — name it explicitly.

Cross-reference. The §B synthesis slide (GNN vs crystal transformer), the §C “why transformers beat CNNs for diffraction” slide (local vs long-range), the EM foundation-model and pretrain→finetune recipe slides (pretraining is the escape hatch), ML-PC u06 (transfer) and u09 (SSL pretraining).

Pre-empt the question. “So when should I use one?” — the green box: long-range/relational AND (data OR pretraining). That is the decision rule.

42. The pretrain → finetune recipe for materials

  1. Collect the unlabelled mountain you already have (4D-STEM stacks, micrograph archives, in-situ AM video, spectra).
  2. Self-supervised pretrain a ViT — MAE (ML-PC u09) or DINOv2-style (the EM foundation-model slide) — on it. No labels needed.
  3. Freeze or lightly finetune the backbone; train a small head on your 50–500 labels.
  4. Validate honestly — group split (by scan/build/specimen), fair tuned baseline, calibrated uncertainty (ML-PC u11).

Note

This four-step recipe is the materials answer to “transformers are data-hungry”. The unlabelled data is free; the labels are the bottleneck — so pretrain on the former, spend the latter sparingly.

Mechanism + materials hook. This operationalises slides 19/34. The key materials twist is step 4’s group split: random splitting leaks (positions within a scan, layers within a build are correlated) and silently inflates accuracy.

Cross-reference. ML-PC u09 (MAE), the EM foundation-model slide (EM foundation model), ML-PC u06 (transfer), ML-PC u11 (calibration). This is the recipe the §E exercise instantiates.
