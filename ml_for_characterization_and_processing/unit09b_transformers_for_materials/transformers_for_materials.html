01. Where we are

Recap of Unit 9 — and why we now need attention

Unit 9 (PCA / AE / MAE on spectra):

  • PCA — linear, closed-form, interpretable; a strong baseline on EELS / XRD / Raman.
  • Autoencoders — non-linear, but each layer sees only a small local neighborhood.
  • MAE — masked-autoencoder pretraining; already uses a transformer encoder, but we treated it as a black box.

What is still missing:

  • A principled way to mix information across distant positions of the input.
  • The Bragg disc on the left of a 4D-STEM pattern should be allowed to talk to the disc on the right in one layer, not after 20 convolutions.
  • This is exactly what attention gives us.

Note

Theory is in MFML W10 (transformer derivation, complexity, optimisation). Today: using transformers on materials data on a 1080Ti budget.

Materials hook: recap that PCA / AE / MAE were the spectroscopy workhorses of Unit 9; the only one of them that already had global context was the MAE — because it had a transformer inside. We are now opening that black box. 1080Ti budget: everything in this deck must fit on 11 GB VRAM at fp16 — that is the constraint we keep returning to. Theory pointer: MFML W10 has the full derivation (positional encodings, layer-norm placement, optimiser quirks); ML-PC W10b is about using the result on lab data.

02. Why attention for materials

Long-range correlations exceed practical CNN receptive fields

  • A diffraction pattern, a STEM image, an EELS line scan, an LPBF layer stack — all contain structure that spans the entire field of view.
  • The Bragg discs of a 4D-STEM pattern are geometrically constrained pairs: positions on opposite sides of the central beam carry coupled information (lattice vectors).
  • A CNN with 3×3 kernels needs ~\(L/2\) layers to connect two pixels \(L\) apart. On a 256×256 pattern that is 128 layers for global context — unrealistic at 1080Ti budgets.
  • A transformer mixes all positions in one layer, at the cost of \(\mathcal{O}(L^2)\) in sequence length.
  • For micrographs we trade pixels for patches (ViT) to make \(L\) manageable.

Warning

“Long-range” is not a buzzword on materials data. EELS edges span hundreds of channels; LPBF microstructure depends on the previous five layers. If your model cannot see that far in one layer, it cannot learn the physics.

Materials hook: I want students to feel why the 3×3 conv hammer hits a ceiling on diffraction and on layered builds. Walk through the Bragg-pair example explicitly — pick a 256×256 CBED pattern, point at two discs 200 px apart, count the conv layers needed. 1080Ti budget: even a 14×14-patch ViT on 256×256 has L=256, well inside what a 1080Ti runs with SDPA at batch 32. MFML W10 reference: receptive-field calculation is done formally there.

03. Refresher: scaled dot-product attention

The formula and the cost

\[ \mathrm{Attn}(Q, K, V) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V \]

  • \(Q, K, V \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d_k}\) — queries, keys, values; one row per token.
  • \(QK^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L}\) — pairwise similarity; this is the memory bottleneck.
  • \(\sqrt{d_k}\) scaling keeps softmax saturation in check at high \(d_k\).
  • Multi-head: run \(h\) attention blocks of dim \(d_k / h\) in parallel, concat; cheap diversity.

Complexity:

  • Compute: \(\mathcal{O}(L^2 \, d_k)\).
  • Memory (naive): \(\mathcal{O}(L^2)\) to materialise the attention matrix.
  • At \(L = 1024\), \(L^2 = 10^6\) entries per head per layer — fits, but tight on 11 GB.
  • This is why Flash Attention matters (slide 5): same math, \(\mathcal{O}(L)\) memory.

Note

Reference: (Vaswani et al. 2017). Derivation, gradient flow, why pre-norm beats post-norm — all in MFML W10.

Materials hook: re-emphasise that \(QK^\top\) is what couples every token to every other token — which is what we wanted for diffraction patterns. 1080Ti budget: a naive attention on L=4096 needs 16M entries times batch times heads — blows past 11 GB instantly. This number motivates slide 5. MFML W10: refer students there for the full per-head, per-layer FLOP and memory count and for the positional-encoding zoo.

04. Vision Transformer (ViT) in five lines

Patchify, embed, encode, classify

  1. Patchify the image into \(P\times P\) patches (e.g. 16×16). Each patch becomes one token. A 224×224 image gives \(L = 196\).
  2. Linear embed each flattened patch into a \(d\)-dimensional vector.
  3. Prepend a learnable [CLS] token; add learnable positional embeddings (one per slot, including CLS).
  4. Stack \(N\) transformer encoder blocks (LayerNorm → MHA → MLP → residual).
  5. The final state of [CLS] is the image representation; classify with a linear head (Dosovitskiy et al. 2021).

Diagram in words: image → grid of patches → flatten → linear projection → token sequence → [CLS] + pos. emb. → \(N \times\) encoder → take [CLS] → MLP head.

Warning

ViT was originally trained on JFT-300M. Pretraining matters: on small materials datasets, a ViT trained from scratch will overfit catastrophically — pretrain (MAE / DINO) or use a pretrained backbone. See anti-patterns slide.

Materials hook: the patch size is the lever. On a 256×256 4D-STEM CBED pattern, P=16 gives 256 tokens — comfortable on 1080Ti; P=8 gives 1024 tokens — tight; P=4 is naive-attention infeasible without SDPA / Flash. 1080Ti budget: a ViT-Tiny (d=192, 12 layers, 3 heads) at L=256 trains at batch 64 fp16 with SDPA — that is the workhorse setting I use. MFML W10: positional-embedding choices (learned vs sinusoidal vs 2-D RoPE) are covered there; for diffraction data 2-D positional info is non-trivial and matters.

05. Flash Attention

The kernel that makes long sequences tractable

  • Same mathematics as scaled dot-product attention; different IO pattern.
  • Naive attention writes the full \(L\times L\) matrix to HBM, then reads it back for softmax and the \(\cdot V\) multiply. Memory-bound.
  • Flash Attention tiles the computation, keeps the running softmax statistics in SRAM, and never materialises the full matrix in HBM (Dao et al. 2022).

Net effect:

  • \(\mathcal{O}(L)\) memory at fixed head size.
  • 2–10× wall-clock speedup at long \(L\).
  • Exact, not approximate — same gradients.

Practical PyTorch:

import torch.nn.functional as F

# auto-dispatches to the best available kernel
y = F.scaled_dot_product_attention(
        q, k, v,
        attn_mask=None,
        dropout_p=0.0,
        is_causal=False,
)

Note

On Pascal (1080Ti) SDPA dispatches to the memory-efficient kernel (not true Flash, but still \(\mathcal{O}(L)\) memory and a real speedup). On Ampere+ it dispatches to true Flash Attention. Either way: prefer SDPA to a hand-rolled softmax(QK^T/√d)@V.

Materials hook: this is the single piece of plumbing that unlocks training a 4D-STEM ViT on a 1080Ti at all. Without SDPA, batch 8 on L=1024 OOMs; with SDPA, batch 32 fits. 1080Ti budget: explicitly call out that we do not get true Flash Attention on Pascal — students on lab 1080Ti hardware still benefit from the memory-efficient kernel, just not the full 5× speedup the paper advertises (that is Ampere+). MFML W10 reference: complexity arguments are derived there; ML-PC W10b shows the wall-clock numbers.

06. Materials application 1 — ViT on 4D-STEM

Diffraction patches → token sequence → ViT encoder

  • 4D-STEM: at each scan position \((x, y)\) the detector records a 2-D diffraction pattern \(I(k_x, k_y)\). The dataset is a 4-D tensor.
  • Treat each diffraction pattern as a 2-D image; patchify; ViT-encode.
  • Output: per-scan-position embedding → downstream tasks (phase map, strain map, orientation map, defect detection).

Why ViT and not a 2-D CNN:

  • Bragg discs are geometrically paired across the central beam — a non-local relationship.
  • The relevant features (disc positions, Friedel pairs, kinematic vs dynamic intensities) live at all spatial scales.
  • A ViT mixes all patches in one layer; a CNN needs many layers to do the same.

Note

Practical recipe: ViT-Tiny (\(d=192\), 12 layers), patch 16, image 256×256, MAE-pretrained on unlabelled 4D-STEM stacks, then fine-tuned for the labelled task. On 1080Ti: batch 32 fp16 with SDPA.

Materials hook: this is the slide that pays off the whole unit. Tie back to Unit 9 — MAE pretraining on 4D-STEM is exactly what the autoencoder family was preparing for. Mention concrete tasks: orientation mapping (currently template matching), strain mapping (currently disc fitting), phase identification (currently library search). Each of these has been shown to benefit from ViT embeddings. 1080Ti budget: a single 4D-STEM scan is ~64×64 positions × 256×256 detector = 256M values — does not fit in VRAM as one batch; train on randomly-sampled scan-position minibatches. MFML W10 reference: the per-token compute count derived there explains the patch-size trade-off.

07. Materials application 2 — long-stack micrograph context

Cross-attention across LPBF layers

  • Laser powder-bed fusion (LPBF): part is built layer by layer; microstructure at layer \(\ell\) depends on the thermal history of layers \(\ell, \ell-1, \ldots, \ell-k\).
  • A per-layer CNN sees only the current layer — it cannot learn that an overhang two layers below changed the local cooling rate.
  • Stack the last \(k\) in-situ thermal/optical layer images as a sequence; cross-attention lets layer \(\ell\) attend to layers \(\ell - 1, \ldots, \ell - k\).

Architecture sketch:

  • Per-layer ViT encoder produces a token sequence \(T_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}\).
  • A cross-attention block over the layer axis: \(Q\) from \(T_\ell\), \(K, V\) from \(\{T_{\ell-1}, \ldots, T_{\ell-k}\}\).
  • Output predicts local microstructure / defect probability for layer \(\ell\).

Warning

Naive concatenation along the token axis blows \(L\) by \(k\times\) → \(L^2\) blows by \(k^2\). Use cross-attention with separate \(K, V\) (not self-attention on the concatenated stack) to keep cost linear in \(k\).

Materials hook: this is the killer application of attention for the processing side of the course. Thermal history is the hidden variable in LPBF; we have layer-resolved in-situ data; we just need a model with the right inductive bias. 1080Ti budget: a ViT-Tiny per layer at L=256 plus a cross-attention head over k=5 layers fits comfortably; k=20 starts to be tight. MFML W10 reference: cross-attention is the original encoder–decoder construct from the 2017 paper — point students at that derivation rather than re-deriving here.

08. Scaling alternatives (mention only)

Beyond dense attention

  • Mixture of Experts (MoE) — replace the MLP block with \(E\) parallel MLPs and a router that sends each token to the top-\(k\) experts. Sparse compute: \(E\)× parameters, only \(k\)× FLOPs. Useful when we want a large model on a small inference budget.
  • Mamba / structured state-space models (SSMs) — recurrent formulation with \(\mathcal{O}(L)\) compute and constant memory; competitive with transformers on long sequences (Gu and Dao 2023). Cross-reference the supplementary time-series deck (sequence models).
  • For our 1080Ti / lab-scale budget, neither is needed on the typical materials sequence lengths (\(L \lesssim 4096\) with SDPA).
  • They become relevant for whole-build LPBF sequences (\(L \sim 10^5\) layers × patches) or multi-spectrum joint models.

Note

Pick the right tool. A vanilla ViT + SDPA solves 90 % of materials tasks at our scale. MoE / Mamba are for the long-tail.

Materials hook: I deliberately keep this short. Students should know the names so they recognise them in papers; they should not leave thinking they need a 70B-parameter MoE to do strain mapping. Supplementary time-series deck: SSMs were introduced as a sequence-model alternative there; this slide is just a pointer.

09. Practical recipe

nn.MultiheadAttention vs F.scaled_dot_product_attention

Old-style (nn.MultiheadAttention):

import torch.nn as nn

mha = nn.MultiheadAttention(
        embed_dim=192,
        num_heads=3,
        batch_first=True,
)
y, attn_w = mha(q, k, v)
# pre-PT 2.0: materialises L×L
  • Returns attention weights for free.
  • Materialises the \(L\times L\) matrix on older PyTorch.
  • Use only when you actually need the weights (e.g. for an attention-rollout visualisation).

New-style (F.scaled_dot_product_attention):

import torch.nn.functional as F

y = F.scaled_dot_product_attention(
        q, k, v, is_causal=False,
)
# auto-dispatches to Flash / mem-eff / math
  • No attention weights returned (by design).
  • \(\mathcal{O}(L)\) memory; 2–10× faster on Ampere+.
  • Default choice for all new code.

Wall-clock comparison — ViT-Tiny forward + backward, batch 16, fp16, 1080Ti (indicative):

seq_len \(L\) naive MHA SDPA (mem-eff) speedup
64 1.0× 1.0× ~equal
256 1.0× 1.6× 1.6×
1024 OOM at batch 16 runs at batch 16 \(\infty\) in practice

Note

Numbers are 1080Ti / fp16 / ViT-Tiny; trends hold, absolute values depend on driver and PyTorch version. Re-measure on your hardware before quoting.

Materials hook: this is the slide students will copy-paste from. Stress: nn.MultiheadAttention is not deprecated, it is just the wrong default — use it only when you need the attention weights. 1080Ti budget: the table is the punchline — at L=1024 naive MHA OOMs at batch 16, SDPA runs comfortably. Re-measure: 1080Ti drivers and PyTorch versions matter; have students run the bench themselves on lab hardware. MFML W10 reference: complexity is derived there; this slide is the empirical confirmation.

10. Anti-patterns

What to not do

(a) Naive MHA on long sequences.

  • Materialising the \(L\times L\) matrix at \(L > 512\) on a 1080Ti will OOM at any non-trivial batch.
  • Fix: F.scaled_dot_product_attention everywhere. No reason not to in 2026.

(b) ViT on a small dataset, trained from scratch.

  • ViTs have weak inductive bias compared to CNNs. With ~\(10^3\) labelled micrographs and no pretraining, they overfit hard and a ResNet18 will beat them.
  • Fix: pretrain with MAE (Unit 9) on unlabelled data, then fine-tune. Or start from a publicly-pretrained backbone.

(c) Forgetting positional embeddings on materials data.

  • A diffraction pattern is not permutation-invariant: token at \((0, 0)\) is the central beam, token at \((7, 7)\) is a high-\(k\) Bragg disc. Their positions encode physics.
  • Fix: always add (learned or 2-D sinusoidal / RoPE) positional embeddings. Verify by ablating — accuracy should crash without them.

Warning

A ViT without positional embeddings is a deep set. On diffraction data, a deep set is wrong by construction.

Materials hook: each anti-pattern maps to a real lab mistake I have seen. (a) someone runs the textbook MHA on 2048-token EELS edges; OOM. (b) someone retrains a ViT-Base from scratch on 800 labelled SEM images; underperforms a ResNet18. (c) someone permutes tokens for data augmentation; deletes the physics. 1080Ti budget: (a) is partly a budget anti-pattern — at A100 scale you might get away with it, but the wasted memory still costs throughput. MFML W10 reference: deep-set vs positional-aware distinction is formalised there.

11. Exercise preview

Tiny ViT on 4D-STEM patches — bench it

Task. Build a ViT-Tiny that ingests 4D-STEM diffraction patches (\(128 \times 128\), patch 16) and predicts a per-scan-position label (e.g. crystalline / amorphous, or one of \(K\) orientations).

Baseline. A small ResNet (ResNet8 or ResNet18) on the same input and label.

Variants to compare:

  • ViT-Tiny with nn.MultiheadAttention.
  • ViT-Tiny with F.scaled_dot_product_attention.
  • ViT-Tiny without positional embeddings (sanity-check anti-pattern (c)).
  • ResNet baseline.

Report:

  • Validation accuracy.
  • Wall-clock per epoch on 1080Ti.
  • Peak VRAM (torch.cuda.max_memory_allocated).
  • One-paragraph discussion: when does ViT beat ResNet on your data?

If you do not have 4D-STEM data, the exercise notebook ships with a small synthetic CBED generator — enough to make the architectural comparison meaningful.

12. Recap & References

Recap — what to take away

  1. Attention exists to handle long-range correlations — and materials data (diffraction, EELS, LPBF layer stacks) is full of them.
  2. ViT is the default vision transformer: patchify, embed, [CLS], positional emb., \(N\) encoder blocks. Needs pretraining on small datasets.
  3. Flash Attention / SDPA is non-negotiable — same math, \(\mathcal{O}(L)\) memory, 2–10× speedup. Use F.scaled_dot_product_attention.
  4. Use the right tool: vanilla ViT covers most materials tasks at lab scale; MoE / Mamba are for the long tail. Theory in MFML W10; today was using it.
